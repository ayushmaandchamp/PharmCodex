Insulin · oral & injectable antidiabetics

✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Glucose-dependent vs glucose-independent is the safety divide

The drugs split into those that push insulin out regardless of glucose (sulfonylureas, meglitinides — hence hypoglycaemia) and those that only act when glucose is high or work by other mechanisms (incretins, SGLT2, metformin — hence little hypo risk). That single distinction predicts most of the safety profile.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a factory and its glucose "thermostat"

Picture insulin release as a heater controlled by a thermostat reading blood glucose. A sulfonylurea jams the heater ON regardless of the room temperature — great when it's cold (high glucose), dangerous when it's already warm (normal glucose) because it keeps blasting heat → hypoglycaemia. Incretin drugs (GLP-1, DPP-4) turn up the thermostat's sensitivity — the heater fires harder only when it's genuinely cold, so it can't overshoot. Metformin doesn't touch the heater at all — it tells the liver to stop pouring in cold air (gluconeogenesis). SGLT2 inhibitors open a drain in the floor — excess glucose just runs out in the urine, independent of insulin entirely.

⚠️ Warning — high-yield traps: Metformin → lactic acidosis risk; hold before iodinated contrast and in renal impairment. SGLT2 inhibitors → euglycaemic DKA, genital mycotic infections, volume depletion. Pioglitazone (TZD) → fluid retention worsening heart failure, fracture risk, bladder-cancer signal. Sulfonylureas → hypoglycaemia (esp. elderly, renal impairment) and weight gain. Only regular insulin is given IV (for DKA). GLP-1 agonists → nausea, contraindicated with personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma/MEN2.

Thyroid & Antithyroid Drugs

Levothyroxine · thionamides · radioiodine
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Two clinical states, two treatment logics: hypothyroidism → replace (levothyroxine, T₄), and hyperthyroidism → suppress (thionamides block synthesis, radioiodine ablates, β-blockers control symptoms).

Hypothyroidism — replacement

  • Levothyroxine (T₄): drug of choice — long half-life, steady, converted peripherally to active T₃. Titrate to TSH.
  • Take on an empty stomach; absorption reduced by calcium, iron, PPIs.

Hyperthyroidism — three strategies

β-blockersPropranololRapid symptom control (tremor, tachycardia); propranolol also ↓ T₄→T₃

Iodide (high dose)Lugol's, KITransiently ↓ release (Wolff-Chaikoff); pre-op / thyroid storm

Guiding primers

  • Why is levothyroxine (T₄) used for replacement rather than the active hormone T₃?
  • Why do you monitor TSH, not T₄, to titrate replacement (link to feedback loops, Module 46)?

✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Replace the deficient, throttle the excessive

Hypothyroidism is simply too little hormone — give back T₄ and let the body convert what it needs to T₃. Hyperthyroidism is too much — you can stop the gland making it (thionamides), destroy the gland (radioiodine), or just blunt the adrenergic symptoms while you wait (β-blockers).

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a furnace running the body's metabolism

Thyroid hormone is the thermostat setting for the body's whole furnace. Hypothyroidism is a furnace set too low — sluggish, cold, slow heart, weight gain — so you simply turn the dial back up (levothyroxine). Hyperthyroidism is the furnace roaring out of control — hot, racing heart, weight loss. You can cut the fuel supply (thionamides block hormone synthesis), rip out the furnace entirely (radioiodine ablation), or — while those slow methods take effect — put a blanket over the alarm bells (β-blockers calm the racing heart and tremor without touching the furnace itself).

⚠️ Warning: both thionamides can cause agranulocytosis (warn patients: sore throat + fever → stop and check FBC) and hepatotoxicity. In pregnancy: PTU in the first trimester (methimazole is teratogenic — aplasia cutis), then switch to methimazole. Over-replacement with levothyroxine → iatrogenic hyperthyroidism (AF, osteoporosis). High-dose iodide before radioiodine blocks its uptake — timing matters.

ACTH, Adrenal Steroids & the Adrenal Cortex

Glucocorticoids · mineralocorticoids
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
The adrenal cortex makes three steroid families (salt, sugar, sex); pharmacology centres on the glucocorticoids — superb anti-inflammatories whose every therapeutic action is shadowed by a predictable adverse effect, because they switch the body into a chronic "stress + high-cortisol" state.

Choosing a corticosteroid (potency grading)

DrugGluco potencyMineralo activityUse
Hydrocortisone1 (reference)YesAdrenal insufficiency, acute
Prednisolone~4SomeMost chronic anti-inflammatory use
Dexamethasone~25NegligibleCerebral oedema, potent suppression
Fludrocortisone—HighMineralocorticoid replacement (Addison's)

Glucocorticoid effects = their adverse effects (Cushingoid picture)

  • ↑ gluconeogenesis → hyperglycaemia/steroid diabetes
  • Protein catabolism → muscle wasting, thin skin, poor healing
  • Fat redistribution → moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity
  • Immunosuppression → infection risk; ↓ bone → osteoporosis
  • Mineralocorticoid → Na⁺/water retention, hypertension, hypokalaemia
  • HPA-axis suppression → adrenal atrophy (never stop abruptly)

A patient has been on oral prednisolone for three months and wants to stop because of weight gain. Blurt out why you must NEVER stop it abruptly — what has happened to their own adrenal glands — and what catastrophic event abrupt withdrawal could trigger.

Guiding primers

  • Why does giving exogenous steroid shrink the patient's own adrenal glands (link to feedback, Mod 46)?
  • Why does dexamethasone barely cause fluid retention while hydrocortisone does?
  • Why are nearly all the "side effects" of steroids just their normal physiological actions in excess?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

One hormone, the whole stress programme

Cortisol prepares the body for stress: raise blood glucose, free up amino acids and fat, and damp down inflammation. A therapeutic glucocorticoid exploits the anti-inflammatory part — but you can't take just that slice; the whole stress programme runs, which is why chronic use reproduces Cushing's syndrome. And because exogenous steroid suppresses ACTH, the adrenals stop working and atrophy.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — outsourcing a factory until it shuts down

Your adrenal glands are a factory that the pituitary keeps busy by sending daily orders (ACTH). When you give long-term steroid from outside, the warehouse is always full, so the pituitary stops sending orders — and a factory with no orders for months lays off its workers and rusts shut (adrenal atrophy). If you then suddenly cut off the outside supply, there's no cortisol from either source — an adrenal crisis. That's why you must taper: gradually reducing the outside supply lets the pituitary start sending orders again and the factory slowly restart.

⚠️ Warning: Never stop chronic glucocorticoids abruptly — taper to avoid adrenal crisis. Long-term effects: osteoporosis (give bone protection), steroid diabetes, infection (including reactivation of TB), cataracts/glaucoma, growth suppression in children, peptic ulcer risk (esp. with NSAIDs). During intercurrent illness/surgery, patients on long-term steroids need stress-dose cover. Mineralocorticoid effect varies by drug — dexamethasone has almost none.

📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Slow the demolition or hire the builders

Bone is constantly remodelled by osteoclasts (demolition) and osteoblasts (construction). In osteoporosis demolition outpaces construction. Antiresorptives (bisphosphonates, denosumab) slow the demolition crew; anabolics (teriparatide) hire more builders. The PTH paradox: continuous high PTH favours resorption, but intermittent pulses favour formation.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a building under constant renovation

Bone is a building with a demolition crew and a construction crew always at work. Osteoporosis is demolition winning. A bisphosphonate is like chaining the wrecking ball — it embeds in the bone and poisons osteoclasts that try to chew it, so demolition slows (and the effect lingers in the bone for years). Denosumab fires the demolition foreman (blocks RANKL) — but the moment you stop paying, the whole crew rushes back with a vengeance (rebound). Teriparatide is the clever twist: a quick daily visit from the site manager (a PTH pulse) motivates the builders, but if that manager never leaves (continuous PTH) they start directing demolition instead.

⚠️ Warning: oral bisphosphonates → oesophagitis (take upright, empty stomach, full glass of water, stay upright 30 min). Rare but classic: osteonecrosis of the jaw and atypical femoral fractures with prolonged use. Denosumab → rebound vertebral fractures on discontinuation — never stop without follow-on therapy. Correct vitamin D/calcium before starting antiresorptives (risk of hypocalcaemia). Teriparatide carries an osteosarcoma signal (limit duration).

Estrogens, Progestins & the Female Reproductive Tract

Contraception · HRT · SERMs
Tier 2 · Solid yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Estrogen and progestin given exogenously exploit negative feedback: they suppress the hypothalamic-pituitary axis, switching off the LH surge so ovulation never happens — the core of hormonal contraception. The same hormones, used tissue-selectively (SERMs) or blocked (aromatase inhibitors), treat breast cancer and osteoporosis.

Guiding primers

  • Why does adding exogenous estrogen/progestin stop the body's own ovulation cycle?
  • Why can one molecule (a SERM) be an estrogen blocker in one tissue and an activator in another?
  • Why does estrogen-containing contraception raise venous thromboembolism risk?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Feedback off, and the SERM "context-dependent key"

A steady supply of exogenous estrogen + progestin convinces the hypothalamus and pituitary that hormone levels are already adequate, so they stop sending FSH/LH — no follicle matures, no LH surge, no ovulation. SERMs are cleverer: the same molecule behaves as an agonist or antagonist depending on which co-regulators a tissue expresses.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a key that turns differently in different locks

A SERM is a master key cut to a strange shape. In the breast lock it jams the mechanism so the real estrogen key can't turn it (antagonist — useful in ER-positive breast cancer). But the bone lock and the endometrial lock are built slightly differently, and in those the same strange key actually turns the mechanism on (agonist). That's why tamoxifen protects bone (good) yet stimulates the endometrium (raising cancer risk) — one key, three locks, three outcomes. For contraception, the logic is simpler feedback: keep the warehouse stocked with hormone and head office stops ordering new production (no ovulation).

⚠️ Warning: estrogen-containing contraceptives/HRT → venous thromboembolism (avoid in smokers >35, prior VTE, migraine with aura). Tamoxifen → endometrial cancer + VTE risk. Aromatase inhibitors → osteoporosis (they remove protective estrogen). Mifepristone is a progesterone antagonist. Many interactions with enzyme inducers (rifampicin, certain anticonvulsants) reduce contraceptive efficacy.

✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

The master gland and its thermostat

The hypothalamus and pituitary sit atop each endocrine axis, and the target hormone feeds back to keep output stable. Pharmacology either supplies a missing hormone, mimics a releasing signal, or blocks an overactive one. Two quirks dominate exams: dopamine inhibits prolactin (so dopamine agonists treat prolactinomas, dopamine blockers raise prolactin), and GnRH effect depends on pattern — pulses stimulate, a continuous flood desensitises and shuts the gonads down.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a thermostat-controlled heating system

An endocrine axis is a house thermostat. The hypothalamus sets the target temperature, the pituitary is the relay that switches the boiler (target gland) on, and the room temperature (hormone level) feeds back to the thermostat to stop it overshooting — that's negative feedback. The GnRH quirk is like a doorbell: ringing it in normal rhythm (pulses) keeps summoning a response, but holding the button down continuously just makes everyone tune it out (desensitisation) — which is exactly how continuous GnRH analogues are used to switch off sex-hormone production in prostate cancer.

⚠️ Warning: GnRH analogues cause an initial testosterone/estrogen "flare" before suppression — cover with an anti-androgen in prostate cancer. Dopamine agonists (cabergoline) → nausea, postural hypotension, rare valvulopathy/impulse-control disorders. Desmopressin → hyponatraemia (water retention). This axis logic underlies steroid-taper (Module 50) and contraceptive feedback (Module 48).

Replace it, or cut it off at one of three points

Testosterone is converted by 5α-reductase to the more potent DHT in tissues like prostate and scalp. To lower androgen action you can stop central production (GnRH analogues), block the conversion to DHT (finasteride), or block the receptor itself (bicalutamide) — a layered system that mirrors how you'd dam a river at the source, midstream, or at the delta.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — shutting off water at three points

Androgen action is water reaching a garden through a pipe. To stop it you can close the main valve at the reservoir (GnRH analogues stop testicular production), narrow a booster pump midway (finasteride blocks conversion of testosterone to the more powerful DHT), or cap the sprinkler heads at the garden itself (receptor blockers like bicalutamide). For prostate cancer you often hit two points at once; for BPH/baldness, throttling the midway booster (finasteride) is enough because DHT is what overgrows the prostate and miniaturises hair follicles.

⚠️ Warning: finasteride/dutasteride are teratogenic — pregnant women must not handle crushed tablets (risk to male fetus); they also lower PSA (interpret prostate screening with care). GnRH analogues cause an initial testosterone flare — pair with an anti-androgen at the start in prostate cancer. Anabolic-steroid abuse → suppressed spermatogenesis, cardiovascular and hepatic harm. Testosterone is contraindicated in prostate cancer.

🃏 Clinical Vignette Flashcards

Tap a card to reveal the answer, mechanism, clinical pearl, and exam trap. Twenty high-yield scenarios across Endocrine Pharmacology.

01 A 58-year-old woman with T2DM and eGFR 28 mL/min is on metformin. She is admitted for elective contrast CT. What should be done, and why?
Hold metformin 48 h before and 48 h after IV contrast. Contrast can cause transient AKI; if metformin continues during AKI, lactic acid clearance falls → metformin-associated lactic acidosis (MALA). Also, metformin is contraindicated when eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m² and should be dose-reduced at eGFR 30–45. Mechanism of metformin: activates AMPK → inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis + enhances peripheral insulin sensitivity. No hypoglycaemia risk.
💎 Clinical Pearl: MALA is rare but carries ~50% mortality when it occurs. Classic triad: high anion-gap metabolic acidosis + elevated lactate + metformin use in the setting of CKD, contrast exposure, excessive alcohol, or sepsis.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Metformin does NOT cause hypoglycaemia as monotherapy — it only sensitises to insulin already present. This makes it the preferred first-line agent in T2DM and safe in combination with agents that do cause hypoglycaemia (sulfonylureas).
02 A T2DM patient on glipizide is found unconscious with blood glucose of 36 mg/dL. IV dextrose is given and she wakes, but hypoglycaemia recurs two hours later. Why does this happen, and how long must she be monitored?
Sulfonylureas (glipizide, gliclazide, glibenclamide) stimulate insulin secretion by blocking K⁺-ATP channels on β-cells → depolarisation → Ca²⁺ influx → insulin release, independent of glucose level. Their duration of action (6–24 h) means hypoglycaemia can be prolonged and recurrent even after initial dextrose correction. Observe for 24–48 h with continuous glucose infusion; hospital admission is often required.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Long-acting sulfonylureas (glibenclamide/glyburide) are the most dangerous in overdose and in elderly/CKD patients — prefer shorter-acting agents (gliclazide, glipizide) or avoid the class entirely in CKD (accumulation risk).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Octreotide (somatostatin analogue) is used for refractory sulfonylurea-induced hypoglycaemia — it suppresses insulin secretion directly, reducing the frequency of dextrose infusions needed. This is the specific antidote for SU overdose.
03 A pregnant woman at 8 weeks with Graves' hyperthyroidism is on methimazole. Her obstetrician insists on switching drugs. Why, and to what?
Methimazole is associated with embryopathy in the 1st trimester: aplasia cutis (scalp skin defects), choanal and oesophageal atresia — the 'methimazole embryopathy' syndrome. Switch to propylthiouracil (PTU) for the first trimester. After the 1st trimester, switch back to methimazole — PTU carries a risk of severe hepatotoxicity (including fulminant hepatic failure) that outweighs its embryopathy advantage beyond week 14.
💎 Clinical Pearl: PTU has an additional mechanism absent in methimazole: it inhibits peripheral 5'-deiodinase → blocks T4 → T3 conversion. This makes PTU the drug of choice in thyroid storm (faster reduction of circulating T3).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Both thionamides cross the placenta AND enter breast milk. PTU crosses less into breast milk → preferred during breastfeeding. Neither drug is 'safe' — the risk-benefit must be weighed. Target: maternal FT4 in the upper-normal range to avoid foetal hypothyroidism.
04 A patient with thyroid storm is admitted. List the pharmacological steps in the correct order, including why one drug must be given before another.
Correct sequence: (1) PTU first — blocks new hormone synthesis AND T4→T3 conversion; (2) Wait ≥1 hour, then Lugol's iodine / SSKI — blocks thyroid hormone release (Wolff–Chaikoff). Critical: iodine given before PTU provides substrate for a new surge of hormone synthesis ('escape'). (3) Propranolol — controls adrenergic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor) AND inhibits peripheral T4→T3 conversion; (4) Hydrocortisone — prevents concurrent adrenal crisis AND reduces peripheral T4→T3 conversion; (5) Supportive care (cooling, fluids).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Cholestyramine can be added to bind thyroid hormones in the gut (reduces enterohepatic recirculation of T4/T3), accelerating their clearance in severe thyroid storm.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Aspirin is contraindicated in thyroid storm — it displaces T4 from thyroid-binding globulin (TBG) → acutely raises free T4. Use paracetamol (acetaminophen) for fever instead.
05 A T2DM patient on semaglutide loses 12 kg over 6 months. He also reports nausea and early satiety. What are the mechanisms of weight loss and GI side effects?
GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) activate GLP-1 receptors in: (1) Pancreas → glucose-dependent insulin secretion + glucagon suppression; (2) Hypothalamus/brainstem → reduced appetite, increased satiety; (3) Stomach → slowed gastric emptying → earlier satiety + prolonged nutrient absorption. The same mechanism of gastric slowing causes nausea, vomiting, and early satiety — the most common side effects (start low, titrate slowly).
💎 Clinical Pearl: SUSTAIN-6 (semaglutide) and LEADER (liraglutide) trials demonstrated significant reductions in MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events) — cardiovascular benefit independent of glucose lowering. GLP-1 agonists are now first-line add-on in T2DM with established CVD.
⚠️ Exam Trap: GLP-1 agonists and DPP-4 inhibitors both enhance GLP-1 signalling — but DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin) are weight-neutral, while GLP-1 agonists promote weight loss. GLP-1 agonists are also associated with pancreatitis and theoretical thyroid C-cell tumour risk (contraindicated in MEN2/personal history of MTC).
06 A 45-year-old woman with T2DM is started on empagliflozin. She develops vulvovaginal itching and discharge. What is the mechanism, and what serious adverse effect must also be counselled?
Genital mycotic infections: SGLT2 inhibitors block glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule → glucosuria → glucose-rich environment in the genital area → Candida overgrowth. Common in women (vulvovaginitis) and uncircumcised men (balanitis). Treat with topical antifungals; reduce/stop if recurrent. Serious adverse effect: euglycaemic DKA — normal or near-normal blood glucose (150–200 mg/dL) but ketoacidosis — because SGLT2 inhibitors promote ketonaemia. Must hold drug during illness, fasting, or surgery.
💎 Clinical Pearl: EMPA-REG OUTCOME trial: empagliflozin reduced CV death by 38% and HF hospitalisation by 35% in T2DM with CVD. DAPA-HF and EMPEROR-Reduced showed benefit even in patients without diabetes. SGLT2 inhibitors are now standard of care in HFrEF regardless of diabetes status.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Fournier's gangrene (necrotising fasciitis of the perineum) is a rare but FDA black-box warning for SGLT2 inhibitors. Also: SGLT2 inhibitors cause a modest rise in LDL (~2–5%) and increase UTI risk slightly (though overall infection rate is low due to urinary glucose washout effect on uropathogens).
07 A T1DM patient in DKA has an initial K⁺ of 5.4 mEq/L. As insulin is infused, K⁺ drops to 3.0 mEq/L in 4 hours. Explain the physiology and the monitoring protocol.
Despite normal/elevated serum K⁺ at presentation, DKA causes total body K⁺ depletion (vomiting, osmotic diuresis, inability to retain K⁺ intracellularly without insulin). Acidosis shifts K⁺ extracellularly, masking the deficit. Insulin therapy: activates Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → K⁺ rapidly enters cells → serum K⁺ falls precipitously. Protocol: check K⁺ every 2 h; add KCl to IV fluids once K⁺ <5.0; hold insulin if K⁺ <3.5 until repleted (to prevent life-threatening arrhythmia).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Phosphate also drops with insulin therapy (enters cells with glucose). If phosphate <1.0 mg/dL or patient has haemolysis/respiratory failure, supplement with potassium phosphate (repletes both K⁺ and PO₄³⁻ simultaneously).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Never start insulin in DKA without first knowing the K⁺ level. If K⁺ <3.5, giving insulin can precipitate hypokalaemia → VF → death. This is the leading preventable cause of DKA mortality. Glucose-containing fluids (D5W) are NOT used in initial DKA management — use 0.9% NaCl for initial resuscitation.
08 A patient with Cushing's disease awaiting pituitary surgery is prescribed metyrapone. He develops hypertension and acne. Explain both the mechanism and the side effects.
Metyrapone inhibits 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1) — the final step in cortisol synthesis (11-deoxycortisol → cortisol). Cortisol falls, ACTH rises (pituitary no longer suppressed), and cortisol precursors accumulate. Side effects explained by precursor accumulation: 11-deoxycortisol has weak glucocorticoid activity; DHEA and androstenedione accumulate → androgenic effects (acne, hirsutism, oligomenorrhoea). Precursor mineralocorticoid activity → sodium retention → hypertension.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Metyrapone test: normally the hypothalamic-pituitary axis responds to falling cortisol by secreting more ACTH, raising 11-deoxycortisol. An absent response (low ACTH, low 11-deoxycortisol) implies secondary adrenal insufficiency. Used diagnostically to test the HPA axis integrity.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Ketoconazole (azole antifungal) also inhibits multiple adrenal steroidogenesis enzymes (CYP11A1, CYP17, CYP11B1) → used for Cushing's but limited by hepatotoxicity and extensive CYP3A4 inhibition. Osilodrostat (CYP11B1 inhibitor, newer, more selective) is now preferred over ketoconazole.
09 A patient with primary hypothyroidism is started on levothyroxine. She is also taking ferrous sulphate and calcium carbonate for anaemia and osteoporosis. How should the timing be managed?
Take levothyroxine on an empty stomach, 30–60 minutes before any food and separate from: calcium salts, iron supplements, antacids (Al³⁺/Mg²⁺), cholestyramine, sucralfate, PPIs, and soy products — all reduce levothyroxine absorption by chelation or pH alteration. Separate these by ≥4 hours. Coffee also reduces absorption.
💎 Clinical Pearl: TSH is the most sensitive monitoring parameter. Aim for TSH 0.5–2.5 mIU/L in most adults; 0.1–1.5 in pregnant women (thyroid hormone critical for foetal neurodevelopment). In subclinical hypothyroidism with TSH 4.5–10, treat if symptomatic, pregnant, or planning pregnancy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Starting levothyroxine in a patient on warfarin increases thyroid hormone-stimulated clearance of clotting factors → warfarin requirements INCREASE, INR FALLS. Conversely, treating new-onset hypothyroidism in a stable warfarin patient → monitor INR closely and expect to increase warfarin dose.
10 A woman with Addison's disease is admitted in shock with BP 70/40, hyponatraemia (Na⁺ 122), and hyperkalaemia (K⁺ 6.1). What is the immediate management?
Addisonian crisis: acute adrenal insufficiency → deficiency of both cortisol (glucocorticoid) and aldosterone (mineralocorticoid). Immediate treatment: (1) IV hydrocortisone 100 mg bolus, then 50–100 mg q6–8h — provides both glucocorticoid and (at high doses) mineralocorticoid activity; (2) IV 0.9% NaCl — volume resuscitation + sodium replacement; (3) IV dextrose if hypoglycaemic; (4) Treat hyperkalaemia if K⁺ >6.5 (cardiac risk). Fludrocortisone is not needed acutely (hydrocortisone at stress doses covers mineralocorticoid function).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Maintenance replacement: hydrocortisone 15–25 mg/day in divided doses (AM-weighted to mimic cortisol diurnal rhythm) + fludrocortisone 50–200 µg/day. Sick day rules: double/triple hydrocortisone dose during illness, injury, or surgery — failure to do so → crisis.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do NOT delay hydrocortisone while waiting for cortisol or ACTH results in suspected Addisonian crisis. If needed, give dexamethasone 4 mg IV (does not interfere with cortisol assay) → draw cortisol/ACTH → then switch to hydrocortisone. Time is critical.
11 A patient with prostate cancer is started on leuprolide (GnRH agonist) depot injection. His PSA and testosterone initially rise at week 2, worsening bone pain. Explain.
Testosterone flare: continuous GnRH agonist initially stimulates pituitary LH/FSH release ('flare' effect, days 1–14) before causing receptor downregulation → desensitisation → castrate testosterone levels (week 3–4 onwards). The initial testosterone surge can cause disease flare: worsening bone pain, urinary obstruction, spinal cord compression. Prevention: co-prescribe an anti-androgen (bicalutamide 50 mg/day) for the first 4 weeks to block AR during the testosterone surge.
💎 Clinical Pearl: GnRH antagonists (degarelix, relugolix) directly block the GnRH receptor → no initial LH/FSH surge → no testosterone flare → immediate castration levels within 3 days. Preferred over agonists when flare risk is high (impending cord compression, severe bone pain).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Continuous (non-pulsatile) GnRH agonist = suppression. Pulsatile GnRH (as in physiological secretion) = stimulation. This paradox is the basis of both the therapeutic flare and the ultimate hormonal suppression.
12 Octreotide is prescribed for carcinoid syndrome. Six months later, the patient develops right upper quadrant pain and ultrasound confirms gallstones. Explain the mechanism.
Octreotide (somatostatin analogue) inhibits cholecystokinin (CCK) release and gallbladder smooth muscle contraction → biliary stasis → supersaturation of bile → cholesterol or pigment gallstones in 20–50% of long-term users. Other actions of octreotide: inhibits GH/IGF-1 (acromegaly), serotonin/VIP/substance P (carcinoid/VIPoma), and splanchnic blood flow (variceal bleeding).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) can be given prophylactically during long-term octreotide use to reduce gallstone formation. Lanreotide is a longer-acting somatostatin analogue (monthly depot) with similar efficacy and gallstone risk.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Octreotide improves carcinoid symptoms (flushing, diarrhoea) but does NOT significantly shrink carcinoid tumours. For tumour control, add everolimus (mTOR inhibitor) or sunitinib, or use peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) with lutetium-177-DOTATATE.
13 A postmenopausal woman on anastrozole for breast cancer develops wrist and hip fractures after 3 years of therapy. What pharmacological mechanism explains the bone loss?
Anastrozole (aromatase inhibitor, AI) inhibits CYP19 → blocks peripheral conversion of androgens → oestrogens. In postmenopausal women, peripheral oestrogen production (adrenal androstenedione → oestrone in fat/muscle) is the primary oestrogen source. AI therapy → profound oestrogen deficiency → uncoupled bone remodelling (osteoclasts > osteoblasts) → accelerated bone loss → fractures. AIs cause more bone loss than tamoxifen (which has agonist activity at bone ER).
💎 Clinical Pearl: DXA scan before and every 2 years on AI therapy. Prescribe calcium 1200 mg + vitamin D 1000–2000 IU/day to all AI patients. Add bisphosphonate (zoledronic acid, alendronate) or denosumab if T-score ≤ -2.0 or multiple risk factors.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Tamoxifen (SERM) protects bone in postmenopausal women (bone ER agonist) but INCREASES endometrial cancer risk (uterine ER agonist). AIs have NO endometrial cancer risk (no ER stimulation) but worsen bone. Choose AI over tamoxifen in postmenopausal women unless fracture risk is very high.
14 A 70-year-old man is started on teriparatide for severe osteoporosis. His doctor says it can only be given for 2 years. Why is there a time limit?
Teriparatide (recombinant PTH 1–34) is an anabolic bone agent — it stimulates osteoblast proliferation and activity. Animal studies showed dose-dependent osteosarcoma in rats with prolonged high-dose exposure. Although not proven in humans, the FDA imposed a 2-year cumulative lifetime limit as a precautionary measure. After stopping teriparatide, bone density rapidly decreases unless followed immediately by an antiresorptive agent (bisphosphonate or denosumab).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Abaloparatide (PTHrP 1–34 analogue) has the same anabolic mechanism and 2-year limit, with possibly less hypercalcaemia than teriparatide. Romosozumab (anti-sclerostin antibody) — dual anabolic + antiresorptive, 12-month course, but has a cardiovascular safety signal (not in patients with recent MI/stroke).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Teriparatide is contraindicated in: Paget's disease of bone, prior bone radiation therapy, hypercalcaemia, and paediatric patients (open epiphyses — risk of bone tumours). Do NOT combine with bisphosphonates during teriparatide therapy (bisphosphonates blunt the anabolic response).
15 A patient with type 1 DM uses insulin glargine at night and insulin aspart before meals. She is prescribed propranolol for her new diagnosis of essential tremor. What risk does the beta-blocker introduce?
Propranolol (non-selective β-blocker) masks the adrenergic symptoms of hypoglycaemia: tachycardia, palpitations, tremor — the early warning signs. The patient may not recognise she is hypoglycaemic until she is confused or loses consciousness. Additionally, β-blockade impairs glycogenolysis (hepatic glucose release) → prolongs hypoglycaemic episodes. Diaphoresis (sweating) is a parasympathetic-mediated sign — NOT masked by beta-blockers — but this alone is insufficient warning.
💎 Clinical Pearl: If a beta-blocker is needed in a diabetic patient on insulin, prefer a cardioselective beta-blocker (bisoprolol, metoprolol) — β1 selectivity is somewhat less likely to mask glucagon-mediated recovery, but still masks adrenergic warning symptoms. Counsel all insulin patients on this risk.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Beta-blockers do NOT cause hypoglycaemia — they only mask and prolong it. The hypoglycaemia is caused by too much insulin. Diaphoresis is preserved as a warning sign (mediated by sympathetic cholinergic fibres to sweat glands, not β-adrenoceptors).
16 Radioactive iodine (¹³¹I) is given for Graves' disease. The patient calls 3 days later with sore throat and neck pain. Eight months later she is hypothyroid. Are either of these expected?
Day 3 symptoms: Radiation thyroiditis — ¹³¹I damages thyroid tissue → acute inflammatory response → sore throat, neck tenderness, transient worsening of hyperthyroidism (hormone release from damaged follicles). Usually self-limiting; treat with NSAIDs or prednisolone if severe. Month 8: Permanent hypothyroidism is the intended outcome of ¹³¹I therapy — occurs in ~80% within 10 years. Replace with levothyroxine lifelong.
💎 Clinical Pearl: ¹³¹I is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (crosses placenta → foetal thyroid ablation) and breastfeeding. It is also avoided in active, moderate-to-severe Graves' ophthalmopathy (TED) — can worsen eye disease; give oral glucocorticoids if ¹³¹I must be used.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Pre-treat with methimazole to render the patient euthyroid before ¹³¹I (especially elderly and cardiac patients) — prevents post-ablation thyroid storm from radiation-induced hormone release. Stop methimazole 3–5 days before ¹³¹I (it reduces iodine uptake).
17 A patient with PCOS presents with irregular cycles and infertility. She declines OCP. What pharmacological options address both the metabolic and reproductive aspects?
Metformin: reduces insulin resistance → lowers androgens (reduces ovarian androgen overproduction driven by hyperinsulinaemia) → may restore ovulatory cycles; first-line for metabolic management of PCOS. Clomiphene (clomifene): SERM — blocks oestrogen receptors in hypothalamus → perceived oestrogen deficiency → increased GnRH → LH/FSH surge → ovulation induction; first-line for ovulation induction. Letrozole (aromatase inhibitor): now preferred over clomiphene for ovulation induction in PCOS (higher live birth rates per PPCOS-II trial).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Spironolactone (anti-androgen via aldosterone receptor block + CYP17 inhibition) is added for hirsutism and acne in PCOS. Must use contraception with spironolactone in women of childbearing age — it feminises a male foetus.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do NOT use clomiphene beyond 6 cycles (increased risk of ovarian cancer with prolonged use). Monitor for ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS) during induction cycles — abdominal bloating, ascites, haemoconcentration.
18 Desmopressin (DDAVP) is prescribed for central diabetes insipidus. A patient with nephrogenic DI is mistakenly given the same drug. Why will it fail?
Central DI: ADH deficiency (hypothalamic/pituitary damage). Desmopressin = synthetic ADH V2 receptor agonist → activates renal collecting duct V2 receptors → aquaporin-2 insertion → water reabsorption. Works because the kidney's receptor and response machinery is intact. Nephrogenic DI: the kidney cannot respond to ADH (V2 receptor or aquaporin mutations, lithium toxicity, hypercalcaemia, CKD) → desmopressin is ineffective. Treat nephrogenic DI with: thiazide diuretics (paradoxically reduce free water excretion via volume depletion), amiloride (if lithium-induced), low-sodium diet.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Desmopressin also stimulates von Willebrand factor (vWF) release from endothelium (V2 effect) and factor VIII release → used for mild haemophilia A, type 1 von Willebrand disease, and platelet disorders.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Desmopressin can cause severe hyponatraemia (dilutional) if patients drink excessive water while on the drug — common in children given desmopressin for nocturnal enuresis. Monitor Na⁺; restrict fluid intake after dosing.
19 A 28-year-old woman is prescribed isotretinoin for severe cystic acne. What monitoring programme is mandated, and what is the teratogenic mechanism?
Isotretinoin (13-cis-retinoic acid) is a vitamin A (retinol) derivative — activates retinoic acid receptors (RAR) → regulates gene expression critical for cell differentiation and apoptosis in sebaceous glands. Profound teratogenicity: retinoic acid signalling is essential for normal embryonic development; excess → craniofacial defects, CNS malformations, cardiac defects, thymic aplasia. iPLEDGE programme (USA): mandatory monthly pregnancy testing, two forms of contraception, prescriber/pharmacy registration, monthly dispensing limits.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Side effects (all reversible except potential teratogenicity): mucocutaneous dryness (cheilitis, epistaxis, xerosis), elevated triglycerides, transient LFT rise, joint/muscle pain, and potential psychiatric effects (depression — monitor, though causality debated).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Vitamin A supplementation at doses >10,000 IU/day in pregnancy also causes identical birth defects — the same RAR-mediated mechanism. This is why pregnant women are advised to avoid high-dose vitamin A supplements and liver (very high in retinol).
20 Bisphosphonates are prescribed for osteoporosis. After 7 years, a patient develops a mid-shaft femur fracture from a minimal fall. X-ray shows a transverse fracture with cortical thickening. What is the diagnosis, and why does it occur?
Atypical femoral fracture (AFF) — a rare but recognised complication of long-term bisphosphonate therapy (>5 years). Bisphosphonates inhibit osteoclast farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase (mevalonate pathway) → impaired osteoclast activity → suppressed bone turnover. Prolonged suppression prevents normal micro-crack repair (normally osteoclasts resorb damaged bone) → fatigue fractures accumulate → spontaneous transverse fracture at mid-shaft (not osteoporotic compression fracture). Risk increases with >5 years of use.
💎 Clinical Pearl: AFF is often bilateral (30–40%) and preceded by prodromal thigh or groin pain ('stress reaction' visible on MRI before fracture). A 'drug holiday' after 5 years in lower-risk patients reduces AFF risk while preserving fracture protection (bisphosphonate is stored in bone mineral and continues to be released slowly).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Also monitor for osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ): especially with IV bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid) used for malignancy. Risk factors: dental extraction, poor oral hygiene, corticosteroid use, chemotherapy. ONJ presents as exposed non-healing bone in the jaw. Dental clearance before starting IV bisphosphonates.

A GH-receptor antagonist.

Inhibits prolactin release.

Dopamine agonists (cabergoline, bromocriptine).
9Why do dopamine-blocking antipsychotics cause galactorrhoea?
Loss of dopamine inhibition → rising prolactin.
10Effect of pulsatile GnRH?
Stimulates gonadotropin release.
11Effect of continuous GnRH (analogue)?
Desensitises receptors → suppresses the gonadal axis.
12Give a GnRH analogue.
Leuprolide (goserelin).

Prostate cancer, endometriosis, central precocious puberty.

A transient rise in sex hormones before suppression.

Co-administer an anti-androgen initially.

Negative feedback suppresses hypothalamic/pituitary output.

7AndrogensModule 49 · 22 Q
1Drug for male hypogonadism?
Testosterone (gel, patch, IM).
2Why is oral testosterone problematic?
Extensive hepatic first-pass metabolism.
3Enzyme converting testosterone to DHT?
5α-reductase.
4Mechanism of finasteride?
5α-reductase inhibitor → ↓ DHT.
5Two uses of finasteride?
Benign prostatic hyperplasia and male-pattern baldness.
6Why does one drug treat both prostate and scalp?
Both are DHT-driven; reducing DHT helps both.
7Difference between finasteride and dutasteride?
Dutasteride inhibits both 5α-reductase isoenzymes (more complete).
8Mechanism of flutamide/bicalutamide?
Androgen-receptor antagonists.
9Main use of androgen-receptor antagonists?
Prostate cancer.
10Three levels at which androgen action can be blocked?
Central synthesis (GnRH analogue), DHT conversion (finasteride), receptor (bicalutamide).
11Mechanism of abiraterone?
Inhibits androgen synthesis (CYP17) — used in prostate cancer.
12Why combine a GnRH analogue with an anti-androgen initially?
To cover the testosterone flare from the GnRH analogue.
13Key teratogenic warning for finasteride?
Pregnant women must not handle crushed/broken tablets (risk to male fetus).
14Effect of finasteride on PSA?
Lowers it — interpret prostate cancer screening accordingly.
15Why is testosterone contraindicated in prostate cancer?
It can stimulate androgen-dependent tumour growth.
16Consequences of anabolic-steroid abuse?
Suppressed spermatogenesis, cardiovascular and hepatic toxicity, mood effects.
17How does exogenous androgen suppress sperm production?
Negative feedback ↓ LH/FSH → ↓ testicular function.
18Which hormone does 5α-reductase produce in the prostate?
Dihydrotestosterone (DHT).
19GnRH analogue example used in prostate cancer?
Leuprolide (goserelin).
20Why is DHT more relevant than testosterone in BPH?
DHT is the more potent androgen acting on prostate growth.
21Is the GnRH suppression principle the same in men and women?
Yes — continuous GnRH suppresses the gonadal axis in both sexes.
22Ketoconazole's relevance to androgens?
At high dose it inhibits steroid (androgen) synthesis enzymes.
