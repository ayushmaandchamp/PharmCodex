Pharmacokinetics — what the body does to the drug

Absorption · Distribution · Metabolism · Elimination
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
All of pharmacokinetics reduces to four parameters — bioavailability (F), volume of distribution (Vd), clearance (CL) and half-life (t½) — and the handful of equations that link a dose to a plasma concentration over time.

The high-yield elements

  • Drug transport: passive diffusion favours small, lipophilic, non-ionised molecules; carrier-mediated transport (SLC uptake, ABC efflux) is saturable and selective.
  • pH partitioning / ion trapping: a drug is trapped where it is more ionised — acids accumulate in basic compartments, bases in acidic ones.
  • The four parameters: F (fraction reaching circulation), Vd (apparent volume), CL (volume cleared/time), t½ (derived from the other two).
  • Order of kinetics: first-order (constant fraction/time, most drugs) vs zero-order (constant amount/time — ethanol, phenytoin, high-dose aspirin).
  • Steady state ≈ 4–5 half-lives; loading dose fills Vd, maintenance dose matches CL.

pH partitioning & ion trapping

Weak acids and bases exist as a lipid-soluble non-ionised form (crosses membranes) and a poorly diffusible ionised form. The ratio is set by the Henderson–Hasselbalch relationship, and a drug accumulates wherever it becomes more ionised.

EQ 2·1 log([protonated]/[unprotonated]) = pKa − pH

The parameters interlock

Vd governs the loading dose (the peak you reach), CL governs the maintenance dose (the steady state you hold), and their ratio sets the half-life. t½ is therefore a consequence, not a cause.

EQ 2·3 Css = (F × Dose) / (CL × τ)  |  EQ 2·15 t½ = 0.693 · Vd/CL

Order of kinetics

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a sink with a drain

Picture the body as a sink being filled by a tap and emptied by a drain. The tap rate is your dose × bioavailability. The size of the sink is the volume of distribution — a big sink (lipophilic drug spread into tissues) means the same dose barely raises the water level. The drain width is clearance. Steady state is when the water level holds constant because inflow equals outflow — and it always takes about four "drain-times" (half-lives) to get there, no matter how hard you open the tap.

First-order kinetics is a normal drain: the fuller the sink, the faster it empties (constant fraction). Zero-order is a drain clogged to a fixed trickle — it removes the same amount per minute regardless of how full the sink is, so a little extra inflow overflows the basin fast. That's phenytoin and ethanol.

⚠️ Warning — the zero-order trap: ethanol, phenytoin and high-dose aspirin (also theophylline) saturate their elimination, so small dose increases produce disproportionate, non-linear rises in plasma level → toxicity. For high-extraction drugs (propranolol, lidocaine, morphine) clearance is flow-limited with large first-pass; for low-extraction drugs it's capacity-limited and vulnerable to enzyme induction/inhibition.

Potency vs efficacy — don't confuse them

Potency is about dose (where the curve sits left–right); efficacy is about maximal effect (how high the ceiling). A more potent drug is not better — efficacy and therapeutic index matter more. Furosemide is less potent but more efficacious than thiazides.

GPCR signalling cascade

💡 The Feynman Analogy — keys, locks & volume knobs

A receptor is a lock and a drug is a key. Affinity is how well the key fits; efficacy is how far it can turn the lock once in. A full agonist turns it all the way; a partial agonist only half-turns it no matter how hard you push (and if it's hogging the keyhole, it blocks the full agonist — acting as a brake). A competitive antagonist is a blank key in the slot you can pop out by jamming in more real keys; an irreversible one is superglued in. Potency is just how many keys you needed to bring.

Downstream, the GPCR is a volume knob: Gs turns the cAMP volume up, Gi turns it down, Gq switches to a different channel (calcium). Phosphodiesterase is the hand slowly turning cAMP back down — block it (caffeine, sildenafil) and the signal stays loud.

⚠️ Warning — narrow-TI drugs: warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, theophylline, aminoglycosides sit close to their toxic dose — small changes risk toxicity, so therapeutic drug monitoring is mandatory. Also remember receptor regulation: chronic agonist → down-regulation (tolerance); chronic antagonist → up-regulation (rebound on withdrawal).

Drug Metabolism — biotransformation

Phase 1 · Phase 2 · the cytochrome P450 system
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
One theme runs the whole chapter — Phase 1 functionalises, Phase 2 conjugates — and the CYP450 enzymes sit at the centre of nearly every clinically important drug interaction.

The high-yield elements

  • Phase 1 (oxidation/reduction/hydrolysis) adds or exposes a functional group; usually inactivates, but can activate prodrugs or create toxic metabolites. Enzymes: CYP450, FMO, epoxide hydrolase.
  • Phase 2 (conjugation — glucuronidation, sulfation, acetylation, etc.) adds a polar group → almost always inactivates and aids excretion. Can occur without Phase 1.
  • CYP3A4 alone metabolises >50% of clinical drugs; the big players are CYP2C, 2D, 3A subfamilies.
  • Induction (slow, ↓drug level) vs inhibition (fast, ↑drug level) — effects invert for prodrugs.

Induction vs inhibition — the interaction engine

Induction means the cell builds more enzyme (takes days–weeks): metabolism speeds up, drug levels fall, efficacy is lost — but a prodrug is activated more. Inhibition blocks the enzyme directly (hours): drug levels rise toward toxicity — but a prodrug is under-activated.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — the liver's recycling plant

The liver is a recycling plant that shreds drugs into disposable pieces. Phase 1 is the machine that cuts a handle onto the molecule; Phase 2 bolts on a bag so it can be thrown out in urine. An inducer is like hiring more shredder staff over a few weeks — everything gets processed faster, so drug levels drop. An inhibitor is jamming the shredder today — drugs pile up and overflow into toxicity.

The twist: a prodrug arrives needing the shredder to switch it on (codeine → morphine). So more shredding means more effect, and a jammed shredder means no effect — the exact opposite of a normal drug. Same machine, opposite consequence.

⚠️ Warning — mnemonics & two killers: Inducers "CRAP GPS" (Carbamazepine, Rifampin, Alcohol-chronic, Phenytoin, Griseofulvin/Phenobarbital, Smoking, St John's wort); inhibitors "SICKFACES.COM" (valproate, Isoniazid, Cimetidine, Ketoconazole, Fluconazole, Alcohol-acute, Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin, Sulfonamides, Ciprofloxacin, Omeprazole, Metronidazole). Two classics: rifampin → oral contraceptive failure; chronic alcohol (CYP2E1) + paracetamol → hepatotoxic NAPQI.

Principles of Clinical Toxicology

Dose-response · poisoning management · antidotes
Tier 1 · High yield (clinical)
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
"The dose makes the poison" — toxicology applies PK/PD to the overdosed patient: stabilise, decontaminate, give a specific antidote where one exists, and enhance elimination.

The high-yield elements

  • Dose-response: LD₅₀ = dose lethal to 50%; curve slope matters as much as position. Non-monotonic curves: U-shaped (hormesis), inverted-U, hockey-stick (saturable removal).
  • Management ladder: stabilise (ABCs) → decontaminate (charcoal) → antidote → enhance elimination → support.
  • Activated charcoal binds most toxins but not metals, alcohols, hydrocarbons, caustics, lithium, iron.
  • Acetaminophen = the saturated-pathway paradigm → CYP2E1 makes NAPQI, glutathione depletes, liver necroses.

Acetaminophen — the saturation paradigm

At therapeutic doses, paracetamol is conjugated (glucuronide/sulfate) and only a little NAPQI forms, mopped up by glutathione. In overdose those pathways saturate → CYP2E1 makes excess NAPQI → glutathione runs out → centrilobular hepatic necrosis. N-acetylcysteine refills glutathione.

Enhanced elimination

  • Urinary alkalinisation (NaHCO₃) — weak acids: salicylate, phenobarbital.
  • Haemodialysis favours drugs with low Vd, low protein binding, low MW, water-soluble: lithium, salicylate, methanol, ethylene glycol, theophylline, valproate, metformin, barbiturates.
💡 The Feynman Analogy — a factory's overflow drain

The liver detoxifies paracetamol through two wide main drains (glucuronidation, sulfation) and one small hazardous-waste pipe (CYP2E1 → NAPQI), which is normally neutralised by a tank of cleaner (glutathione). In overdose the two main drains clog and back up, forcing the flood through the hazardous pipe — and the cleaner tank empties. Now raw toxin (NAPQI) corrodes the building (hepatocytes). N-acetylcysteine is a tanker refilling the cleaner before the corrosion is fatal.

⚠️ Warning — the dialysis trap: large-Vd or highly protein-bound drugs (digoxin, TCAs, benzodiazepines) are NOT effectively dialysed — most of the drug sits in tissue, not plasma. And note the toxicology link to earlier chapters: many antidotes are chemical antagonists (chelators, protamine) or physiological antagonists from the pharmacodynamics chapter.

Membrane Transporters & Drug Response

SLC uptake · ABC efflux
Tier 2 · Specialty layer
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Two superfamilies decide where drugs go and how interactions arise — SLC carriers pull drugs in, ABC pumps push drugs out — at the gut, liver, kidney and blood-brain barrier.
  • SLC (solute carriers): uptake / secondary active transport — OATP (statin uptake), OCT (metformin), OAT (anion secretion), PEPT.
  • ABC (ATP-binding cassette): efflux — P-glycoprotein (ABCB1/MDR1), MRP, BCRP.
  • P-gp limits gut absorption, guards the BBB, and confers tumour multidrug resistance.

Guiding primers

  • Why does an OATP1B1 variant increase the risk of statin myopathy?
  • Why does probenecid prolong penicillin's action (which transporter does it block)?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Mechanism

SLC carriers move drugs into cells down gradients (or coupled to Na⁺); ABC pumps burn ATP to throw drugs out. P-glycoprotein (ABCB1/MDR1) is the one to know — it exports digoxin and many chemo agents.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — bouncers and turnstiles

Picture each cell as a nightclub. SLC transporters are turnstiles letting specific guests in (metformin via OCT, statins via OATP). ABC pumps like P-gp are bouncers throwing drugs back out the door — at the gut they bounce drugs before they're absorbed, at the brain they keep drugs out entirely, and tumour cells hire extra bouncers to eject chemotherapy (multidrug resistance). Block the bouncer (verapamil) and the drug (digoxin) piles up inside.

⚠️ Warning: Transporter interactions are a hidden cause of toxicity that don't show up as CYP interactions — e.g., a P-gp inhibitor raising digoxin, or an OATP inhibitor raising statin levels. Always consider transport, not just metabolism.

Mechanism

A polymorphism changes how much functional enzyme you make. A poor metaboliser of CYP2D6 can't convert codeine to morphine → no analgesia; an ultrarapid one makes too much → toxicity. For a drug that the enzyme inactivates, the consequences flip.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — different-sized engines

Think of each person's metaboliser status as the size of their engine for a given drug. For codeine (a prodrug that must be "revved up" into morphine), a huge engine (ultrarapid) over-revs into toxicity, while a stalled engine (poor metaboliser) never gets going — no pain relief. For a normal drug the engine burns it off, so the stalled engine lets it accumulate to toxic levels instead. Same engine sizes, opposite outcomes depending on whether the drug needs revving up or burning off.

⚠️ Warning: HLA-B*57:01 → abacavir and HLA-B*15:02 → carbamazepine (at-risk ancestry) are guideline-mandated pre-prescription genetic screens — skipping them risks fatal hypersensitivity / Stevens-Johnson syndrome.

Mechanism of the pipeline

Discovery starts by identifying and validating a target, screening libraries for a lead, optimising it (medicinal chemistry), then preclinical animal testing. An IND opens human trials: Phase I (safety), II (efficacy + dose), III (confirmation). An NDA wins approval; Phase IV watches the population.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — auditioning for a role

Drug development is a brutal audition with rounds of cuts. Thousands of molecules try out; medicinal chemistry is the rehearsal that polishes a few. Preclinical is the screen test on animals. Phase I asks "will it harm a healthy person?", Phase II "can it actually act?", Phase III "is it better than what we already cast?" — and only then does it get the part (NDA). Phase IV is the reviews after opening night, when rare flaws finally show in a huge audience.

⚠️ Warning: attrition is enormous and the timeline is ~10–15 years — most candidates fail. And because trials are short and modest in size, rare or delayed harms are precisely what Phase IV pharmacovigilance (Ch 8) exists to catch.

The GI Microbiome & Drug Response

The "forgotten organ" of metabolism
Tier 3 · Framework
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Gut bacteria run their own metabolism — chiefly reductions, hydrolyses and deconjugations — that can activate, inactivate, or recycle a drug behind the host's back.
  • β-glucuronidase deconjugates glucuronides in the gut → regenerates active drug → enterohepatic recirculation (prolongs effect; basis of irinotecan gut toxicity).
  • Eggerthella lenta inactivates digoxin — antibiotics that suppress it raise digoxin levels.
  • Sulfasalazine is a prodrug cleaved by gut bacteria into 5-ASA (active) + sulfapyridine.
  • Antibiotics disrupt the microbiome and can alter co-administered drug metabolism.

Guiding primers

  • How does bacterial β-glucuronidase prolong a drug's effect (what does it undo)?
  • Why is sulfasalazine useless without gut bacteria?
  • Why can a course of antibiotics change the effectiveness of an unrelated oral drug?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the Feynman notes
📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Mechanism

The liver tags drugs with glucuronide "bags" for disposal in bile. Gut bacteria carrying β-glucuronidase rip those bags off, freeing active drug to be reabsorbed — enterohepatic recycling. Other bacteria inactivate drugs (digoxin) or activate prodrugs (sulfasalazine).

💡 The Feynman Analogy — recycling bandits in the gut

The liver throws used drugs into the bile "rubbish chute" wrapped in disposal bags. But the gut is full of recycling bandits who tear the bags open and toss the drug back into circulation (β-glucuronidase → enterohepatic recycling), making the dose last longer than expected. Some bandits instead destroy useful drugs (digoxin), and some assemble a prodrug into its active form (sulfasalazine). Kill the bandits with antibiotics and all these hidden effects shift.

⚠️ Warning: microbiome effects are an under-recognised, invisible source of variable drug response and interactions — easy to miss because they don't appear in standard CYP interaction tables.

Mechanism & systems

Type A reactions are augmented pharmacology — predictable, dose-related, common (more warfarin → more bleeding). Type B are bizarre, idiosyncratic, dose-independent and rarer (penicillin anaphylaxis). After approval, spontaneous reporting systems collect cases that aggregate into signals, which can lead to black-box warnings or withdrawal.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a car recall after sale

Clinical trials are like test-driving a new car for a few thousand miles with a few hundred drivers. That catches obvious, predictable problems (Type A — "press the brake too hard and it lurches"). But a defect that strikes one driver in fifty thousand (Type B — idiosyncratic) only shows once millions are on the road. Pharmacovigilance is the recall hotline: scattered reports accumulate into a signal, triggering a warning label or, if serious enough, a full recall (withdrawal).

⚠️ Warning: a spontaneous-report "signal" is a hypothesis, not proof of causation — it must be weighed against background rates. But persistent serious signals (rofecoxib, cerivastatin) rightly end in withdrawal. This chapter is the real-world payoff of the trial pipeline in Ch 1.

01 A drug has a volume of distribution of 500 L in a 70 kg patient. What does this tell you about its distribution, and how does it affect overdose management?
A Vd of 500 L far exceeds total body water (~42 L), meaning the drug is extensively sequestered in tissues (lipophilic or tissue-bound). High Vd → low plasma concentration per dose, long half-life, and activated charcoal / haemodialysis are ineffective for overdose (most drug is in tissues, not blood). Examples: amiodarone (~60 L/kg), chlorpromazine (~20 L/kg).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Loading dose = Vd × target plasma concentration ÷ bioavailability. Critical when rapid therapeutic levels are needed (digoxin, amiodarone, phenytoin).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Warfarin has LOW Vd (~0.15 L/kg) because it is 99% albumin-bound — it stays in plasma. Yet dialysis is still ineffective because the protein-bound fraction is not filterable.
02 Ethanol is eliminated at ~10 mL/hour regardless of blood level. What kinetic model describes this, and which other clinically important drugs share it?
Zero-order (saturation) kinetics — alcohol dehydrogenase is saturated at typical drinking levels, so elimination rate is constant (independent of concentration). Blood alcohol falls linearly, not exponentially. Other zero-order drugs at clinical/toxic concentrations: phenytoin (Michaelis–Menten / mixed-order — switches to zero-order at therapeutic levels), high-dose salicylates.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Phenytoin follows first-order kinetics at low doses but switches to zero-order at therapeutic/supratherapeutic levels. This means small dose increases cause disproportionately large rises in plasma levels — the key reason phenytoin toxicity is common.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Zero-order drugs have NO constant half-life. Elimination time increases with dose. This makes toxic dose estimation unreliable from standard half-life calculations.
03 A drug has 5% oral bioavailability. The IV dose is 10 mg. What oral dose achieves equivalent systemic exposure, and what factors reduce bioavailability?
Oral dose = IV dose ÷ F = 10 mg ÷ 0.05 = 200 mg. Bioavailability is reduced by: poor GI absorption, first-pass hepatic metabolism, intestinal CYP3A4 metabolism, P-glycoprotein efflux (returns drug to gut lumen), formulation factors, and gut motility.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Nitroglycerin (F ~1%) and lidocaine (F ~35%) are almost entirely destroyed on first pass — must be given sublingually/transdermally/IV. Morphine F ~30% — oral doses must be 3× the IV dose for equivalent effect.
⚠️ Exam Trap: IV bioavailability is always 100% by definition. When converting oral → IV opioids, always use an equianalgesic chart — do not simply halve the dose.
04 A patient on warfarin is started on rifampin for TB. Her INR drops from 2.8 to 1.2 over two weeks despite taking the same warfarin dose. Explain.
Rifampin is a potent inducer of CYP2C9, CYP3A4, and P-glycoprotein. Warfarin (especially the active S-enantiomer) is a CYP2C9 substrate. Enzyme induction → accelerated warfarin metabolism → lower plasma levels → subtherapeutic anticoagulation. Induction develops over 1–2 weeks and reverses 1–2 weeks after stopping rifampin (both windows require close INR monitoring).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Major CYP inducers (RIPS): Rifampin, Isoniazid (mild), Phenytoin/Phenobarbital, St. John's Wort, carbamazepine. All also reduce OCP efficacy — counsel on contraception.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Clarithromycin is a CYP3A4 inhibitor, not an inducer. Erythromycin is also an inhibitor. Azithromycin does NOT significantly inhibit CYP — preferred when drug interactions are a concern.
05 Grapefruit juice is taken with simvastatin. The patient develops severe myalgia and dark urine. Which statins are safest, and what is the mechanism?
Grapefruit contains furanocoumarins that irreversibly inhibit intestinal CYP3A4 → increased first-pass bioavailability of CYP3A4-metabolised statins (lovastatin, simvastatin, atorvastatin) → elevated plasma levels → myopathy / rhabdomyolysis. One glass can inhibit CYP3A4 for 24–72 hours.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Safest statins with CYP3A4 inhibitors or grapefruit: rosuvastatin (primarily renal excretion, not CYP3A4) and pravastatin (not a CYP substrate). Fluvastatin is CYP2C9.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Grapefruit affects intestinal CYP3A4 primarily, not hepatic — so IV drugs given the same day are not affected. The interaction persists even if grapefruit was consumed hours before the drug.
06 A competitive antagonist is added to an agonist preparation — the dose–response curve shifts right but Emax is unchanged. An irreversible antagonist is then used. What happens to Emax?
Competitive antagonist: shifts EC₅₀ right (requires more agonist for the same effect) but Emax is preserved because, at high enough agonist concentrations, all binding sites can be occupied. Irreversible (non-competitive, covalent) antagonist: permanently reduces the number of available receptors → Emax falls even at maximum agonist concentration — cannot be overcome by increasing agonist dose.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Aspirin is the classic irreversible inhibitor — covalently acetylates COX-1 serine. Platelet COX-1 is permanently blocked for the platelet's lifespan (~10 days). Ibuprofen is competitive/reversible — antiplatelet effect lasts only while drug is present.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Non-competitive allosteric antagonists also reduce Emax WITHOUT shifting EC₅₀ — distinguish from irreversible antagonists by reversibility. Some allosteric antagonists are fully reversible.
07 A patient on warfarin is newly prescribed a full-dose NSAID. The INR rises from 2.5 to 4.9 within a week. Identify both the pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic mechanisms.
Pharmacokinetic: NSAIDs displace warfarin from albumin binding sites → transient rise in free warfarin → increased anticoagulant effect. Some NSAIDs (phenylbutazone) also inhibit CYP2C9 → reduced warfarin metabolism → sustained elevation. Pharmacodynamic: NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 in platelets → antiplatelet effect, synergising with anticoagulation; also cause gastric mucosal injury → bleeding risk multiplied.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Protein displacement is usually transient — free drug redistributes and elimination re-equilibrates. The more dangerous, sustained interaction is CYP2C9 inhibition (metronidazole, fluconazole, amiodarone are stronger inhibitors than most NSAIDs).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Even topical diclofenac has measurable systemic absorption sufficient to interact with warfarin. Celecoxib (COX-2 selective) is safer for GI bleeding risk but still inhibits CYP2C9 — warfarin interaction persists.
08 A patient on codeine for post-operative pain develops respiratory depression and pinpoint pupils despite a 'standard' dose. Genetic testing shows she is an ultrarapid CYP2D6 metaboliser. Explain.
Codeine is a prodrug; CYP2D6 converts codeine → morphine (the active opioid). Ultrarapid metabolisers (gene duplication, common in North African/Ethiopian populations: ~10–29%) convert codeine to morphine very rapidly → dangerously elevated morphine levels → respiratory depression, even fatal. The FDA has black-box warnings and restricts codeine use in children and breast-feeding mothers for this reason.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Tramadol is also a CYP2D6 prodrug (→ O-desmethyltramadol). Same ultrarapid metaboliser danger applies. Poor metabolisers (~7–10% Caucasians) derive no benefit from codeine — they cannot produce morphine.
⚠️ Exam Trap: CYP2D6 inhibitors (fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion) convert extensive metabolisers into phenotypic poor metabolisers — codeine is ineffective in these patients and increases codeine toxicity from unconverted parent drug.
09 A child develops haemolytic anaemia with Heinz bodies after starting dapsone for dermatitis herpetiformis. What enzyme deficiency explains this?
G6PD deficiency (X-linked). G6PD is essential for the hexose monophosphate shunt → NADPH production → reduced glutathione → protection against oxidative stress in RBCs. Oxidant drugs (dapsone, primaquine, nitrofurantoin, rasburicase) generate reactive oxygen species → Hb oxidation → Heinz body formation (denatured Hb) → haemolysis. G6PD testing is mandatory before prescribing oxidant drugs.
💎 Clinical Pearl: G6PD activity should be tested during a quiescent (non-haemolytic) period — during active haemolysis, older deficient RBCs are destroyed first; remaining young RBCs have more G6PD, giving a falsely normal result.
⚠️ Exam Trap: G6PD deficiency protects against Plasmodium falciparum malaria (infected RBCs lyse more easily, limiting parasite spread) — this explains its high prevalence in malaria-endemic regions. The same feature that confers malaria resistance causes drug-induced haemolysis.
10 A patient on phenelzine (MAOI) for depression eats aged blue cheese at dinner and develops sudden severe headache, BP 220/130, and diaphoresis. What is happening and how is it treated?
Hypertensive crisis from tyramine ('cheese effect'). MAO-A normally deaminates dietary tyramine in the gut wall and liver before it reaches systemic circulation. MAO inhibition → tyramine absorbed → displaces noradrenaline from sympathetic terminals → massive catecholamine surge → hypertensive crisis. Risk of intracerebral haemorrhage. Treatment: IV phentolamine (alpha-blocker) or labetalol; avoid beta-blockers alone (unopposed alpha → worsens hypertension).
💎 Clinical Pearl: High-tyramine foods: aged cheeses, cured/smoked meats, fermented foods (soy sauce, Marmite), broad beans, Chianti wine. Patients on MAOIs require strict dietary restriction throughout therapy and for 2 weeks after stopping.
⚠️ Exam Trap: MAOI + serotonergic drugs (SSRIs, SNRIs, tramadol, pethidine/meperidine) → serotonin syndrome (NOT hypertensive crisis). Wash-out: 14 days after stopping MAOI before starting serotonergic drug; 5 weeks after stopping fluoxetine (long t½) before starting MAOI.
11 Phenytoin is started for epilepsy. A serum level of 16 µg/mL is reported in a patient with albumin of 2.0 g/dL (normal 4.0). Is dose adjustment needed?
Yes. Phenytoin is 90% albumin-bound; total drug level measures bound + free, but only free drug is active. In hypoalbuminaemia, a normal 'total' level actually represents a higher free (active) fraction. Use the Sheiner–Tozer correction: Corrected level = measured level ÷ [(0.2 × albumin) + 0.1]. Here: 16 ÷ [(0.2 × 2.0) + 0.1] = 16 ÷ 0.5 = 32 µg/mL → already toxic range. Reduce dose; ideally measure free phenytoin directly.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Phenytoin toxicity is dose-related: nystagmus (20–30 µg/mL) → ataxia (30–40 µg/mL) → mental status changes (>40 µg/mL) → paradoxical seizures. Saturable (Michaelis–Menten) kinetics mean small dose increases can cause large level spikes.
⚠️ Exam Trap: IV phenytoin must be infused ≤50 mg/min with ECG monitoring — the propylene glycol vehicle causes cardiac conduction block and hypotension. Fosphenytoin (water-soluble prodrug) can be infused at 150 mg PE/min and given IM.
12 A drug has a therapeutic index of 2. Compare this to penicillin and explain why monitoring is required.
Therapeutic index (TI) = TD₅₀ ÷ ED₅₀. TI of 2 means the toxic dose is only twice the effective dose — a very narrow safety margin. Classic narrow TI drugs requiring therapeutic drug monitoring: lithium, digoxin, warfarin, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, theophylline, phenytoin, cyclosporine. Penicillins have an extremely wide TI (essentially no upper toxicity limit from direct drug effects at achievable doses) — monitoring is unnecessary.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Narrow TI drugs are sensitive to: renal/hepatic impairment (reduced clearance), age extremes, drug interactions, protein binding changes, and pharmacogenomic variation. Always check TI before prescribing a new interacting drug.
⚠️ Exam Trap: TI is a population-derived ratio, not a guarantee for an individual patient. A patient with CKD, elderly age, or TPMT deficiency may reach toxic levels well within the 'therapeutic' population range.
13 Isoniazid is given to two patients with latent TB: a slow NAT2 acetylator and a fast NAT2 acetylator. How does acetylator status affect each patient's risk profile?
Slow acetylators (common in Caucasians/Egyptians): higher isoniazid plasma levels → more effective bactericidal activity, but higher risk of peripheral neuropathy (pyridoxine-responsive) and drug–drug interactions (inhibits CYP2C9/CYP2E1 more). Fast acetylators (common in East Asians): lower isoniazid levels → potential treatment failure; acetylhydrazine metabolite accumulates → higher hepatotoxicity risk. Pyridoxine (B6) 25–50 mg/day is co-prescribed for all patients to prevent neuropathy.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Hydralazine (antihypertensive) and procainamide (antiarrhythmic) are also NAT2 substrates. Slow acetylators on hydralazine or procainamide are at higher risk for drug-induced lupus (DIL) — check ANA and anti-histone antibodies.
⚠️ Exam Trap: 'Fast' acetylators of isoniazid have more hepatotoxicity, not less — the acetylhydrazine intermediate is the hepatotoxin. Slow acetylators have more neuropathy. This counter-intuitive relationship is a classic exam question.
14 A patient is at steady state on a drug taken every 8 hours. The dose is doubled. When does the new steady state occur?
Steady state is reached after ~5 × t½, regardless of dose. Doubling the dose doubles the steady-state plasma concentration but does NOT change when steady state is reached — only the half-life determines that. If t½ = 8 h, new steady state occurs ~40 h after the dose change whether the dose is 1× or 100×.
💎 Clinical Pearl: A loading dose shortens the time to therapeutic concentrations without waiting 5 × t½. Practical example: digoxin (t½ 36–48 h) — without a loading dose, therapeutic levels take 7–10 days to develop.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Changing the dosing interval (not dose) changes the peak–trough fluctuation. Same total daily dose given more frequently → smoother, less fluctuating plasma levels → same average steady-state concentration.
15 Phase I vs Phase II metabolism: which phase more commonly produces reactive toxic intermediates, and give a clinical example.
Phase I (CYP450-mediated oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis) introduces/exposes polar functional groups — often generating reactive electrophilic intermediates. Example: paracetamol (acetaminophen) → CYP2E1/CYP3A4 → NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine) → normally conjugated with glutathione (Phase II). In overdose, glutathione depletes → NAPQI accumulates → covalent binding to hepatocytes → necrosis. Phase II (glucuronidation, sulphation, acetylation, glutathione conjugation) produces water-soluble, pharmacologically inactive metabolites — generally harmless.
💎 Clinical Pearl: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) replenishes glutathione stores in paracetamol overdose. Works best if given within 8–10 hours. Give empirically if >150 µg/mL at 4 h on the Rumack–Matthew nomogram.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Lorazepam and oxazepam bypass Phase I — they only require Phase II glucuronidation. They are the 'safe' benzodiazepines in severe liver disease (no Phase I enzymes needed). Remember 'LOT' — Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam.
16 Enterohepatic recirculation prolongs the effect of some drugs. Explain the mechanism and give two clinical implications.
Parent drug or its glucuronide conjugate is excreted into bile → reaches the intestine → gut bacteria express β-glucuronidase → deconjugate the drug → free drug reabsorbed → recirculates through the liver → prolonged half-life. Clinical implications: (1) Drug prolongation: morphine-6-glucuronide, oestrogens, some NSAIDs, colchicine have prolonged action via EHC; (2) Drug interaction: cholestyramine sequesters drugs in bile, interrupting EHC and reducing their efficacy/level (relevant for levothyroxine, digoxin, warfarin if taken simultaneously).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Cholestyramine is used therapeutically to interrupt EHC of leflunomide (active metabolite) during 'washout' in patients who need rapid drug elimination (e.g., before pregnancy or hepatotoxicity).
⚠️ Exam Trap: The claimed 'antibiotic disrupts gut flora → reduces EHC of OCP oestrogens → contraceptive failure' has very weak evidence. The FDA does not require OCP warnings with most antibiotics (exception: rifampin — but this works via CYP induction, not EHC).
17 A receptor is exposed to a high agonist concentration for 48 hours. Describe the molecular sequence of desensitisation and downregulation.
Acute (minutes): GRK (G-protein-coupled receptor kinase) phosphorylates the agonist-occupied receptor → β-arrestin recruitment → uncoupling from G-protein → desensitisation (reduced signalling despite continued agonist). Hours: β-arrestin mediates receptor internalisation (endocytosis) → sequestration in endosomes. Days: Internalised receptors directed to lysosomes for degradation → receptor downregulation (reduced total receptor number). Net result: tachyphylaxis/tolerance — diminished response to same drug concentration.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Upregulation occurs with prolonged antagonist exposure. Example: chronic beta-blocker use upregulates β-adrenoceptors → abrupt withdrawal → supersensitivity to catecholamines → rebound tachycardia, hypertension, angina. Always taper beta-blockers; never abruptly stop in cardiac patients.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Nitrate tolerance (ISDN/nitroglycerin patches) involves receptor-level and downstream desensitisation of guanylate cyclase. Requires a 'nitrate-free interval' (8–12 h/day) to restore sensitivity.
18 A 70-year-old patient with HF and CKD (eGFR 18) needs gentamicin for Gram-negative bacteraemia. How should dosing be adjusted?
Two strategies for renal impairment: (1) Extend the dosing interval (preferred): maintain same dose, lengthen interval proportional to CrCl reduction → maintains high peak (efficacy — aminoglycosides are concentration-dependent killers) while reducing trough accumulation (toxicity is trough-driven). (2) Reduce dose per interval (less preferred — low peaks may compromise efficacy). Monitor trough <1 µg/mL (traditional dosing) or use Hartford nomogram for extended-interval dosing.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Nephrotoxicity is usually reversible (proximal tubule regenerates). Ototoxicity (cochleotoxicity and vestibulotoxicity) is often irreversible — damaged inner ear hair cells cannot regenerate. Never resume an aminoglycoside that caused confirmed ototoxicity.
⚠️ Exam Trap: TDM for aminoglycosides: peak (30 min after infusion end) measures efficacy; trough (just before next dose) measures toxicity risk. Extended-interval dosing uses a single 6–14 h post-dose level plotted on a nomogram — do NOT use traditional peak/trough targets.
19 Two drugs, each 90% protein-bound, are given together. Drug A is displaced from albumin by Drug B. Describe the immediate effect and the long-term equilibrium.
Immediately: Free (active) Drug A rises → increased pharmacological effect and potential toxicity, increased Vd. Long-term: The additional free drug is available for hepatic metabolism and renal filtration → clearance increases → free drug concentration gradually returns toward baseline (new equilibrium). For most drugs, the net clinical effect is modest, but for narrow TI drugs, the initial spike is clinically dangerous (e.g., phenytoin displaced by valproate → transient phenytoin toxicity).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Valproate both displaces phenytoin from albumin (transient free drug ↑) AND inhibits CYP2C9 (persistent total drug ↑). The combination creates a complex, biphasic interaction that can be severely misleading if total phenytoin level is used alone.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Protein displacement is clinically significant only when: (1) the displaced drug has a narrow TI, (2) >90% protein-bound, and (3) clearance cannot immediately compensate (e.g., saturated hepatic metabolism). Most displacement interactions are transient and of limited clinical significance.
20 A patient is on a prodrug that requires hepatic CYP2C19 for activation. She is also prescribed omeprazole. What problem arises, and name the drug.
Clopidogrel (antiplatelet prodrug for ACS/stent). CYP2C19 converts clopidogrel → active thiol metabolite that irreversibly blocks P2Y12 receptors on platelets. Omeprazole (and esomeprazole) are CYP2C19 inhibitors → reduced clopidogrel activation → up to 45% reduction in antiplatelet effect → increased risk of stent thrombosis and MACE. Use pantoprazole instead (weakest CYP2C19 inhibition among PPIs).
💎 Clinical Pearl: CYP2C19 poor metabolisers (10–15% Caucasians, 15–25% Asians) have inherently reduced clopidogrel activation — consider genotyping or switch to ticagrelor/prasugrel (direct-acting P2Y12 inhibitors, no CYP activation needed).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Ticagrelor and prasugrel do NOT require metabolic activation — they are direct-acting P2Y12 inhibitors. They are NOT affected by CYP2C19 status or PPIs. Prasugrel is contraindicated in prior TIA/stroke and age >75 (excess bleeding risk).

The non-ionised (uncharged, lipid-soluble) form.

log([protonated]/[unprotonated]) = pKₐ − pH.

On the more basic (alkaline) side — it becomes more ionised and is trapped there.

Aspirin is a weak acid; alkaline urine ionises (traps) it in the tubule, preventing reabsorption and enhancing excretion.

Passage of solutes through gaps between cells; large in most capillaries but tight at the BBB.

The fraction of an administered dose that reaches the systemic circulation in unchanged form.

1 (100%) by definition.

P-glycoprotein, by exporting drug back into the gut lumen.

Adipose tissue (fat); also plasma proteins and bone.

Free (unbound) drug.

Approximately 4.5 to 8.0.
2Pharmacokinetics II — Parameters, clearance & dosingCh 2 · 20 Q
1Name the four key pharmacokinetic parameters.
Bioavailability (F), volume of distribution (Vd), clearance (CL), half-life (t½).
2Define volume of distribution.
The apparent volume relating the amount of drug in the body to its plasma concentration: Vd = amount/Cp.
3Why is Vd called "apparent"?
It is a mathematical proportionality constant, not a real anatomical volume; lipophilic, tissue-bound drugs can have Vd far exceeding total body water.
4Define clearance.
The volume of plasma completely cleared of drug per unit time; the body's efficiency at eliminating drug.
5Write systemic clearance as a sum.
CL = CL(renal) + CL(hepatic) + CL(other).
6Give the equation linking half-life to Vd and CL.
t½ ≈ 0.693 × Vd/CL.
7Why is half-life called a "derived" parameter?
Because it depends on the two independent variables CL and Vd; it changes when either changes.
8If clearance falls (e.g., renal disease) but Vd is constant, what happens to t½?
t½ increases (lengthens).
9Define first-order kinetics.
A constant fraction of drug is eliminated per unit time; rate is proportional to concentration; t½ is constant.
10Define zero-order kinetics.
A constant amount of drug is eliminated per unit time (saturable); rate is independent of concentration.
11Name three drugs showing zero-order kinetics.
Ethanol, phenytoin, high-dose aspirin (also theophylline at high dose).
12Roughly how fast is ethanol metabolised?
About 10 g per hour (a fixed amount — zero-order).
13After how many half-lives is steady state essentially reached?
~4–5 half-lives (≈94% after 4 t½).
14Does the dose affect the time to reach steady state?
No — time to steady state depends only on half-life, not on dose.
15Write the steady-state concentration equation for oral dosing.
Css = (F × Dose)/(CL × τ), where τ is the dosing interval.
16How is a maintenance dosing rate calculated?
Dosing rate = CL × target Css (÷ F for oral).
17How is a loading dose calculated, and why give one?
LD = Vd × target Cp / F; given to reach therapeutic levels rapidly when the half-life is long (e.g., digoxin).
18For a high-extraction-ratio drug, hepatic clearance approaches what?
Hepatic blood flow — clearance is flow-limited.
19Which type of drug (high vs low extraction) is more sensitive to enzyme induction/inhibition?
Low-extraction (capacity-limited) drugs.
20What is AUC and what does it relate to?
Area under the concentration–time curve; for F=1, CL = Dose/AUC, so it reflects total drug exposure.
3Pharmacodynamics I — Receptors & vocabularyCh 3 · 20 Q
1Name the four main receptor superfamilies.
GPCRs, ligand-gated ion channels, enzyme-linked (catalytic, e.g., RTK) receptors, and nuclear (intracellular) receptors.
2Which receptor type acts fastest, and on what time scale?
Ligand-gated ion channels — milliseconds.
3Which receptor type acts slowest and why?
Nuclear receptors — hours to days, because they alter gene transcription.
4Define affinity.
The tightness with which a drug binds its receptor; quantified by Kd (lower Kd = higher affinity).
5Define potency.
The amount of drug needed to produce a given effect; measured by EC₅₀/ED₅₀ (horizontal position of the curve).
6Define efficacy.
The maximal effect a drug can produce (Emax) — the ceiling, reflecting intrinsic activity.
7Which matters more clinically, potency or efficacy?
Efficacy (and therapeutic index); potency mainly determines the dose.
8What is a full agonist?
A ligand that produces the maximal response a receptor population can give (full intrinsic activity).
9What is a partial agonist?
A ligand that activates the receptor but cannot produce the maximal response even at full occupancy (submaximal Emax).
10What can a partial agonist do in the presence of a full agonist?
Act as a functional antagonist — competing for receptor and lowering the overall response.
11What is an inverse agonist?
A ligand that reduces constitutive (basal) receptor activity below the no-drug baseline.
12How does an inverse agonist behave when there is no basal activity?
It appears to act as a neutral antagonist.
13In the two-state model, full vs partial agonists differ in what?
Their efficacy — i.e., how far they shift the receptor equilibrium toward the active (Ra) state.
14Effect of a competitive (reversible) antagonist on the agonist curve?
Parallel rightward shift; Emax unchanged (surmountable by more agonist).
15Effect of a non-competitive/irreversible antagonist?
Reduced Emax; not surmountable.
16Define a physiological antagonist with an example.
A drug producing an opposite effect via a different receptor — e.g., adrenaline vs histamine in anaphylaxis.
17Define a chemical antagonist with an example.
A drug that inactivates another by binding it directly — e.g., protamine + heparin, or chelators.
18What are spare receptors?
Receptors in excess of those needed for maximal response — maximal effect occurs before 100% occupancy (EC₅₀ < Kd).
19What is an allosteric modulator?
A ligand binding a site distinct from the orthosteric (agonist) site to enhance (PAM) or reduce (NAM) receptor function — e.g., benzodiazepines on GABAA.
20What does "orthosteric" mean?
Binding at the same site as the endogenous agonist.
4Pharmacodynamics II — Dose-response, TI & signallingCh 3 · 20 Q
1Difference between graded and quantal dose-response curves?
Graded = continuous magnitude of effect in one system; quantal = all-or-none response across a population (fraction responding).
2What does ED₅₀ mean on a quantal curve?
The dose producing the specified effect in 50% of the population.
3What does LD₅₀ mean?
The dose lethal to 50% of the (experimental) population.
4Write the therapeutic index.
TI = LD₅₀ / ED₅₀.
5Why can two drugs with identical LD₅₀ differ in safety?
Curve slope differs — a steeper curve gives less separation between effective and toxic doses.
6What is the certain safety factor and why is it stricter?
LD₁/ED₉₉; it accounts for the extremes of the curves rather than the midpoints.
7Name four narrow-therapeutic-index drugs.
Warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin (also theophylline, aminoglycosides).
8What is the therapeutic window?
The concentration range between minimum effective and minimum toxic concentrations.
9Effector and second messenger for Gs?
Activates adenylyl cyclase → raises cAMP → activates PKA.
10Effector for Gi?
Inhibits adenylyl cyclase (lowers cAMP); also opens K⁺ and inhibits Ca²⁺ channels.
11Effector and messengers for Gq?
Activates phospholipase C-β → IP₃ (releases Ca²⁺) + DAG (activates PKC).
12Which G-protein do β-adrenergic receptors couple to?
Gs.
13Which G-proteins for M₁, M₂, M₃ muscarinic receptors?
M₁ and M₃ → Gq; M₂ → Gi.
14Which G-protein for α₁ and α₂ adrenergic receptors?
α₁ → Gq; α₂ → Gi.
15Who discovered cAMP as a second messenger?
Earl Sutherland (Nobel Prize, 1972).
16What degrades cAMP, and name a drug class that inhibits it?
Phosphodiesterases (PDEs); inhibitors include caffeine, sildenafil, milrinone, theophylline.
17Define tachyphylaxis.
Rapid loss of response on repeated/continuous dosing (acute desensitisation).
18Molecular basis of GPCR desensitisation?
GRK phosphorylates the agonist-occupied receptor → β-arrestin binds → uncouples it from the G-protein and promotes internalisation.
19Why does chronic β-blockade cause rebound on abrupt withdrawal?
Receptor up-regulation/supersensitivity → exaggerated response (rebound tachycardia/hypertension) when the drug is stopped.
20What does down-regulation of receptors underlie clinically?
Tolerance to chronic agonist exposure (reduced receptor number).
5Drug Metabolism I — Phase 1/2 & CYPCh 5 · 20 Q
1What is the overall purpose of drug metabolism?
To convert lipophilic drugs into more water-soluble, excretable metabolites.
2What do Phase 1 reactions do?
Introduce or expose a functional group (–OH, –NH₂, –SH) via oxidation, reduction or hydrolysis (functionalisation).
3What do Phase 2 reactions do?
Conjugate the drug (or its Phase 1 product) with an endogenous molecule, greatly increasing water solubility.
4Name the major Phase 2 conjugation reactions.
Glucuronidation, sulfation, acetylation, methylation, glutathione conjugation, glycine conjugation.
5Which is the most common Phase 2 reaction and its enzyme?
Glucuronidation, by UDP-glucuronosyltransferases (UGTs).
6Must Phase 1 always precede Phase 2?
No — some drugs are conjugated directly (e.g., directly glucuronidated).
7Where in the cell are CYPs and UGTs located?
In the endoplasmic reticulum (microsomal fraction).
8Decode the name CYP3A4.
Cytochrome P450, family 3, subfamily A, gene 4.
9Which CYP subfamilies are most important in drug metabolism?
CYP2C, CYP2D, and CYP3A.
10What fraction of clinical drugs does CYP3A4 metabolise?
Over 50%.
11Which CYP is most abundant in the liver?
CYP3A4.
12Which CYP metabolises codeine to morphine?
CYP2D6.
13Which CYP activates clopidogrel?
CYP2C19.
14Which CYP metabolises S-warfarin?
CYP2C9.
15Which CYP is induced by cigarette smoking?
CYP1A2 (affecting theophylline, caffeine, clozapine).
16Which CYP generates the toxic paracetamol metabolite, and what is it?
CYP2E1 generates NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine).
17List the commonly polymorphic CYPs.
CYP2A6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6.
18What is a prodrug?
An inactive (or less active) compound that is metabolised into its active form.
19Give three prodrug examples and their active forms.
Codeine→morphine, clopidogrel→active thiol metabolite, enalapril→enalaprilat (also levodopa, valacyclovir).
20What enzyme class besides CYP carries out Phase 1 oxidations?
Flavin-containing monooxygenases (FMOs) and epoxide hydrolases; esterases for hydrolysis.
6Drug Metabolism II — Induction, inhibition & interactionsCh 5 · 20 Q
1What is enzyme induction?
Increased synthesis of a metabolising enzyme, raising metabolic capacity over days to weeks.
2What is enzyme inhibition, and how fast does it act?
Blockade of enzyme activity (often competitive at the active site); near-immediate (hours).
3Effect of induction on a normal (active) drug's level and effect?
Lower drug level → loss of efficacy.
4Effect of inhibition on a normal drug's level?
Higher drug level → risk of toxicity.
5How do induction/inhibition effects invert for a prodrug?
Induction increases active metabolite (more effect/toxicity); inhibition decreases activation (reduced effect).
6List five classic CYP inducers.
Rifampin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, St John's wort (also chronic alcohol, smoking, griseofulvin).
7List five classic CYP inhibitors.
Ketoconazole, erythromycin/clarithromycin, cimetidine, ritonavir, grapefruit juice (also valproate, isoniazid, fluconazole).
8Which component of grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4?
Furanocoumarins (and flavonoids like naringin) — chiefly intestinal CYP3A4.
9Why is rifampin + oral contraceptive a classic interaction?
Rifampin induces CYP3A4 → faster OCP metabolism → contraceptive failure.
10How does chronic alcohol increase paracetamol hepatotoxicity?
It induces CYP2E1 → more NAPQI formed → greater glutathione depletion.
11What does St John's wort induce, and a consequence?
CYP3A4 (and P-gp) → lowers levels of cyclosporine, OCPs, many drugs.
12Why was terfenadine withdrawn?
It is a prodrug; CYP3A4 inhibitors (erythromycin, grapefruit) raised the parent level → fatal QT prolongation/arrhythmia.
13What does an inducer mnemonic "CRAP GPS" stand for?
Carbamazepine, Rifampin, Alcohol(chronic), Phenytoin, Griseofulvin/Phenobarbital, Smoking, St John's wort.
14What enzyme detoxifies NAPQI?
Glutathione (conjugation via glutathione-S-transferase).
15How does N-acetylcysteine treat paracetamol toxicity?
It replenishes hepatic glutathione (provides cysteine), allowing NAPQI detoxification.
16What enzyme acetylates isoniazid, and why does it matter?
N-acetyltransferase 2 (NAT2); slow acetylators accumulate INH → neuropathy/hepatotoxicity.
17What does TPMT metabolise?
Thiopurines (azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine); low activity → myelosuppression.
18What is the first-pass effect in relation to metabolism?
Pre-systemic metabolism by gut wall and liver that reduces oral bioavailability.
19Generally, do Phase 1 reactions activate or inactivate drugs?
Usually inactivate — but they can activate prodrugs or create reactive/toxic metabolites.
20Which Phase 2 reaction can produce reactive electrophiles linked to toxicity?
Some conjugations (and Phase 1 oxidations) yield reactive metabolites; the classic toxic intermediate is NAPQI from paracetamol.
7Clinical Toxicology — Antidotes & managementCh 9 · 20 Q
1Whose principle is "the dose makes the poison"?
Paracelsus.
2What does an LD₅₀ value describe?
The dose lethal to 50% of a test population.
3What is a U-shaped (hormetic) dose-response curve?
Both deficiency and excess are harmful — typical of essential metals and hormones.
4What does a hockey-stick curve indicate?
No effect until a saturable removal process is overwhelmed, then effect appears (zero-order saturation).
5List the five steps of poisoning management.
Stabilise (ABCs), decontaminate, give antidote, enhance elimination, supportive care/monitoring.
6What does activated charcoal NOT bind well?
Metals (iron, lithium), alcohols, hydrocarbons, and caustics.
7What does multiple-dose activated charcoal achieve?
Interrupts enterohepatic recirculation (e.g., theophylline, carbamazepine, dapsone, phenobarbital).
8Antidote for paracetamol?
N-acetylcysteine.
9Antidote for opioids?
Naloxone (μ-opioid antagonist).
10Antidote for benzodiazepines, and main caution?
Flumazenil; can precipitate seizures (especially in dependence or co-ingested proconvulsants).
11Antidotes for organophosphate poisoning?
Atropine (blocks muscarinic effects) + pralidoxime (reactivates acetylcholinesterase).
12Antidote for methanol/ethylene glycol?
Fomepizole (or ethanol) — inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase.
13Antidote for iron overdose?
Deferoxamine (chelation).
14Antidote for digoxin toxicity?
Digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Fab).
15Antidote for heparin?
Protamine sulfate (chemical antagonist).
16Antidote for warfarin over-anticoagulation?
Vitamin K, plus FFP or prothrombin complex concentrate for active bleeding.
17Antidote for cyanide?
Hydroxocobalamin and/or sodium thiosulfate (± nitrites).
18Which drug properties favour removal by haemodialysis?
Low Vd, low protein binding, low molecular weight, water-soluble.
19Name dialysable toxins.
Lithium, salicylate, methanol, ethylene glycol, theophylline, valproate, metformin, barbiturates.
20Why is acetaminophen overdose the classic "saturated pathway" example?
Conjugation pathways saturate in overdose, diverting metabolism via CYP2E1 to toxic NAPQI and depleting glutathione → hepatic necrosis.
8Transporters & PharmacogeneticsCh 4 & 7 · 20 Q
1What do SLC transporters generally do?
Mediate uptake (facilitated or secondary active transport) into cells.
2What do ABC transporters do, and what powers them?
Efflux drugs out of cells, powered by ATP hydrolysis.
3Give the alternative names for P-glycoprotein.
ABCB1 / MDR1.
4Name three sites where P-gp is clinically important.
Gut enterocytes (limits absorption), the BBB (limits brain entry), and tumour cells (multidrug resistance).
5Which classic cardiac drug is a P-gp substrate?
Digoxin (P-gp inhibitors like verapamil/quinidine raise its level).
6What does OATP1B1 transport, and a consequence of inhibition?
Hepatic uptake of statins; inhibition/variants → higher statin levels → myopathy.
7Which transporter secretes metformin renally?
OCT2 (organic cation transporter).
8How does probenecid prolong penicillin action?
It inhibits OAT-mediated tubular secretion of penicillin, reducing renal clearance.
9Define a poor metabolizer (PM).
An individual with little/no functional enzyme activity (e.g., two no-function CYP2D6 alleles).
10Define an ultrarapid metabolizer (UM).
Someone with increased enzyme activity, often from gene duplication.
11Codeine in an ultrarapid CYP2D6 metabolizer — risk?
Excess morphine → respiratory depression/toxicity (infant deaths via breast milk reported).
12Codeine in a CYP2D6 poor metabolizer — effect?
Little or no analgesia (cannot form morphine).
13CYP2C19 poor metabolizer on clopidogrel — risk?
Reduced activation → reduced antiplatelet effect → stent thrombosis risk.
14TPMT deficiency with thiopurines — danger?
Severe, potentially fatal myelosuppression.
15UGT1A1*28 with irinotecan — risk?
Increased neutropenia and diarrhoea.
16G6PD deficiency — which drugs cause haemolysis?
Primaquine, rasburicase, sulfonamides, dapsone, nitrofurantoin.
17Which genes guide warfarin dosing?
VKORC1 (target) and CYP2C9 (metabolism).
18Which HLA allele predicts abacavir hypersensitivity?
HLA-B*57:01.
19Which HLA allele predicts carbamazepine SJS/TEN, and in whom?
HLA-B*15:02, particularly in people of Southeast/East Asian ancestry.
20Why does CYP3A5 expression vary by ancestry?
Allele frequencies differ; many individuals carry no-function alleles, affecting drugs like tacrolimus dosing.
9Drug Discovery, Microbiome & Drug SafetyCh 1, 6 & 8 · 20 Q
1What is target validation?
Confirming that modulating a chosen molecular target produces the desired therapeutic effect.
2What does "druggable" mean?
A target that can be modulated by a small molecule or biologic to therapeutic benefit.
3What is high-throughput screening?
Rapid automated testing of large compound libraries against a target to find hits/leads.
4What document must be filed before first-in-human trials?
An Investigational New Drug (IND) application.
5What is studied in Phase I trials?
Safety, tolerability and pharmacokinetics, usually in healthy volunteers.
6What is the focus of Phase II?
Efficacy and dose-finding in patients.
7What does Phase III establish?
Confirmatory efficacy and safety in large patient numbers vs standard therapy.
8What is filed for marketing approval?
A New Drug Application (NDA) — or BLA for biologics.
9What is a biosimilar?
A biologic highly similar to an approved reference biologic — not an identical generic, due to manufacturing complexity.
10Which bacterial enzyme drives enterohepatic recycling of drugs?
Bacterial β-glucuronidase (deconjugates glucuronides, regenerating active drug).
11Which gut bacterium inactivates digoxin?
Eggerthella lenta.
12How do gut bacteria activate sulfasalazine?
They cleave it into 5-aminosalicylate (active) and sulfapyridine.
13What types of reactions does the microbiome predominantly perform?
Reductions, hydrolyses and deconjugations.
14Why is postmarketing surveillance necessary?
Premarketing trials are short and small, missing rare or delayed adverse effects.
15Distinguish Type A from Type B adverse drug reactions.
Type A = augmented, dose-related, predictable, common; Type B = bizarre, idiosyncratic, dose-independent, rare.
16Give an example of a Type B reaction.
Penicillin anaphylaxis or Stevens-Johnson syndrome.
17What is a spontaneous reporting system, with an example?
Voluntary ADR reporting that generates safety signals — e.g., FDA FAERS, India's PvPI/IPC.
18What is a black box warning?
The FDA's strongest labelling warning about a serious or life-threatening risk.
19Name two drugs withdrawn for safety postmarketing.
Rofecoxib (CV events), cerivastatin (rhabdomyolysis); also terfenadine, troglitazone.
20Give an example of a genetic ADR risk factor recognised in pharmacovigilance.
TPMT variants predisposing to thiopurine myelosuppression.
10Integrated rapid-fire — mixed high-yieldAll chapters · 20 Q
1A drug has a very large Vd — is it more likely lipophilic or hydrophilic?
Lipophilic (it partitions widely into tissues, leaving little in plasma).
2Doubling clearance, with Vd unchanged, does what to half-life?
Halves it (t½ ∝ Vd/CL).
3Why give sodium bicarbonate in salicylate poisoning?
It alkalinises urine, ionising and trapping the weak-acid salicylate → enhanced renal excretion.
4A patient on clopidogrel has recurrent stent thrombosis — name a pharmacogenetic cause.
CYP2C19 poor-metaboliser status (failed prodrug activation).
5Starting rifampin lowers a patient's cyclosporine level — mechanism?
CYP3A4 (and P-gp) induction increases cyclosporine metabolism/efflux.
6Why is IV the only reliable route for a drug with ~100% first-pass metabolism?
Oral bioavailability is near zero because the liver/gut clear it before systemic entry.
7A β-blocker is stopped abruptly and the patient has rebound tachycardia — why?
Chronic blockade up-regulated β-receptors (supersensitivity).
8Which is more dangerous to overdose: a high-TI or low-TI drug?
Low-TI drug (narrow margin between effective and toxic doses).
9Naloxone reverses opioid overdose by acting as what?
A competitive μ-opioid receptor antagonist.
10A chronic alcoholic takes a modest paracetamol overdose and develops severe hepatotoxicity — why?
CYP2E1 induction increases NAPQI formation, overwhelming glutathione.
11Which receptor superfamily do steroids act on, and how fast?
Nuclear receptors — slow (hours–days), via gene transcription.
12Salbutamol acts on β₂ receptors coupled to which G-protein/messenger?
Gs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP.
13A partial agonist given with a full agonist reduces the response — what is it acting as?
A competitive (functional) antagonist.
14Grapefruit juice raises a statin's level — mechanism and risk?
Inhibits intestinal CYP3A4 → higher statin exposure → myopathy/rhabdomyolysis risk.
15Why is digoxin given with a loading dose?
Its long half-life would otherwise delay therapeutic levels for days; LD fills the large Vd quickly.
16A drug is highly protein-bound with small Vd — is it dialysable?
Poorly — most drug is bound/in plasma compartment but protein binding limits free drug removal; large Vd also reduces dialysability. (Best dialysed: low binding + low Vd.)
17An irreversible antagonist does what to Emax?
Reduces it (non-surmountable).
18Phenytoin shows a sudden jump from therapeutic to toxic levels with small dose increases — why?
It follows zero-order (saturable) kinetics at therapeutic doses.
19A test for HLA-B*57:01 is ordered before which drug?
Abacavir (to prevent hypersensitivity).
20State the master equation linking maintenance dose to clearance and target level.
Dosing rate = CL × Css(target); equivalently Css = F·Dose/(CL·τ).
