📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

The branch point is everything

Phospholipase A₂ liberates arachidonic acid from the membrane; from there it can go down COX (→ prostanoids) or 5-LOX (→ leukotrienes). Because the two branches share one substrate pool, blocking one branch can shunt substrate down the other — the mechanism behind aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (more arachidonic acid → more leukotrienes → bronchospasm).

💡 The Feynman Analogy — a river that forks

Arachidonic acid is a river fed by one upstream dam (phospholipase A₂). Downstream it forks into two channels: the COX channel (prostaglandins) and the LOX channel (leukotrienes). Glucocorticoids close the dam — both channels run dry, the broadest effect. NSAIDs dam only the COX channel — but the water still has to go somewhere, so it surges down the leukotriene channel (why NSAIDs can trigger bronchospasm in sensitive asthmatics). Montelukast doesn't touch the river at all — it just blocks the leukotriene "outflow valve" (the CysLT₁ receptor) at the destination.

⚠️ Warning: PAF (platelet-activating factor) is a separate phospholipid mediator, not an eicosanoid, but it amplifies the same inflammatory response (bronchoconstriction, ↑vascular permeability). And remember the protective face of prostaglandins: COX-1-derived PGE₂/PGI₂ guard the gastric mucosa and maintain renal perfusion — which is exactly why blocking them causes the classic NSAID toxicities in the next module.

📝 Section 3 · Immaculate Feynman Notes

Why one mechanism explains both benefit and harm

Prostaglandins do two jobs: they signal inflammation, pain and fever (bad), and they maintain the gastric mucus barrier, renal perfusion and the platelet/endothelium balance (good). An NSAID can't tell the difference — block COX and you lose all of it at once. That's why the GI, renal and bleeding effects aren't side-effects in the usual sense; they're the same effect in a different tissue.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — firing the whole maintenance crew

Prostaglandins are a maintenance crew working all over a building. At the inflamed site they're the ones ringing the fire alarm (pain, fever, swelling). But the same crew also reglazes the stomach lining, keeps the kidney's water pressure steady, and balances the "sticky vs slippery" setting of the blood. An NSAID fires the entire crew — the alarm stops (great), but no one is patching the stomach lining (ulcer), managing renal pressure (fluid retention, AKI) or balancing clotting (bleeding). A coxib fires only the alarm-ringers (COX-2), sparing the stomach crew — but it also removes the endothelium's anti-clotting signal (PGI₂) while leaving platelets' pro-clotting TXA₂ intact, tipping toward thrombosis.

⚠️ Warning — high-yield traps: Aspirin in children with viral illness → Reye syndrome (avoid). NSAID "triple whammy" = NSAID + ACEi/ARB + diuretic → acute kidney injury. Allopurinol can precipitate an acute flare if started during one, and causes severe reactions (HLA-B*58:01); it also blocks metabolism of azathioprine/6-MP (both xanthine-oxidase substrates) → toxicity. Never give aspirin in gout — low doses are urate-retaining.

Relievers treat the symptom; controllers treat the disease

A β₂-agonist raises cAMP in airway smooth muscle, relaxing the spasm within minutes — but it does nothing about the inflammation that caused the spasm. Inhaled corticosteroids switch off the inflammatory gene programme over days to weeks, shrinking the underlying problem so attacks become rarer.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — fire alarm vs the fire

The inhaled corticosteroid is the fire crew putting out the actual flames. LABAs must always ride with an ICS.

In COPD, the dominant bronchodilator tone is cholinergic, so LAMAs/SAMAs matter more than in asthma.

Replace what's missing — but identify it first

Both B₁₂ and folate are needed for DNA synthesis, so deficiency of either gives a macrocytic anaemia. But B₁₂ has a second job — maintaining the myelin sheath — so only B₁₂ deficiency causes neurological signs. 💡 The Feynman Analogy — a factory missing different supplies

The bone marrow is a red-cell factory. Iron is the raw brick — without it, cells come out small and pale (microcytic). B₁₂ and folate are the blueprints for copying DNA — without them, cells can't divide properly and come out large and immature (macrocytic). Erythropoietin is the foreman shouting "make more!" — in kidney disease the foreman is gone, so production stalls even though bricks and blueprints are plentiful. And the dangerous twist: B₁₂ also maintains the factory's electrical wiring (myelin). If you hand over only folate, you restock the blueprints and the anaemia improves — masking the fact that the wiring is still corroding, leading to irreversible neurological damage.

⚠️ Warning: Always check B₁₂ before treating macrocytic anaemia with folate — folate alone corrects the blood picture but lets subacute combined degeneration of the cord progress. Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents raise thrombosis/stroke risk and need adequate iron to work — don't over-correct haemoglobin. Oral iron is poorly tolerated (GI upset, black stools) and absorption is reduced by PPIs and antacids.

Two arms, and what happens when they overreact

💡 The Feynman Analogy — security guards vs detectives

Innate immunity is the building's security guards — they tackle anyone suspicious instantly, but they don't remember faces. Adaptive immunity is the detective unit — slow to build a case the first time, but they keep a permanent file, so the second time the same intruder appears they respond instantly (that file is immunological memory, and it's what a vaccine pre-loads). Hypersensitivity is when this security overreacts: an itchy trigger-finger (Type I allergy), guards attacking the building's own staff (Type II), debris from fights clogging the corridors (Type III immune complexes), or the detectives mounting a slow, heavy raid days later (Type IV). Checkpoint molecules are the "stand down" orders that stop friendly fire — cancer hides behind them, and checkpoint inhibitors cancel the stand-down (unleashing the attack, but risking friendly fire = autoimmunity).

Teach vs hand-over

A vaccine teaches the adaptive immune system to recognise an antigen and form memory — protection is delayed but durable. An immune globulin hands over finished antibodies — protection is instant but borrowed, fading as those antibodies are cleared. In a rabies exposure you need both: the globulin covers you now while the vaccine builds lasting defence.

💡 The Feynman Analogy — teaching to fish vs handing over fish

Active immunisation is teaching someone to fish — it takes time before they can feed themselves, but then they can do it for life. Passive immunisation is handing them a cooked fish — they eat immediately, but when it's gone they're hungry again with no new skill. A rabies exposure is an emergency where you do both: hand over a fish for tonight (immune globulin) and start the fishing lessons (vaccine) for the weeks ahead.

⚠️ Warning: live-attenuated vaccines are contraindicated in pregnancy and significant immunocompromise (risk of disease from the vaccine strain). 🃏 Clinical Vignette Flashcards

Tap a card to reveal the answer, mechanism, clinical pearl, and exam trap. Twenty high-yield scenarios spanning inflammation, immunity, and haematopoiesis pharmacology.

01 A 28-year-old woman with SLE is found to have anti-dsDNA antibodies, low complement (C3/C4), proteinuria 3.2 g/day, and haematuria. Renal biopsy shows diffuse proliferative lupus nephritis (class IV). Which drug specifically targets the B-cell survival factor implicated in lupus?
Belimumab — a monoclonal antibody targeting BLyS (B-lymphocyte stimulator / BAFF), a cytokine essential for B-cell survival and maturation. In SLE, excess BLyS promotes survival of autoreactive B cells → anti-dsDNA antibody production → immune complex deposition in glomeruli → complement activation → nephritis. Belimumab reduces B-cell counts, lowers anti-dsDNA titres, and reduces flare rates. It is approved as add-on therapy to standard care (hydroxychloroquine ± immunosuppression) for active, autoantibody-positive SLE.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Class IV lupus nephritis (diffuse proliferative) is the most severe and most common — treat aggressively with mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) or cyclophosphamide + high-dose steroids. MMF is now preferred over cyclophosphamide as induction in non-severe cases (comparable efficacy, better toxicity profile). Voclosporin (calcineurin inhibitor) + MMF is a newer combination with faster proteinuria remission.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Anti-dsDNA and anti-Sm antibodies are specific to SLE. Anti-Sm (anti-Smith) is highly specific (~25–30% sensitive but >99% specific) and does NOT fluctuate with disease activity. Anti-dsDNA titres DO fluctuate with disease activity and correlate with lupus nephritis — use to monitor, not diagnose.
02 Rituximab is given for rheumatoid arthritis. Six months later the patient develops progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML). Which immune cell did rituximab deplete, and why did this lead to PML?
Rituximab depletes CD20+ B cells via complement-dependent cytotoxicity and ADCC. JC virus (John Cunningham polyomavirus) normally causes asymptomatic latent infection in most adults — kept in check by CD4+ T cells AND B cells (which provide immune surveillance and contribute to anti-JCV humoral immunity). Rituximab-induced profound B-cell depletion (lasting 6–12 months) reduces anti-JCV antibody levels and impairs viral immune control → JC virus reactivation in oligodendrocytes → demyelination → PML.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Anti-JCV antibody serology before rituximab does NOT reliably predict PML risk the way it does with natalizumab. For rituximab, duration of therapy and cumulative B-cell depletion are the main risk factors. Screen for JCV antibodies; monitor for neurological symptoms throughout therapy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Rituximab does NOT deplete plasma cells (which are CD20−) — immunoglobulin levels (IgG) fall slowly over months but existing antibodies persist initially. Do NOT give live vaccines after rituximab (B cells are depleted → no antibody response). Minimum 6-month gap before live vaccine; check B-cell reconstitution first.
03 A patient on methotrexate for RA develops a dry cough, dyspnoea, and bilateral interstitial infiltrates on CXR after 8 months of therapy. What is the diagnosis and mechanism?
Methotrexate pneumonitis — an idiosyncratic, immune-mediated hypersensitivity reaction (NOT dose-dependent, NOT predictable from folate levels). MTX inhibits dihydrofolate reductase → reduced tetrahydrofolate → impaired purine/thymidylate synthesis. In the lung, MTX may trigger a CD4+ T-cell-mediated granulomatous/hypersensitivity reaction → pneumonitis with BAL showing lymphocytosis. Stop methotrexate immediately; give corticosteroids for moderate-severe cases. MTX cannot be restarted after pneumonitis.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Distinguish MTX pneumonitis from infection before stopping immunosuppression. Key features suggesting MTX pneumonitis: onset 8–18 months (can be earlier), fever + peripheral eosinophilia, bilateral ground-glass opacities, NO infective organism on BAL. Exclude PCP (order beta-D-glucan, BAL PCR) before starting steroids.
⚠️ Exam Trap: MTX hepatotoxicity (fibrosis → cirrhosis) is dose- and duration-dependent and prevented by folate supplementation. Pneumonitis is NOT prevented by folate — these are different toxicity mechanisms. High-dose MTX for cancer can cause acute tubular necrosis (crystallisation in renal tubules) — prevented by urine alkalinisation and leucovorin rescue.
04 A patient with Crohn's disease on infliximab for 2 years develops fever, night sweats, and a new cavitating upper lobe lung lesion. TB IGRA is positive. What happened, and when was this risk preventable?
Reactivation of latent tuberculosis (LTBI) due to TNF-α blockade. TNF-α is essential for granuloma formation and maintenance — it promotes macrophage activation and containment of mycobacteria within caseating granulomas. Infliximab (anti-TNF-α monoclonal antibody) disrupts this defence → latent TB reactivates, often presenting as extrapulmonary or disseminated disease (pleural, abdominal, miliary). This risk was preventable by LTBI screening before starting infliximab — IGRA or TST + CXR — and treating LTBI with isoniazid for 9 months before beginning biologic therapy.
💎 Clinical Pearl: All TNF inhibitors (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept, certolizumab, golimumab) carry TB reactivation risk. Screen ALL patients for LTBI before initiating. Treat LTBI for ≥4 weeks before starting the biologic if possible (some guidelines say 2–4 weeks minimum). Risk is highest with monoclonal antibodies vs etanercept (soluble receptor).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Etanercept (soluble TNF receptor fusion protein) has a lower TB reactivation risk than anti-TNF monoclonal antibodies — it does not neutralise membrane-bound TNF as efficiently. However, LTBI screening is still mandatory before all TNF blockers including etanercept.
05 A child with X-linked agammaglobulinaemia (XLA) presents repeatedly with encapsulated bacterial infections (Streptococcus pneumoniae, H. influenzae). What is missing, at what developmental stage, and what is the treatment?
XLA is caused by mutations in Bruton's tyrosine kinase (BTK) — essential for pre-B cell → B cell maturation in the bone marrow. BTK signalling transduces signals downstream of the pre-B cell receptor. Without BTK: B-cell development is arrested at the pro-B/pre-B cell stage → no mature B cells → no plasma cells → near-zero serum IgG/IgA/IgM → profound humoral immunodeficiency. Encapsulated bacteria (with polysaccharide capsules) require opsonising antibodies for phagocytic killing — without IgG, patients cannot clear these organisms. Treatment: lifelong IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin) replacement every 3–4 weeks.
💎 Clinical Pearl: BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib, acalabrutinib) are used to treat B-cell malignancies (CLL, mantle cell lymphoma) — they intentionally exploit BTK's role in B-cell survival. A side effect is immunosuppression with encapsulated bacterial infections — mirroring XLA pathophysiology.
⚠️ Exam Trap: XLA presents after ~6 months of age when maternal IgG (transplacental) wanes. Boys are affected (X-linked). Distinguish from common variable immunodeficiency (CVID) — which presents in adulthood with low immunoglobulins but B cells ARE present (B-cell function is defective, not absent). Both treated with IVIG.
06 A 45-year-old patient on long-term prednisolone (15 mg/day for 2 years) for autoimmune hepatitis develops avascular necrosis of the femoral head. What is the mechanism, and what other corticosteroid complications should be monitored?
Corticosteroid-induced avascular necrosis: chronic GC excess → adipocyte hypertrophy in bone marrow → fat emboli occlude terminal end-arteries of the femoral head (and other susceptible sites) → ischaemic bone death. Mechanism also involves GC-induced coagulopathy. The femoral head, humeral head, and femoral condyle are classic sites. Monitoring for corticosteroid complications: hyperglycaemia/diabetes (GCs → gluconeogenesis + insulin resistance); osteoporosis (inhibit osteoblasts, increase RANKL, reduce calcium absorption → start bisphosphonate + calcium/vit D if >3 months at >5 mg/day); Cushingoid features, hypertension, hypokalaemia, posterior subcapsular cataracts, skin atrophy, adrenal suppression.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Adrenal suppression is a serious GC complication — hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis suppressed if prednisolone >5 mg/day for >3 weeks. Abrupt withdrawal can precipitate Addisonian crisis. Taper gradually; cover with stress-dose steroids (hydrocortisone 50–100 mg IV 8-hourly) during surgery or severe illness.
⚠️ Exam Trap: PCP prophylaxis with TMP-SMX is recommended when prednisolone dose exceeds 20 mg/day for >4 weeks (especially with other immunosuppressants). GC-induced immune suppression particularly affects cell-mediated immunity → increased risk of fungal, TB, PCP, herpes zoster, and strongyloides hyperinfection.
07 Cyclosporin is given after renal transplantation. Trough levels are maintained at 150–200 ng/mL. What is its mechanism, and what drug would dangerously elevate cyclosporin levels?
Cyclosporin binds cyclophilin → the cyclosporin–cyclophilin complex inhibits calcineurin (a serine/threonine phosphatase) → prevents dephosphorylation of NFAT (nuclear factor of activated T cells) → NFAT cannot translocate to the nucleus → IL-2 gene transcription blocked → T-cell activation and clonal expansion suppressed. Tacrolimus has the same target (calcineurin) but binds FKBP12 instead of cyclophilin. Drugs that dangerously elevate cyclosporin: azole antifungals (fluconazole, voriconazole — CYP3A4 inhibitors), macrolide antibiotics (clarithromycin, erythromycin — CYP3A4 inhibitors), diltiazem, verapamil. All increase cyclosporin AUC → nephrotoxicity, hypertension, neurotoxicity.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Cyclosporin causes gingival hyperplasia (also caused by phenytoin and nifedipine — all share this side effect via gingival fibroblast proliferation) and hirsutism. Tacrolimus causes neither — instead causes alopecia. Tacrolimus has higher risk of post-transplant diabetes mellitus (PTDM) than cyclosporin.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Rifampin is a potent CYP3A4 inducer → dramatically reduces cyclosporin levels → acute rejection. St John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) similarly induces CYP3A4 → major interaction with cyclosporin, tacrolimus, oral contraceptives, and antiretrovirals. Warn transplant patients explicitly about herbal supplements.
08 A patient with Wegener's granulomatosis (GPA) is PR3-ANCA positive with pulmonary haemorrhage and rapidly progressive GN. Which alkylating agent is used for induction, and what is its most feared long-term complication?
Cyclophosphamide (IV pulse, preferred over oral for ANCA vasculitis induction per CYCLOPS trial — equivalent efficacy, lower cumulative dose). Cyclophosphamide is a prodrug activated by CYP2B6/3A4 to phosphoramide mustard → alkylates DNA → cross-links double strands → inhibits rapidly dividing cells (neutrophils, lymphocytes). Most feared long-term complication: haemorrhagic cystitis (acrolein metabolite accumulates in urine → urothelial damage) → prevented with MESNA (2-mercaptoethane sulphonate) which reacts with acrolein in the urine, and aggressive IV hydration. Long-term: transitional cell carcinoma of the bladder (lifetime monitoring required), gonadal failure (especially in women), secondary malignancies.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Rituximab (anti-CD20) is now equally effective to cyclophosphamide for ANCA vasculitis induction and is preferred in: younger patients, women of childbearing age, those with PR3-ANCA (where rituximab may be superior for preventing relapse), or where cyclophosphamide toxicity is a major concern.
⚠️ Exam Trap: The ANCA vasculitis maintenance phase uses rituximab or azathioprine (NOT cyclophosphamide) — ongoing cyclophosphamide use has unacceptable cumulative toxicity. Switch to maintenance after 3–6 months of induction.
09 A 35-year-old man with psoriatic arthritis fails methotrexate. He is started on adalimumab (anti-TNF). Before the second dose, he phones with a fever of 39°C, rigors, and looks unwell. What complication do you suspect, and how is it managed?
Adalimumab-induced serious infection — TNF-α blockade predisposes to bacterial sepsis (especially Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae), TB reactivation, invasive fungal infections (histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis, aspergillosis), and opportunistic infections. In an acutely unwell patient: hold adalimumab immediately, obtain blood cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics empirically, investigate for source. Evaluate for TB (CXR, IGRA), fungal infection if endemic exposure, and viral infections. TNF blockers should not be restarted until the infection is fully resolved and the causative organism identified.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Before starting any biologic: screen for LTBI (IGRA + CXR), hepatitis B (HBsAg, anti-HBc — TNF blockers can reactivate HBV → fulminant hepatitis), HCV status, varicella immunity, and ensure up-to-date vaccinations (live vaccines must be given ≥4 weeks before, not during, biologic therapy).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis, and blastomycosis are endemic fungal infections that may reactivate with TNF blockade — particularly in patients from the Ohio/Mississippi river valley or southwestern US. Serum antigen testing (Histoplasma urine antigen) is the fastest test. These infections can look like miliary TB on CXR — a critical differential.
10 A patient starting hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) for SLE asks about eye complications. Explain the mechanism of retinal toxicity and the monitoring strategy.
HCQ accumulates in melanin-containing tissues including the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). Over years, HCQ binds to RPE melanin → lysosomal enzyme inhibition and mitochondrial damage → RPE cell death → photoreceptor degeneration. The characteristic lesion is bull's-eye maculopathy (ring of RPE loss around the central macula). Risk factors: cumulative dose >5 mg/kg/day ideal body weight (NOT actual weight — use ideal), duration >5 years, CKD (reduced excretion → accumulation), tamoxifen co-administration (independently toxic to retina). Monitoring: baseline ophthalmological exam within first year, then annually after 5 years (or earlier if risk factors).
💎 Clinical Pearl: HCQ is the cornerstone of SLE therapy — it reduces flares, nephritis, and cardiovascular mortality, and is safe in pregnancy (low teratogenicity, actually reduces obstetric complications in SLE/antiphospholipid syndrome). The dose threshold for retinal safety is ≤5 mg/kg/day of ideal body weight.
⚠️ Exam Trap: HCQ can cause QT prolongation — relevant with concurrent azithromycin or other QT-prolonging drugs. HCQ also inhibits CYP2D6 → may raise plasma levels of codeine, metoprolol, and other CYP2D6 substrates. Do NOT confuse HCQ (safe) with chloroquine (higher retinal toxicity at equivalent doses).
11 Iron deficiency anaemia is confirmed in a 65-year-old woman with colorectal cancer. Oral iron is poorly tolerated. What is the mechanism of action of IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose), and what acute reaction must be monitored?
IV iron: ferric carboxymaltose is a colloidal iron complex → taken up by macrophages in the reticuloendothelial system (liver, spleen, bone marrow) → iron released from the carbohydrate shell → stored as ferritin → mobilised for erythropoiesis (incorporated into haemoglobin by erythroid precursors in bone marrow). Monitoring: acute hypophosphataemia (specific to ferric carboxymaltose — triggers FGF23 release → renal phosphate wasting → severe hypophosphataemia, osteomalacia with repeated dosing). Also monitor for acute hypersensitivity reactions (complement activation by the iron complex → anaphylactoid reaction — NOT IgE-mediated). Pre-medication and slow infusion + resuscitation equipment available.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Intravenous iron is preferred over oral iron for: chemotherapy-induced anaemia, pre-surgical optimisation, IBD patients (poor GI absorption/tolerance), CKD patients on erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESA — EPO requires adequate iron for erythropoiesis). Iron sucrose has a lower risk of hypophosphataemia than ferric carboxymaltose.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Oral ferrous sulfate absorption requires an acidic environment — proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) significantly reduce oral iron absorption. Space oral iron and PPI by >2 hours. Oral iron also interacts with antacids, calcium supplements, and fluoroquinolones (chelation). Ascorbic acid (vitamin C) enhances non-haem iron absorption.
12 A patient develops thrombocytopenia 7 days after starting unfractionated heparin (UFH) post-surgery. Platelet count drops from 220 to 55 ×10⁹/L. Explain the mechanism and immediate management.
Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia type II (HIT) — an immune-mediated thrombotic disorder. Heparin binds platelet factor 4 (PF4) → heparin-PF4 complex is immunogenic → IgG antibodies form against the complex → antibody-PF4-heparin complex binds and activates platelets via FcγRIIa → platelet activation → platelet consumption (thrombocytopenia) AND pro-thrombotic state (paradoxical thrombosis despite low platelets). Immediate management: (1) STOP all heparin immediately (including heparin flushes, LMWH); (2) Start a non-heparin anticoagulant — argatroban (direct thrombin inhibitor, preferred in renal failure), bivalirudin, or fondaparinux; (3) Do NOT start warfarin yet (protein C depletion worsens thrombosis — only start after platelet recovery to >150).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Use the 4T score to assess HIT probability: Thrombocytopenia (≥50% drop, nadir >20 = 2 pts), Timing (5–10 days = 2 pts), Thrombosis (new = 2 pts), other causes of Thrombocytopenia (none = 2 pts). Score ≥6 = high probability. Confirm with ELISA (PF4 antibody) + functional assay (serotonin release assay — gold standard).
⚠️ Exam Trap: HIT type I is a benign, non-immune direct effect of heparin on platelets — occurs in first 2 days, mild thrombocytopenia (rarely <100), no thrombosis, platelet count normalises spontaneously with continued heparin. HIT TYPE II is the dangerous immune-mediated version — occurs days 5–10 (or earlier if prior heparin exposure within 100 days).
13 A patient with aplastic anaemia requires immunosuppressive therapy. What is the rationale for combining anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG) with ciclosporin?
Aplastic anaemia is largely immune-mediated: autoreactive T cells (particularly CD8+ cytotoxic T cells releasing IFN-γ and TNF-α) destroy haematopoietic stem cells (HSC) → bone marrow failure. Rationale for combination: ATG (equine or rabbit-derived polyclonal antibodies against human thymocytes) depletes T cells via complement-mediated cytotoxicity and ADCC, removing the effector cells attacking the HSC niche. Ciclosporin inhibits calcineurin → blocks IL-2 → suppresses residual T-cell activation, preventing recurrence after ATG. Adding eltrombopag (TPO receptor agonist) to ATG + ciclosporin triples complete response rates (NEJM 2017) by stimulating HSC proliferation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: ATG (equine) infusion causes serum sickness 7–14 days later (fever, urticaria, arthralgia, lymphadenopathy) — immune complex deposition. Prevent/treat with corticosteroids. Monitor for anaphylaxis during the first infusion — always administer a test dose first.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Aplastic anaemia must be distinguished from hypocellular MDS (myelodysplastic syndrome) and paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH) — both can present similarly. PNH clone (GPI-anchor deficiency → susceptibility to complement → haemolysis + thrombosis) can co-exist with aplastic anaemia. Test with flow cytometry for CD55/CD59 deficiency. Eculizumab (anti-C5) treats PNH haemolysis and thrombosis.
14 A patient with polycythaemia vera (PV) is at high risk of thrombosis. Beyond aspirin and phlebotomy, what is the cytoreductive drug of choice, and what is the molecular target therapy for patients failing or intolerant of it?
First-line cytoreduction for high-risk PV (age >60, or prior thrombosis): hydroxyurea (hydroxycarbamide) — inhibits ribonucleotide reductase → reduces dNTP pool → impairs DNA synthesis → selectively reduces rapidly dividing haematopoietic cells (RBCs, platelets, WBCs). Molecular target therapy for HU-resistant/intolerant PV: ruxolitinib — a JAK1/JAK2 inhibitor. >95% of PV patients carry the JAK2 V617F mutation (point mutation in the pseudokinase domain → constitutive JAK2 activation → cytokine-independent haematopoiesis). Ruxolitinib blocks JAK1/2 → reduces cell proliferation, splenomegaly, and symptom burden.
💎 Clinical Pearl: JAK2 V617F is present in ~95% of PV, ~50% of ET, and ~50% of PMF. Calreticulin (CALR) mutations are found in ~25% of ET and PMF but NOT in PV. MPL mutations in ~5% of ET/PMF. JAK2 exon 12 mutations are PV-specific. Panel testing for JAK2/CALR/MPL is the molecular workup for MPNs.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Hydroxyurea is teratogenic — contraindicated in pregnancy. For PV in pregnancy, use interferon-alpha (preferred — non-teratogenic, reduces JAK2 V617F allele burden). Aspirin 75–100 mg daily is given to all PV patients (reduces thrombosis; at low dose avoids paradoxical bleeding from von Willebrand abnormality seen in extreme thrombocytosis).
15 A 52-year-old man with CLL is started on ibrutinib. Six months later he develops atrial fibrillation and a major gastrointestinal bleed. What is the mechanistic explanation?
Ibrutinib is a covalent inhibitor of Bruton's tyrosine kinase (BTK). BTK is essential for BCR signalling → B-cell survival (tumour) AND platelet activation (via GPVI collagen receptor signalling). Ibrutinib off-target inhibition of TEC kinase and platelet BTK impairs: (1) Platelet aggregation (collagen-induced platelet function) → bleeding risk (major bleeding ~6% per year); (2) Atrial fibrillation — mechanism involves cardiac BTK/TEC kinase effects on atrial electrical remodelling and inflammation, plus autonomic effects. Overall AF risk ~10–15% (significantly higher than standard therapy). BTK inhibition also causes ibrutinib-specific AF — typically managed by switching to acalabrutinib or zanubrutinib (more selective BTK inhibitors with lower AF and bleeding rates).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Ibrutinib + warfarin: concurrent anticoagulation dramatically increases bleeding risk. DOAC + ibrutinib: also increases bleeding but may be preferable to warfarin. For AF requiring anticoagulation in a patient on ibrutinib, consider switching to a next-gen BTK inhibitor + DOAC with careful risk-benefit analysis.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Ibrutinib is a CYP3A4 substrate — rifampin dramatically reduces levels; azole antifungals dramatically increase levels. Given the frequency of fungal prophylaxis in CLL patients on ibrutinib, this interaction is clinically critical.
16 A patient with multiple myeloma undergoes stem cell transplant conditioning with high-dose melphalan. Forty-eight hours later he develops painful oral ulcers, diarrhoea, and neutropaenia. What defines his mucosal recovery, and what drug reduces the duration and severity of mucositis?
High-dose melphalan → alkylation of rapidly dividing cells → mucositis (basal epithelial cell loss in oral mucosa, GI tract) AND neutropaenia (bone marrow ablation — cells nadir ~day 10, recover when engraftment occurs). Palifermin (recombinant keratinocyte growth factor-1, KGF-1) — given before conditioning → stimulates epithelial cell proliferation and differentiation in the mucosa → thickens mucosal barrier → reduces mucositis severity and duration. Mucosal recovery requires engraftment (WBC recovery) — supported by G-CSF (filgrastim) which stimulates neutrophil production from the transplanted stem cells.
💎 Clinical Pearl: G-CSF (filgrastim) mechanism: binds G-CSF receptor on neutrophil precursors → JAK2/STAT3 signalling → accelerated granulopoiesis → shorter duration of neutropaenia. Pegfilgrastim is pegylated G-CSF (longer half-life → single dose per chemotherapy cycle). GM-CSF (sargramostim) stimulates both myeloid and monocyte lineages.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Palifermin is contraindicated in solid tumours — KGF receptors on tumour cells (particularly squamous cell cancers) may stimulate tumour growth. Palifermin is approved specifically for haematological malignancies receiving myeloablative conditioning. Do NOT give in head-and-neck cancer or other KGF receptor-expressing malignancies.
17 A 65-year-old with immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) fails corticosteroids and rituximab. What is the mechanism of eltrombopag and romiplostim, and when would splenectomy be considered?
Eltrombopag: small molecule that binds the transmembrane domain of the thrombopoietin receptor (c-Mpl/TPO-R) — activates JAK2/STAT5 signalling → megakaryocyte proliferation and maturation → increased platelet production. Does NOT compete with endogenous TPO. Romiplostim: peptibody (Fc-peptide fusion) that mimics TPO → binds extracellular domain of TPO-R → same downstream signalling. Both are TPO receptor agonists — they increase platelet production to compensate for immune-mediated destruction. Splenectomy is considered after ≥12 months of ITP (to allow spontaneous remission chance) when 2 lines of therapy fail — the spleen is the primary site of anti-platelet IgG production AND platelet destruction. ~60–70% durable response.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Eltrombopag has an additional off-target effect: chelates non-transferrin-bound iron in hepatocytes and HSCs → stimulates HSC proliferation. This is the basis for its use in aplastic anaemia (iron chelation from HSC niche → promotes HSC self-renewal independent of TPO-R effect).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Eltrombopag must be taken on an empty stomach — polyvalent cation chelation (calcium, aluminium, iron, magnesium) reduces absorption by up to 70%. Take 2 hours before or 4 hours after dairy, antacids, or mineral supplements. This is a commonly missed pharmacokinetic interaction.
18 Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) is used in transplant immunosuppression. What is its mechanism, and why is it specific to lymphocytes?
MMF is a prodrug hydrolysed to mycophenolic acid (MPA) → inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase (IMPDH) — the rate-limiting enzyme in the de novo purine synthesis pathway (converts IMP → xanthine monophosphate → GMP). Specificity to lymphocytes: most cells (hepatocytes, enterocytes, renal tubular cells) can use the salvage pathway (recycle purines from nucleoside breakdown) to meet GMP requirements. T and B lymphocytes lack an effective salvage pathway and rely almost entirely on de novo synthesis → MMF preferentially suppresses lymphocyte proliferation with relative sparing of other rapidly dividing cells.
💎 Clinical Pearl: MMF is used in: renal/cardiac/hepatic transplant rejection prophylaxis, lupus nephritis, IgA nephropathy, ANCA vasculitis maintenance. It is safer than azathioprine (less myelosuppression, no thiopurine methyltransferase issue) and has replaced azathioprine in most transplant protocols.
⚠️ Exam Trap: MMF is a teratogen (FDA category D/X) — causes ear malformations (microtia), cleft palate, and cardiac defects. Requires negative pregnancy test before starting and reliable contraception throughout. The FDA issued a specific black-box warning after reports of MMF embryopathy. Switch to azathioprine if pregnancy is planned.
19 A patient with myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) with del(5q) is treated with lenalidomide. Explain the mechanism and the unique teratogenicity concern.
Lenalidomide targets cereblon (CRBN), a substrate-recognition component of the CRL4 E3 ubiquitin ligase complex. Lenalidomide binding to CRBN changes its substrate specificity → the complex now ubiquitinates and degrades IKZF1 (Ikaros) and IKZF3 (Aiolos) — transcription factors essential for myeloma and certain lymphoma cell survival. In del(5q) MDS: lenalidomide also degrades CK1α (encoded on chromosome 5q) — CK1α haploinsufficiency from del(5q) makes MDS cells exquisitely sensitive to further CK1α degradation → selective cytotoxicity against del(5q) clone. Teratogenicity: lenalidomide is a thalidomide analogue → phocomelia (limb defects) and other severe fetal malformations. REMS programme required (RevAssist) — monthly pregnancy tests, male contraception, no blood/semen donation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Thalidomide was originally a morning sickness drug withdrawn in the 1960s after causing phocomelia in thousands of infants. Its mechanism of teratogenicity is now known: cereblon-mediated degradation of SALL4 (transcription factor essential for limb development). The same cereblon mechanism is its anticancer mechanism — thalidomide is now a bedrock of myeloma therapy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Lenalidomide + dexamethasone in myeloma: high risk of venous thromboembolism (DVT/PE) — prophylactic anticoagulation required (aspirin for low-risk; LMWH or DOAC for high-risk). Also causes peripheral neuropathy (less than thalidomide but still significant) and cytopenias (neutropenia requiring G-CSF support).
20 A 22-year-old woman with immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) has a platelet count of 8 ×10⁹/L and intracranial haemorrhage. She needs rapid platelet increase. What three interventions would you give simultaneously?
Life-threatening ITP (ICH) requires rapid multi-modal platelet rescue: (1) IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day × 3 days — blocks splenic macrophage FcγR → reduces phagocytosis of opsonised platelets; also reduces anti-platelet antibody synthesis. (2) IVIG 1 g/kg/day × 2 days — saturates splenic FcγR (Fc receptor blockade) → reduces platelet destruction; fastest mechanism (platelets rise within 24–48 hours). (3) Platelet transfusion — in ITP, transfused platelets are rapidly destroyed (same as native platelets), but in ICH, give anyway: any temporary increase in platelet count buys time for haemostasis. Anti-D immunoglobulin (if Rh-positive, non-splenectomised) is an alternative to IVIG. Consider romiplostim/eltrombopag for sustained response.
💎 Clinical Pearl: IVIG response is faster than steroids in acute severe ITP. In ICH, the combination of IVIG + steroids achieves higher and faster platelet increments than either alone. Add platelet transfusion despite their rapid destruction — the brief window of platelet activity can slow bleeding.
⚠️ Exam Trap: IVIG in ITP does NOT block anti-platelet antibody production (that is the role of rituximab, which takes weeks). IVIG works by FcγR blockade — a temporary measure. Platelet counts often fall again after IVIG effect wears off (7–10 days). Plan definitive therapy (rituximab, splenectomy, TPO-RA) simultaneously.

Risk of Reye syndrome.

Both are inactivated by xanthine oxidase; allopurinol blocks it → drug accumulates → myelosuppression.
3Pulmonary PharmacologyModule 44 · 15 Q
1What are the two underlying problems in asthma?
Acute bronchoconstriction and chronic airway inflammation.
2Reliever vs controller — what does each address?
Relievers (bronchodilators) open the airway fast; controllers (anti-inflammatory) treat the underlying inflammation.
3Mechanism of salbutamol?
Short-acting β₂-agonist → ↑cAMP → smooth muscle relaxation.
4What is the controller cornerstone of asthma therapy?
Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS).
5Why must a LABA never be used as monotherapy in asthma?
LABA monotherapy increases asthma deaths — must be combined with an ICS.
6Mechanism of tiotropium and where is it most useful?
Long-acting muscarinic (M₃) antagonist; mainstay in COPD.
7Mechanism and role of montelukast?
CysLT₁ receptor antagonist; add-on controller, useful in allergic/exercise-induced asthma.
8Why is theophylline rarely first-line?
Narrow therapeutic index (nausea, arrhythmia, seizures).
9Theophylline is metabolised by which enzyme, and what happens in smokers?
CYP1A2; smoking induces it → lower levels (may need higher dose).
10Give two biologic options for severe asthma and their targets.
Omalizumab (anti-IgE); mepolizumab/benralizumab (anti-IL-5/eosinophils).
11Which second messenger rises when a β₂-agonist binds its receptor?
cAMP (Gs-coupled GPCR).
12Why does escalating reliever (SABA) use signal poor control?
It treats only the symptom (spasm), not the inflammation — rising use means the disease is uncontrolled.
13Why are anticholinergics relatively more important in COPD than asthma?
COPD bronchomotor tone is dominantly cholinergic.
14What class is budesonide?
Inhaled corticosteroid (ICS).
15Formoterol differs from salmeterol how?
Formoterol is a LABA with a fast onset (can be used in some maintenance-and-reliever regimens with ICS).
4Hematopoietic AgentsModule 45 · 15 Q
1Microcytic anaemia — likely deficiency and treatment?
Iron deficiency; oral or IV iron.
2Macrocytic anaemia — two possible deficiencies?
Vitamin B₁₂ and/or folate.
3Single clinical feature distinguishing B₁₂ from folate deficiency?
Neurological signs occur with B₁₂ deficiency, not folate.
4Why is giving folate alone in B₁₂ deficiency dangerous?
It corrects the anaemia but lets neurological damage (subacute combined degeneration) progress.
5What causes anaemia in chronic kidney disease?
Reduced erythropoietin production by the kidney.
6Why does erythropoietin fail if iron stores are inadequate?
Red-cell production needs iron as raw material; EPO alone is insufficient.
7Name two erythropoiesis-stimulating agents.
Epoetin alfa, darbepoetin alfa.
8Major risks of ESAs?
Hypertension and thrombosis/stroke (especially if Hb over-corrected).
9What does G-CSF (filgrastim) do?
Stimulates neutrophil production — used for chemotherapy-induced neutropenia.
10Where is dietary iron mainly absorbed?
Duodenum.
11Common side effects of oral iron?
GI upset, constipation, black stools.
12What reduces oral iron absorption?
PPIs/antacids (raised gastric pH); food.
13Vitamin B₁₂ absorption requires what factor, and what disease destroys it?
Intrinsic factor; pernicious anaemia (give B₁₂ IM).
14Why is folate important periconceptually?
Prevents neural tube defects.
15Name a thrombopoietin-receptor agonist for ITP.
Romiplostim or eltrombopag.
5ImmunosuppressantsModule 39 · 15 Q
1Mechanism of calcineurin inhibitors?
↓ calcineurin → ↓ IL-2 transcription → ↓ T-cell activation.
2Name two calcineurin inhibitors.
Ciclosporin and tacrolimus.
3Signature toxicity of calcineurin inhibitors?
Nephrotoxicity (also ↑BP, tremor, hyperglycaemia).
4Why is a rising creatinine ambiguous in a transplant patient on tacrolimus?
It could be graft rejection OR calcineurin-inhibitor nephrotoxicity — hence drug monitoring.
5Mechanism of sirolimus?
mTOR inhibitor — blocks IL-2 signal transduction/proliferation.
6Mechanism of mycophenolate mofetil?
Inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase → ↓ purine (guanosine) synthesis in lymphocytes.
7Mechanism of azathioprine?
Pro-drug → 6-mercaptopurine → ↓ purine synthesis; antiproliferative.
8Which common gout drug dangerously potentiates azathioprine, and why?
Allopurinol — it inhibits xanthine oxidase which inactivates the metabolite → toxicity.
9Give the target of infliximab.
TNF-α.
10Give the target of rituximab.
CD20 on B cells.
11Why screen for latent TB before an anti-TNF biologic?
Anti-TNF therapy can reactivate latent tuberculosis (and hepatitis B).
12Calcineurin inhibitors are substrates of which enzyme?
CYP3A4 (interactions with azoles, macrolides, grapefruit).
13Broadest-acting immunosuppressant class?
Glucocorticoids.
14Universal trade-off of immunosuppression?
Increased risk of infection and malignancy.
15What does belatacept block?
T-cell co-stimulation (CTLA-4–Ig fusion blocking CD80/86).
6Histamine & AntagonistsModule 43 · 15 Q
1Which histamine receptor mediates allergic responses and is the antihistamine target?
H₁.
2Which histamine receptor controls gastric acid secretion?
H₂.
3Why are 1st-generation antihistamines sedating?
They are lipophilic and cross the blood-brain barrier.
4Why are 2nd-generation antihistamines non-sedating?
They are more polar / P-gp substrates and do not cross the BBB significantly.
5Name two 1st-generation antihistamines.
Diphenhydramine, chlorpheniramine (or promethazine).
6Name two 2nd-generation antihistamines.
Loratadine, cetirizine (or fexofenadine).
7Besides allergy, give two uses of 1st-gen antihistamines.
Motion sickness/vertigo, nausea, insomnia.
8Why do 1st-gen antihistamines cause dry mouth and urinary retention?
They also block muscarinic (antimuscarinic) receptors.
9Why are 1st-gen agents risky in the elderly?
Antimuscarinic and sedative effects → confusion, falls, retention, glaucoma.
10Name two H₂-receptor antagonists.
Famotidine, ranitidine (cimetidine).
11Which autacoid causes the ACE-inhibitor cough?
Bradykinin (accumulates when ACE is inhibited).
12What other ACE-inhibitor adverse effect is bradykinin-mediated?
Angio-oedema.
13Which generation is preferred for daytime allergy control?
2nd generation (non-sedating).
14What triggers histamine release from mast cells in Type I hypersensitivity?
Allergen cross-linking of IgE on mast cells.
15Are H₃/H₄ receptors major current drug targets?
No — largely investigational.
7Immunity & InflammationModule 38 · 15 Q
1Two arms of the immune system?
Innate (fast, non-specific, no memory) and adaptive (slow, specific, memory).
2Which arm forms immunological memory?
Adaptive.
3List the four hypersensitivity types (ACID).
I Anaphylactic/immediate, II Cytotoxic, III Immune-complex, IV Delayed.
4Which hypersensitivity type is IgE-mediated?
Type I.
5Classic example of Type I?
Anaphylaxis, atopy, allergic rhinitis/asthma.
6Which type is antibody-against-cell-surface (e.g. haemolytic anaemia)?
Type II.
7Which type is immune-complex mediated (e.g. SLE, serum sickness)?
Type III.
8Which type is T-cell mediated and delayed?
Type IV.
9Why does a TB skin test take ~48–72 h to read?
It is a Type IV (delayed, T-cell) reaction.
10Which three hypersensitivity types are antibody-mediated?
Types I, II, and III.
11What is a cytokine storm?
Excessive cytokine release causing systemic inflammation.
12Name two immune checkpoint molecules.
CTLA-4 and PD-1/PD-L1.
13What do checkpoint inhibitors do in cancer therapy?
Release the brakes on T cells so they attack tumour cells.
14Main toxicity class of checkpoint inhibitors?
Immune-related adverse events (autoimmune: colitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis, etc.).
15Key innate immune players?
Neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells, complement.
8Immune Globulins & VaccinesModule 40 · 15 Q
1Active vs passive immunisation — core difference?
Active = body makes its own antibodies (vaccine); passive = ready-made antibodies given.
2Which has faster onset?
Passive (immediate).
3Which lasts longer?
Active (durable memory).
4Why give BOTH vaccine and immune globulin after a rabies exposure?
Globulin protects immediately; the vaccine builds lasting immunity over weeks.
5Four broad vaccine types?
Live-attenuated, inactivated, subunit, mRNA (nucleic acid).
6Which vaccine type is contraindicated in pregnancy/immunocompromise?
Live-attenuated.
7Give two live-attenuated vaccines.
MMR, varicella (or BCG).
8Why is a live vaccine dangerous in immunocompromise?
The attenuated organism may cause disease.
9Classic passive-immunisation product in pregnancy?
Anti-D (Rho) immune globulin.
10What does anti-D prevent?
Rh sensitisation → haemolytic disease of the newborn.
11Why does passive immunity fade?
The transferred antibodies are gradually cleared; no memory is formed.
12Which immune arm does a vaccine rely on?
Adaptive immunity (memory).
13Why can't a transplant patient on tacrolimus get the MMR vaccine?
It is live-attenuated and they are immunosuppressed → risk of vaccine-strain disease.
14Can passive antibody interfere with a simultaneous live vaccine?
Yes — it can blunt the immune response to it.
15Example of post-exposure passive immunisation besides rabies?
Tetanus immune globulin, hepatitis B immune globulin.
