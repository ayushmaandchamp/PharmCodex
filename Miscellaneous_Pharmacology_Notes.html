Section 1 · Experimental Pharmacology & Screening Methods

▸★★★Radioimmunoassay (RIA) — principle, methodology, validation & applications; compare with modern immunoassays & mass spectrometry.

Principle

RIA (Yalow & Berson, Nobel 1977) is based on competitive binding: a fixed amount of radiolabelled antigen (e.g. ¹²⁵I) and the unlabelled antigen in the sample compete for a limited number of specific antibody binding sites. The more unlabelled (sample) antigen present, the less labelled antigen binds → bound radioactivity is inversely proportional to sample concentration.

Methodology

  1. Mix known labelled antigen + specific antibody + sample (unknown antigen).
  2. Allow competitive binding to equilibrium.
  3. Separate bound from free antigen (charcoal, second antibody, precipitation).
  4. Measure radioactivity of bound fraction (gamma counter).
  5. Read concentration from a standard curve.

Validation parameters

Sensitivity, specificity (cross-reactivity), precision (intra/inter-assay CV), accuracy (recovery), linearity, limit of detection.

Applications

Assay of hormones (insulin, thyroid, cortisol, hCG), drugs (digoxin, TDM), vitamins, tumour markers, peptides present in very low concentration.

RIA is now largely replaced by non-radioactive immunoassays (ELISA, CLIA) and LC-MS/MS for safety, automation & specificity.

▸★★★Actophotometer — principle, procedure, drug-class applications & limitations; note on recent advances (AI & home-cage monitoring).

Principle

The actophotometer measures spontaneous locomotor (motor) activity of rodents. The chamber has photoelectric cells with light beams; each time the animal crosses a beam, the circuit is interrupted and recorded as a count — quantifying CNS stimulation or depression.

Procedure

  1. Place animal in the apparatus; allow brief acclimatisation.
  2. Record baseline locomotor counts over a fixed time (e.g. 10 min).
  3. Administer test drug; re-record activity counts.
  4. Compare before/after (and vs control) → ↓ counts = CNS depressant; ↑ counts = CNS stimulant.

Drug-class applications

  • CNS depressants (sedatives/hypnotics — benzodiazepines, barbiturates; antipsychotics) → ↓ activity.
  • CNS stimulants (caffeine, amphetamine) → ↑ activity.
  • Screening of anxiolytics, neuroleptics, muscle relaxants.

Limitations

Only measures gross locomotion (not specific behaviour); affected by handling stress, habituation, circadian rhythm, environment; cannot distinguish sedation from motor impairment.

Recent advances: video-tracking software (ANY-maze, EthoVision) and AI/deep-learning pose-estimation (DeepLabCut) give detailed behavioural analysis; automated home-cage monitoring systems allow continuous, stress-free, 24-h activity assessment (refinement under 3R).
▸★★★Bioassay — principles, classification, methodology & applications; role in estimating relative potency & shift toward non-animal methods.

Definition & principle

Bioassay = estimation of the concentration or potency of a substance by measuring its biological response on living tissue/organism, compared against a standard. Principle: under controlled conditions, the magnitude of response is proportional to the dose/concentration.

Classification

  • Quantal (all-or-none — e.g. death, convulsion) vs graded (continuous response).
  • Direct (response measured directly, e.g. digitalis cardiac arrest) vs indirect (response compared with standard).
  • Methods of comparison: matching, interpolation, bracketing, three-point & four-point assay, multiple-point assay.

Methodology (example — four-point assay)

Two doses each of standard (S1,S2) & test (T1,T2) on the same tissue; construct parallel log dose-response lines; relative potency calculated from the horizontal distance between the lines.

Estimating relative potency

Relative potency = ratio of equi-effective doses of standard to test. Requires parallel log dose-response curves & the same maximal effect (same mechanism).

Applications

Standardisation of drugs lacking chemical assays (historically insulin, heparin, oxytocin, vaccines, antisera, digitalis, d-tubocurarine), hormone potency, antibiotic assay (microbiological).

Shift to non-animal methods

Per 3R, animal bioassays are replaced by physicochemical (HPLC, LC-MS), immunoassays (ELISA/RIA), and cell-based/in-vitro assays — more precise, reproducible & ethical.

▸★★★Define meta-analysis — steps, merits, demerits, role in evidence-based medicine; compare with systematic review.

Definition

Meta-analysis = a statistical technique that quantitatively combines the results of multiple independent studies addressing the same question to produce a single pooled estimate of effect.

Steps

  1. Define focused question (PICO) & protocol.
  2. Comprehensive literature search; apply inclusion/exclusion criteria.
  3. Assess study quality / risk of bias.
  4. Extract data; choose effect measure (OR, RR, mean difference).
  5. Pool using fixed- or random-effects model; assess heterogeneity (I², Q).
  6. Present forest plot; assess publication bias (funnel plot); sensitivity analysis.

Merits

  • ↑ statistical power & precision, resolves conflicting studies, higher generalisability, top of evidence hierarchy, can detect small effects.

Demerits

  • Publication bias, heterogeneity, "garbage in–garbage out" (poor primary studies), combining dissimilar studies (apples & oranges), reliance on published data.

Systematic review vs meta-analysis

Systematic reviewMeta-analysis
Comprehensive, structured qualitative synthesis of all relevant studiesStatistical pooling of quantitative data from those studies
May or may not include statisticsAlways quantitative; usually within a systematic review

Both sit at the top of the evidence-based-medicine pyramid, guiding guidelines & clinical decisions.

▸★★Importance of sample size in clinical trials & methods of calculation; note on confidence intervals & equivalence trials.

Importance of sample size

  • Too small → underpowered, may miss a true effect (Type II error), inconclusive, unethical (exposes subjects without yielding answers).
  • Too large → wastes resources, exposes excess subjects, may detect clinically trivial differences.
  • Adequate size ensures sufficient power & valid, generalisable conclusions.

Determinants & calculation

Sample size depends on: effect size (expected difference), variability (SD), significance level α (usually 0.05), power 1−β (usually 80–90%), study design & expected dropouts. Calculated using standard formulae/software (e.g. n ∝ σ²/effect²); larger for smaller effects & greater variance.

Confidence intervals (CI)

A range (e.g. 95% CI) within which the true population value is expected to lie. Narrow CI = greater precision. More informative than p-value alone — shows magnitude & direction of effect.

Equivalence trials

Aim to show two treatments differ by no more than a pre-defined equivalence margin (±Δ). Conclusion based on whether the CI of the difference lies entirely within ±Δ (not on p-value). Non-inferiority trials use a one-sided margin.

▸★★Type I & Type II errors (examples); interpretation of p-value; statistical vs clinical significance.

Type I & Type II errors

H₀ trueH₀ false
Reject H₀Type I (α) — false positiveCorrect (power)
Fail to reject H₀CorrectType II (β) — false negative
  • Type I (α): concluding a drug works when it doesn't (e.g. approving an ineffective drug); α = 0.05 conventionally.
  • Type II (β): missing a real effect (e.g. rejecting an effective drug); Power = 1−β (≥80%).

p-value interpretation

Probability of the observed (or more extreme) result if H₀ were true. p < 0.05 → statistically significant. It is not the probability H₀ is true, nor the effect size.

Statistical vs clinical significance

A result can be statistically significant but clinically irrelevant (e.g. large trial showing 1 mmHg BP drop, p<0.001) or clinically important but not statistically significant (small underpowered trial). Always interpret p-value with effect size, confidence interval & clinical context.

Section 3 · Clinical Trial Methodology & Design

▸★★★RCTs — design, usefulness & limitations; methods of randomization, blinding, allocation concealment & sampling techniques.

Randomized Controlled Trial

The gold standard for evaluating a new intervention: participants are randomly allocated to test vs control (placebo/standard) groups and followed prospectively for outcomes.

Usefulness

  • Minimises bias & confounding (known + unknown) via randomisation; allows causal inference; basis of drug approval & evidence-based medicine.

Limitations

  • Expensive, time-consuming; strict eligibility → limited generalisability; ethical constraints (placebo); not suitable for rare diseases/long-latency outcomes; Hawthorne effect.

Methods of randomization

MethodDescription
SimpleRandom number table/computer (like coin toss)
BlockEqual group sizes within blocks
StratifiedRandomise within strata (age, sex, severity) to balance prognostic factors
ClusterRandomise groups (clinics/villages) not individuals

Blinding (masking)

  • Single (subject), double (subject + investigator), triple (+ analyst/assessor) — reduces performance & assessment bias.

Allocation concealment

Keeping the upcoming assignment hidden from those enrolling participants (e.g. central randomisation, SNOSE — sequentially numbered opaque sealed envelopes) → prevents selection bias. (Different from blinding, which occurs after allocation.)

Sampling techniques

  • Probability: simple random, systematic, stratified, cluster, multistage. Non-probability: convenience, purposive, quota, snowball.
▸★★★Surrogate markers in clinical trials — attributes, merits, demerits, examples; note on biomarkers in drug evaluation.

Definition

A surrogate marker/endpoint is a measurable substitute for a clinically meaningful endpoint that predicts clinical benefit/harm (e.g. using LDL-C instead of MI/death).

Ideal attributes

  • Biologically plausible & on the causal pathway; strongly & consistently correlated with the true clinical outcome; changes with treatment in a way that reliably predicts the clinical effect; measurable, reproducible & validated.

Examples

SurrogateTrue clinical endpoint
Blood pressureStroke/MI
LDL-cholesterolCardiovascular events
HbA1cDiabetic complications
CD4 count / HIV viral loadAIDS progression/death
Tumour shrinkage / progression-free survivalOverall survival
Bone mineral densityFractures

Merits

  • Faster, smaller, cheaper trials; earlier results; useful when true endpoints are rare/delayed; enable accelerated approval.

Demerits

  • May not reliably predict clinical benefit → misleading (e.g. CAST — antiarrhythmics suppressed PVCs but ↑ mortality; ezetimibe early-LDL debate); miss off-target harms; require validation.

Biomarkers in drug evaluation

Objective indicators of biological/pathological processes or drug response — used as diagnostic, prognostic, predictive, pharmacodynamic & safety markers and companion diagnostics (HER2, EGFR, BRCA) to guide precision therapy.

▸★★Superiority, non-inferiority & equivalence trials; note on pragmatic, academic & real-world-data trials.

Trial objectives compared

TypeAimConclusion based on
SuperiorityShow new drug is better than comparator/placeboStatistically significant difference
Non-inferiorityShow new drug is not worse than standard by more than margin Δ (one-sided)CI upper bound < Δ
EquivalenceShow new drug is neither better nor worse (within ±Δ)CI lies entirely within ±Δ

Non-inferiority/equivalence used when a new drug offers other advantages (safety, cost, convenience) and a placebo arm is unethical.

Other designs

  • Pragmatic trials: conducted in real-world routine practice, broad eligibility, flexible → high external validity/generalisability.
  • Explanatory (vs pragmatic): ideal/controlled conditions → high internal validity.
  • Academic / investigator-initiated trials: non-commercial, address questions industry won't (comparisons, repurposing).
  • Real-world-data (RWD/RWE) trials: use EHRs, registries, claims, wearables → evidence in usual care.
▸★★Role of placebo in clinical trials — ethical considerations & mechanisms of the placebo effect.

Role of placebo

  • An inert substance/dummy used as control to distinguish the true pharmacological effect from the placebo response & natural course; enables blinding; establishes assay sensitivity.

Ethical considerations

  • Declaration of Helsinki: placebo acceptable only when no proven effective treatment exists, or for compelling methodological reasons without risk of serious/irreversible harm.
  • When effective therapy exists, use active comparator or add-on (placebo + standard care).
  • Requires informed consent disclosing possibility of placebo.

Mechanisms of placebo effect

  • Expectation/conditioning (Pavlovian), the therapeutic ritual & doctor-patient relationship.
  • Neurobiological: endogenous opioid & dopamine release (placebo analgesia reversed by naloxone), reduced anxiety.
  • Nocebo effect = negative expectation → adverse symptoms.
▸★Discuss decentralized (virtual) clinical trials and real-world evidence (RWE).

Decentralized (virtual) clinical trials (DCTs): trials in which some or all activities occur away from traditional sites - telemedicine visits, electronic consent, home nursing, wearable/remote monitoring and direct-to-patient drug delivery.

Advantages

  • Wider, more diverse recruitment; better retention and convenience; real-world relevance; accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic.

Challenges

  • Data integrity and standardisation, technology access/equity, privacy, regulatory acceptance.

Real-world data / evidence (RWD/RWE)

Data from routine care - EHRs, claims, registries, wearables - analysed to generate real-world evidence on effectiveness and safety; supports label expansion, post-marketing surveillance and regulatory decisions. Pragmatic trials bridge RCTs and real-world practice.

▸★Discuss the CONSORT statement and its importance in clinical-trial reporting.

CONSORT = Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials - an evidence-based 25-item checklist plus a flow diagram for transparent reporting of parallel-group randomized controlled trials.

Coverage

  • Title/abstract; introduction; methods (design, randomisation, allocation concealment, blinding, sample size); results (participant flow, baseline data, outcomes and estimation, harms); discussion and registration.
  • Flow diagram: Enrollment → Allocation → Follow-up → Analysis, with numbers and exclusions at each step.

Importance

Improves transparency and reproducibility, reduces selective reporting/bias, and aids critical appraisal. Related: PRISMA (systematic reviews), STROBE (observational), SPIRIT (protocols), plus CONSORT extensions.

Section 4 · Ethics in Clinical Research

▸★★★Evolution of ethical issues in clinical research & principles of the Declaration of Helsinki; composition, roles & responsibilities of IEC per ICMR & NDCT Rules 2019.

Evolution of research ethics

  • Nuremberg Code (1947) — after Nazi experiments; voluntary consent essential.
  • Declaration of Helsinki (1964, WMA, revised) — ethical principles for medical research.
  • Belmont Report (1979) — respect for persons, beneficence, justice (after Tuskegee syphilis study).
  • ICH-GCP (1996); ICMR guidelines (India, latest 2017); NDCT Rules 2019.

Key principles of the Declaration of Helsinki

  • Welfare of the subject over science/society; informed consent; independent ethics review; favourable risk-benefit; protection of vulnerable groups; scientific validity; competent investigators; confidentiality; registration & publication of results; post-trial access.

Four core ethical principles

Autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice.

IEC composition (ICMR/NDCT Rules 2019)

  • Minimum 7 members (quorum ~5): Chairperson (external), Member-Secretary; basic medical scientist; clinician(s); legal expert; social scientist/philosopher/ethicist; lay person from community.
  • Balance of gender; majority non-affiliated/independent; must be registered with CDSCO/DCGI.

Roles & responsibilities

  • Protect rights, safety & well-being of participants; review & approve protocol, consent documents, investigator qualifications, compensation; continuing review & monitoring; review SAEs & decide compensation; maintain records; can suspend/terminate non-compliant studies.
▸★★★Informed consent in clinical trials — ethical issues in vulnerable populations; note on compensation for trial-related injury/death.

Informed consent

A voluntary process by which a competent participant, after understanding all relevant information, agrees to take part. Essential elements:

  • Purpose, procedures, duration; risks & benefits; alternatives; confidentiality.
  • Voluntary participation & right to withdraw anytime without penalty; compensation provisions; contact details.
  • Documented on a signed/thumb-impression Informed Consent Form (ICF) in a language the subject understands; audio-video recording required for vulnerable subjects (NDCT Rules).

Vulnerable populations & issues

  • Groups: children, pregnant women, mentally ill, prisoners, illiterate/poor, terminally ill, students/employees (subordinates).
  • Risk of coercion, undue inducement, therapeutic misconception, impaired capacity.
  • Safeguards: legally acceptable representative/assent (children), impartial witness for illiterate, extra IEC scrutiny, justify their inclusion.

Compensation (NDCT Rules 2019, India)

  • Mandatory free medical management for trial-related injury (as long as required).
  • Financial compensation for trial-related injury/disability/death — sponsor liable; formula prescribed by CDSCO; decided by IEC & DCGI.
  • Compensation even if injury is due to placebo (where applicable), failure of investigational product, or protocol-related procedures.
▸★★GCP guidelines — principles & advantages; note on protocol violation vs protocol deviation.

Good Clinical Practice (GCP)

An international (ICH-E6) ethical & scientific quality standard for designing, conducting, recording & reporting trials involving human subjects. India has its own GCP guidelines (CDSCO/ICMR).

Key principles

  • Trials conducted per Declaration of Helsinki & GCP; benefits justify risks.
  • Rights, safety & well-being of subjects prevail over science.
  • Adequate preclinical/clinical information; sound scientific protocol with IEC approval.
  • Qualified investigators; freely given informed consent; accurate recording (ALCOA — Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate); confidentiality; investigational products made per GMP; quality systems.

Advantages

  • Protects subjects, ensures data credibility & integrity, global acceptance/harmonisation of data, regulatory compliance, public trust.

▸★★Discuss publication ethics - plagiarism, research misconduct and authorship.

Publication ethics governs integrity in research reporting; breaches constitute research misconduct.

Research misconduct - "FFP"

  • Fabrication - making up data.
  • Falsification - manipulating/altering data or images.
  • Plagiarism - using others' work or ideas without attribution (includes self-plagiarism and mosaic plagiarism; detected by iThenticate/Turnitin).

Authorship (ICMJE criteria)

All four: substantial contribution; drafting/revising; final approval; accountability. Ghost, gift and guest authorship are unethical.

Other issues

  • Duplicate/redundant publication, salami slicing, image manipulation, undisclosed conflict of interest, predatory journals.
  • Oversight: COPE guidelines, ICMR ethical guidelines, institutional research-integrity committees; consequences include correction/retraction.

Section 5 · Regulatory Affairs

▸★★★Salient features of New Drugs & Clinical Trial (NDCT) Rules 2019; orphan drugs, expedited approval; provisions under Schedule Y & Schedule H.

NDCT Rules 2019 — salient features

  • Notified under the Drugs & Cosmetics Act; consolidated framework for new drugs & clinical trials in India (largely superseding Schedule Y).
  • Defined timelines: trial approval within ~90 days; deemed approval in 30 days for applications using Indian data if no query.
  • Mandatory registration of Ethics Committees; strengthened informed consent & compensation for trial injury/death.
  • Provisions for orphan drugs, expedited/accelerated approval, waiver of local trials in special situations, academic/investigator trials with lighter requirements.
  • Predictable, transparent, ethical conduct; pre- & post-submission meetings with CDSCO.

Orphan drugs

Drugs for rare diseases (defined under National Policy for Rare Diseases). NDCT Rules give incentives: fee waivers, possible local-trial waivers, expedited review.

Expedited drug approval

For drugs treating serious/life-threatening or rare diseases or with unmet need / major therapeutic advance — may allow approval on Phase II data with Phase IV commitment, accelerated/conditional approval.

Schedule Y & Schedule H

  • Schedule Y: earlier requirements & guidelines for clinical trials, import/manufacture of new drugs (data, phases, responsibilities) — now largely incorporated into NDCT Rules 2019.
  • Schedule H / H1: list of prescription-only drugs; H1 covers specified antibiotics, habit-forming & certain drugs requiring a separate register/retention of prescription to curb misuse & AMR.
▸★★Essential Medicines concept & NLEM; note on pharmacoeconomics & cost-effectiveness analysis.

Essential medicines concept (WHO)

Essential medicines satisfy the priority healthcare needs of the population, selected by disease prevalence, evidence of efficacy & safety, and comparative cost-effectiveness; should be available at all times, in adequate amounts, appropriate dosage forms, assured quality & affordable price.

NLEM (India)

  • National List of Essential Medicines — periodically revised (latest 2022, ~384 medicines).
  • Guides procurement, supply, prescribing & rational use; linked to price control via DPCO/NPPA (medicines in NLEM come under the Schedule-I ceiling-price regulation).
  • Criteria for inclusion: relevance to public health, proven efficacy/safety, cost-effectiveness, single drug preferred.

▸★Discuss off-label use of drugs - concept, examples, and ethical/regulatory aspects.

Off-label use = prescribing an approved drug for an unapproved indication, age group, dose or route.

Where common

Paediatrics, oncology, psychiatry, pregnancy and rare diseases (few approved options).

Examples

  • Propranolol for infantile haemangioma; amitriptyline/gabapentinoids for neuropathic pain; spironolactone for acne/hirsutism; low-dose aspirin in pregnancy.

Ethical and regulatory aspects

  • Generally legal when based on sound clinical judgement, but needs reasonable evidence, informed consent and documentation.
  • Manufacturers may not promote off-label uses; the prescriber bears greater liability; adverse events should be reported (pharmacovigilance).

Section 6 · Special & Emerging Topics

▸★★★Gene therapy — principles & applications; approved gene therapies, challenges & recent advances (incl. miRNA & siRNA).

Principle

Introduction, removal, or modification of genetic material in a patient's cells to treat/prevent disease — by replacing a faulty gene, silencing a harmful one, or editing the genome.

Approaches

  • Gene augmentation (add functional copy — recessive disorders).
  • Gene silencing (siRNA, antisense, miRNA — dominant/gain-of-function).
  • Gene editing (CRISPR-Cas9, base/prime editing).
  • Vectors: viral (AAV, lentivirus, adenovirus) & non-viral (lipid nanoparticles); ex-vivo vs in-vivo delivery.

RNA-based therapeutics

  • siRNA (small interfering RNA): degrades target mRNA via RISC → e.g. patisiran (hATTR amyloidosis), inclisiran (↓LDL), givosiran.
  • miRNA (microRNA): endogenous regulators; miRNA mimics/antagomirs under development.
  • Antisense oligonucleotides: nusinersen (SMA), mipomersen.

High-throughput screening (HTS)

Robotic, miniaturised (384/1536-well) assay of huge compound libraries against a target to rapidly identify "hits"; combined with high-content imaging & AI triage.

Reverse pharmacology

"Bedside-to-bench": start from a clinical/traditional observation of efficacy, then identify the mechanism/target — efficient, strong in Indian/Ayurvedic research (e.g. artemisinin, reserpine).

Translational pharmacology

"Bench-to-bedside (& back)": converting basic research findings into clinical applications using biomarkers, PK/PD modelling, and back-translation of clinical observations — bridges discovery and patient care.

Photopharmacology (note)

Use of light to control drug activity — drugs incorporating photoswitches (e.g. azobenzene) that change conformation & activity on illumination → spatial & temporal control of drug action (e.g. light-activated local effect, reducing systemic toxicity). Largely experimental (photoswitchable antibiotics, channel blockers).

▸★★Teratogenicity testing methods & perinatal pharmacology; note on drugs safe vs contraindicated in pregnancy & lactation.

Teratogenicity testing (reproductive toxicity — ICH segments)

  • Segment I: fertility & early embryonic development (pre-mating to implantation).
  • Segment II: embryo-foetal development / teratogenicity (organogenesis) — main test, in 2 species (rat + rabbit).
  • Segment III: peri- & post-natal development (late gestation to lactation/weaning).
  • Alternatives: whole-embryo culture, zebrafish, embryonic stem-cell test, in-silico.

Perinatal pharmacology

Drug handling differs across pregnancy (↑ volume, ↑ GFR, altered metabolism, placental transfer), foetus (immature enzymes/BBB), neonate (immature renal/hepatic function — e.g. chloramphenicol "grey baby") & lactation (drug excretion in milk).

Drugs in lactation

Avoid: cytotoxics, lithium, amiodarone, chloramphenicol, radioactive iodine, ergotamine, high-dose CNS drugs. Prefer drugs with low milk transfer; time dosing after feeds.

Section 7 · Other Important Miscellaneous Topics

▸★★Antioxidants & their role in health and disease; note on prokinetics, potassium-channel openers & chloride-channel activators.

Antioxidants

  • Neutralise reactive oxygen species (ROS)/free radicals implicated in ageing, atherosclerosis, cancer, neurodegeneration, ischaemia-reperfusion injury.
  • Endogenous: superoxide dismutase, catalase, glutathione peroxidase. Exogenous/dietary: vitamins C & E, β-carotene, selenium, flavonoids, N-acetylcysteine, alpha-lipoic acid, coenzyme Q10.
  • Clinical use: NAC (paracetamol poisoning, mucolytic), vitamin E (some uses), antioxidants in ophthalmology (AREDS for AMD). Note: high-dose antioxidant supplements have not consistently shown benefit & may even harm.

Prokinetics

Enhance GI motility: metoclopramide & domperidone (D2 antagonists), 5-HT4 agonists (mosapride, prucalopride, cisapride-restricted), erythromycin (motilin agonist). Uses: GERD, gastroparesis, functional dyspepsia, constipation. ADR: extrapyramidal (metoclopramide), QT prolongation.

Potassium-channel openers

Open K-ATP channels → hyperpolarisation → smooth-muscle relaxation: minoxidil (resistant HTN, alopecia), nicorandil (angina — also NO donor), diazoxide (hypertensive emergency, hypoglycaemia/insulinoma). Pancreatic K-ATP is the sulfonylurea target.

Chloride-channel activators

Lubiprostone activates intestinal ClC-2 chloride channels → ↑ fluid secretion → used in chronic constipation & IBS-C; ivacaftor (CFTR potentiator) for cystic fibrosis.

▸★★Pharmacoepidemiology; Materiovigilance (MvPI), Haemovigilance & Cosmetovigilance.

Pharmacoepidemiology

The study of the use and effects (beneficial & adverse) of drugs in large populations. Combines pharmacology + epidemiology. Methods: cohort, case-control, cross-sectional studies, drug-utilisation reviews, database/record-linkage studies. Uses: post-marketing surveillance, ADR quantification, risk-benefit & outcomes research, policy.

▸★★Intellectual Property Rights (IPR), patents & issues in drug development; note on chiral drugs & their significance.

IPR & patents

  • Patent: exclusive right for an invention (20 years from filing) — incentivises R&D investment.
  • TRIPS & Indian Patents Act 1970 (amended 2005) — product patents; Section 3(d) prevents "evergreening" (Novartis-Glivec case).
  • Compulsory licensing for public health (Natco-Bayer sorafenib); data exclusivity; Bayh-Dole Act (US — academic patents).
  • Issues: high drug prices vs access, evergreening, biopiracy (Traditional Knowledge Digital Library), patent cliffs & generics/biosimilars.

Chiral drugs & their significance

  • Chirality: molecules existing as non-superimposable mirror-image enantiomers (R/S); often one is active (eutomer), the other inactive or harmful (distomer).
  • Examples: S-thalidomide teratogenic vs R-sedative; levodopa (only L active); esomeprazole (S-omeprazole); levosalbutamol; dexketoprofen; escitalopram.
  • "Chiral switch": marketing a single active enantiomer of an existing racemate → better efficacy/safety, fewer side effects, and patent extension.
  • Regulators require characterisation of each enantiomer's pharmacology.
▸★★Medication errors, medication adherence/patient compliance & strategies to improve them.

Medication errors

  • Definition: any preventable event that may cause inappropriate medication use or patient harm while the drug is in the control of the professional/patient.
  • Stages: prescribing, transcribing, dispensing, administration, monitoring.
  • Causes: illegible/verbal orders, LASA (look-alike sound-alike) drugs, abbreviations, calculation errors, workload/fatigue, poor communication.
  • Prevention: CPOE + clinical decision support, e-prescribing, barcode administration, Tall-Man lettering, "five rights," medication reconciliation, pharmacist review, non-punitive reporting & root-cause analysis (WHO "Medication Without Harm").

Medication adherence / compliance

  • Adherence: extent to which patient's behaviour matches agreed recommendations (preferred term — implies patient agreement); compliance implies passive following.
  • Causes of non-adherence: complex regimens, polypharmacy, side effects, cost, poor understanding, asymptomatic disease, forgetfulness, poor doctor-patient relationship.

Strategies to improve adherence

  • Simplify regimen (once-daily, fixed-dose combinations, long-acting formulations).
  • Patient education & counselling; shared decision-making.
  • Reminders (apps, alarms, pill organisers, blister/calendar packs), directly observed therapy (DOTS in TB).
  • Manage side effects & cost; involve family/pharmacist; follow-up & monitoring.

🩺 Clinical Case Vignettes

Apply the concepts — read the scenario, decide your answer, then open to check. Filter to just these with the 🩺 Cases chip.

▸🩺 CaseYou are designing a trial of a new antihypertensive. Which features minimise bias?
Clue: Randomise, conceal, blind.

Randomisation (balances confounders), allocation concealment (hides the upcoming assignment → reduces selection bias), blinding (subject/investigator/assessor → reduces performance and detection bias), adequate sample size/power, intention-to-treat analysis, and an appropriate control.

▸🩺 CaseA very large trial shows a new drug lowers BP by 1 mmHg, p<0.001. Is it worth using?
Clue: Statistical versus clinical significance.

Statistically significant but clinically trivial. A small p only means the result is unlikely by chance; with large samples, tiny effects reach significance. Judge by effect size, confidence interval and clinical relevance, not the p-value alone.

▸🩺 CaseAn illiterate, seriously ill patient is being enrolled in a trial. Which consent safeguards apply?
Clue: Vulnerability.

Consent in an understandable language with an impartial witness and thumb impression; audio-video recording of consent (NDCT Rules for vulnerable subjects); ensure voluntariness (no coercion/undue inducement), capacity and the right to withdraw; extra ethics-committee scrutiny.

▸🩺 CaseAntiarrhythmics suppressed PVCs (a surrogate marker) after myocardial infarction but increased deaths in a trial. What does this teach?
Clue: The CAST trial.

The CAST trial — a surrogate endpoint (PVC suppression) failed to predict clinical benefit and masked harm (increased mortality). Surrogates must be validated and confirmed against hard clinical outcomes.

▸🩺 CaseA woman with epilepsy on valproate is planning pregnancy. Advice?
Clue: Teratogenicity.

Valproate is highly teratogenic (neural-tube defects, neurodevelopmental harm) — switch pre-conception to a safer agent (e.g. lamotrigine/levetiracetam) and give high-dose folic acid. Other key teratogens: warfarin, ACEi/ARB, isotretinoin, methotrexate, tetracyclines.

▸🩺 CaseWhy was esomeprazole marketed when omeprazole already existed, and what is the underlying concept?
Clue: Enantiomers.

Chirality — omeprazole is a racemate; esomeprazole is the purified S-enantiomer (eutomer) with more predictable metabolism and efficacy (a 'chiral switch', which also extends patent life). Enantiomers can differ in activity/toxicity (e.g. S- versus R-thalidomide; levosalbutamol).

▸🩺 CaseA small trial finds no significant difference between two drugs. Can we conclude they are equivalent?
Clue: Power.

No — a small/underpowered study risks a Type II error (false negative); 'no significant difference' is not 'no difference'. Equivalence needs a pre-defined margin and adequate sample size. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
