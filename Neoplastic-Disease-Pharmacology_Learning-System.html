General Principles in the Pharmacotherapy of Cancer

Log-Kill Hypothesis · Cell Cycle Kinetics · Resistance Mechanisms
High Yield
01 The 80/20 Map
Chemotherapy follows first-order kinetics (the log-kill hypothesis): a given dose kills a constant fraction (e.g., 99.9%) of tumor cells, not a constant number. Cure requires repeated cycles.

Cell-Cycle Specificity

  • S-phase specific: Antimetabolites (Methotrexate, 5-FU, 6-MP, Cytarabine) — block DNA/RNA synthesis.
  • M-phase specific: Microtubule inhibitors (Vincristine, Paclitaxel) — arrest mitotic spindle.
  • Cell-cycle non-specific (CCNS): Alkylating agents (Cyclophosphamide), Platinum analogs (Cisplatin), Anthracyclines (Doxorubicin) — damage DNA at any phase.

Combination Chemotherapy Rationale

Uses drugs with different mechanisms and non-overlapping toxicities to maximize log-kill and overcome resistance. Classic example: CHOP regimen (Cyclophosphamide, Hydroxydaunorubicin [Doxorubicin], Oncovin [Vincristine], Prednisone).

02 The Blurting Gate
Before revealing, jot down what the Log-Kill Hypothesis means and list 3 mechanisms of drug resistance.
  • Why does a 1-gram tumor (109 cells) take multiple cycles of chemo to eradicate?
  • Name the protein encoded by MDR1 and explain how it causes resistance.
  • What is the rationale for combining drugs with non-overlapping toxicities?
Reveal Answers

Log-Kill Hypothesis: Chemotherapy kills a constant fraction of cells per cycle. A 3-log kill reduces 109 cells to 106 (99.9% killed). Repeated cycles are needed to push the tumor burden below the level where the immune system can eliminate the remainder. Between cycles, normal rapidly dividing tissues (marrow, GI mucosa) recover.

P-glycoprotein (MDR1): An ATP-dependent efflux pump embedded in the cancer cell membrane. It pumps out hydrophobic drugs (anthracyclines, vinca alkaloids, taxanes) before they reach intracellular targets, rendering multiple drug classes simultaneously ineffective.

Combination rationale: Different mechanisms maximize cell kill across heterogeneous tumor populations. Non-overlapping toxicities (e.g., Vincristine → neuropathy vs. Cyclophosphamide → cystitis) mean each drug can be given at full dose without compounding the same organ injury.

03 Feynman Analogy
🧪 The Log-Kill Analogy

Imagine you have a jar of 1,000 marbles representing tumor cells. Each round of chemo removes 90% of whatever is left (a 1-log kill). After round 1: 100 remain. After round 2: 10 remain. After round 3: 1 remains. You can never remove a fixed number — each round only takes a fraction. That's why you need multiple cycles, and why stopping early allows the remaining marbles to multiply back.

🚨 P-glycoprotein Analogy

Think of P-glycoprotein as a bouncer at a nightclub. Even if drugs manage to get inside the cancer cell, the bouncer (P-gp) physically grabs them and throws them back out. The cancer cell upregulates more bouncers at every door, making it nearly impossible for multiple drug classes to stay inside long enough to do damage.

02 The Blurting Gate
Without looking, name 5 cytotoxic drug classes and a unique toxicity for each.
  • What is the dose-limiting toxicity of Cisplatin vs. Carboplatin?
  • How do Vinca alkaloids differ from Taxanes at the molecular level?
  • Why must you reduce 6-MP dose when prescribing Allopurinol?

Reveal Answers

Cisplatin vs. Carboplatin: Cisplatin → nephrotoxicity (dose-limiting), ototoxicity, severe nausea. Carboplatin → myelosuppression (thrombocytopenia is dose-limiting), much less nephrotoxic.

Vinca vs. Taxanes: Vinca alkaloids prevent assembly of microtubules (hide the bricks). Taxanes prevent disassembly (super-glue the scaffold). Both freeze the mitotic spindle, arresting cells in M-phase.

6-MP + Allopurinol: 6-MP is metabolized by xanthine oxidase. Allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase, causing 6-MP to accumulate to toxic levels. Reduce 6-MP dose by 75%.

Doxorubicin cardiomyopathy: Cumulative, dose-dependent, irreversible dilated cardiomyopathy caused by iron-mediated free radical damage. Prevented by Dexrazoxane (iron chelator that prevents free radical formation in cardiac tissue).

03 Feynman Analogy
🧱 Microtubules: Scaffolding Analogy

Imagine a construction site building a skyscraper (the dividing cell). The scaffolding (microtubules) must go up to position workers (chromosomes), then come down once they're placed.

Vinca alkaloids lock up the warehouse where the scaffolding poles are stored — the crew can't build the scaffolding, so the project freezes.

Taxanes weld the scaffolding together permanently — it can't be taken down, so the crew can never move to the next building. Both outcomes: project canceled (apoptosis).

💣 Doxorubicin: The Time Bomb

Doxorubicin is like hiring a demolition expert who also accidentally drops a time bomb in the control room (heart). Each dose adds another bomb. Once enough bombs accumulate, the control room collapses (cardiomyopathy). Dexrazoxane is the bomb squad that defuses each bomb (chelates iron) before it can detonate.

02 The Blurting Gate
Without looking, write down 5 targeted drugs, their targets, and indications.
  • What is the Philadelphia chromosome and which drug targets it?
  • Why does the acneiform rash from EGFR inhibitors paradoxically indicate efficacy?
  • Why are BRAF inhibitors combined with MEK inhibitors?
Reveal Answers

Philadelphia chromosome: A translocation t(9;22) that creates the BCR-ABL fusion oncoprotein, a constitutively active tyrosine kinase. Imatinib competitively binds the ATP pocket of BCR-ABL, shutting it down.

EGFR rash = efficacy: EGFR is expressed in normal skin. Blocking it in skin causes acneiform rash. The more the skin reacts, the more the drug is reaching its target systemically. Patients with a rash tend to have better outcomes.

BRAF + MEK: BRAF inhibitors alone can paradoxically activate MAPK signaling through wild-type RAF, causing squamous cell carcinomas. Adding a MEK inhibitor (Trametinib) blocks downstream MAPK, preventing this paradoxical activation and improving efficacy.

03 Feynman Analogy
🔌 The "Wrong Key" Analogy

The BCR-ABL kinase is like a car ignition that's stuck in the "ON" position — the engine (cell proliferation) runs nonstop. Imatinib is a perfectly shaped key blank that fits the ignition slot but can't turn it — it blocks ATP (the real key) from inserting, so the engine finally shuts off. The T315I mutation is like changing the lock — the blank no longer fits, and you need a new generation of key (Dasatinib or Ponatinib).

Antibodies, CAR T Cells, and Proteins

Checkpoint Inhibitors · Monoclonal Antibodies · ADCs · CAR-T Engineering
High Yield
01 The 80/20 Map
Immunotherapy shifts the paradigm from directly poisoning the tumor to unmasking it so the patient's own immune system can destroy it. This chapter covers immune checkpoints, therapeutic antibodies, antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), and CAR-T cells.

Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors

Tumors express ligands (PD-L1) that bind to PD-1 on T-cells, sending an "off" signal. Blocking this interaction "takes the brakes off" the immune system.

TargetDrug(s)MechanismKey Toxicity
PD-1Pembrolizumab, NivolumabBlock PD-1 on T-cells, preventing PD-L1-mediated suppression.Immune-related adverse events (irAEs): colitis, pneumonitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis, type 1 DM, hypophysitis.

⚠ irAEs: Checkpoint inhibitor toxicities are autoimmune in nature because you've removed the brakes on the immune system. They can affect any organ. Managed with corticosteroids and sometimes drug discontinuation.

Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs)

ADCs combine the specificity of an antibody with the killing power of a cytotoxic drug. The antibody delivers the toxin directly to the cancer cell surface, is internalized, and releases the cytotoxic payload inside.

  • Ado-trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1): Trastuzumab linked to emtansine (microtubule inhibitor). For HER2+ breast cancer.
  • Brentuximab vedotin: Anti-CD30 linked to MMAE (microtubule inhibitor). For Hodgkin lymphoma.

CAR-T Cell Therapy

Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cells are engineered from the patient's own T-cells:

  • Step 1: Harvest T-cells from the patient (leukapheresis).
  • Step 2: Genetically modify them to express a Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) targeting a tumor antigen (e.g., CD19 for B-cell lymphomas).
  • Step 3: Expand the modified cells ex vivo.
  • Step 4: Infuse them back into the patient after lymphodepleting chemotherapy.

Key drugs: Tisagenlecleucel (Kymriah), Axicabtagene ciloleucel (Yescarta). FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory B-cell ALL and large B-cell lymphoma.

⚠ Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS): CAR-T cells massively activate, releasing IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ. This causes high fever, hypotension, capillary leak, and multi-organ failure. Treated with Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6 receptor antibody). Neurotoxicity (ICANS) is another severe complication.
02 The Blurting Gate
Before opening, recall the difference between PD-1 and CTLA-4 blockade, and the 4 steps of CAR-T therapy.
  • What are irAEs and why do they occur?
  • Name the antibody used to treat Cytokine Release Syndrome and its target.
  • Why must you never combine Trastuzumab with Doxorubicin?
Reveal Answers

PD-1 vs CTLA-4: PD-1 inhibitors (Pembrolizumab) work in the tumor microenvironment — they prevent the tumor from exhausting T-cells. CTLA-4 inhibitors (Ipilimumab) work in the lymph node — they enhance T-cell priming and activation at an earlier stage.

CRS Treatment: Tocilizumab, an anti-IL-6 receptor antibody, blocks the massive IL-6-driven inflammatory cascade. Corticosteroids are used if Tocilizumab is insufficient.

Trastuzumab + Doxorubicin: Both are cardiotoxic. Doxorubicin causes irreversible cardiomyopathy via free radicals; Trastuzumab causes reversible cardiac dysfunction by blocking HER2 signaling in cardiomyocytes. Together, the additive cardiotoxicity can be fatal.

03 Feynman Analogy
🛡️ Checkpoint Blockade: The "Invisibility Cloak"

Tumors wear an invisibility cloak (PD-L1) that makes them invisible to the immune system's soldiers (T-cells). Checkpoint inhibitors rip off the cloak, exposing the tumor so the soldiers can attack. The downside? Without the cloak to calm things down, sometimes the soldiers attack the body's own buildings (autoimmune irAEs).

🧬 CAR-T: "Custom-Built Soldiers"

Imagine you're losing a war because your army can't identify the enemy. So you capture some of your own soldiers (T-cells), take them to a lab, and give them special goggles (CAR receptor) that can see a specific enemy uniform (CD19). Then you clone millions of these upgraded soldiers and send them back to the battlefield. The problem: they get so excited they cause collateral damage (CRS) — you need a fire brigade (Tocilizumab) on standby.

Hormone-dependent cancers (breast, prostate) require endogenous hormones to grow. We starve them by blocking the receptor, halting hormone synthesis, or eliminating the hormone source.

02 The Blurting Gate
Before looking, contrast Tamoxifen vs. Aromatase Inhibitors and explain the Leuprolide flare.
  • Why does Tamoxifen increase endometrial cancer risk?
  • Why are AIs only effective in postmenopausal women?
  • Why must you give Flutamide with Leuprolide at the start?
  • What makes Enzalutamide different from older antiandrogens?
Reveal Answers

Tamoxifen & Endometrial Cancer: Tamoxifen is a SERM: antagonist in breast but agonist in the uterus. Its estrogenic activity on the endometrium stimulates proliferation, increasing the risk of endometrial cancer.

AIs in postmenopausal only: In postmenopausal women, the sole source of estrogen is peripheral aromatase conversion (adipose tissue). AIs effectively eliminate this. In premenopausal women, the ovaries produce estrogen via a pathway that AIs alone cannot suppress.

Leuprolide flare: GnRH agonists initially stimulate LH/FSH release (the normal physiological response) before receptor downregulation occurs. This causes a transient testosterone surge that can worsen prostate cancer symptoms (bone pain, urinary obstruction). Flutamide blocks the androgen receptor during this dangerous window.

Enzalutamide: A next-generation antiandrogen with 5–8x greater affinity for AR than bicalutamide. It also blocks AR nuclear translocation and DNA binding, making it effective in castration-resistant prostate cancer where older antiandrogens fail.

03 Feynman Analogy
🌻 Tamoxifen: The "Two-Faced Key"

Tamoxifen is like a master key that works differently in different locks. In the breast lock, it turns backwards (antagonist) — blocking estrogen from activating growth. But in the uterine lock, it turns forward (agonist) — accidentally stimulating endometrial growth. Same key, different lock shapes, opposite effects.

💥 Leuprolide Flare: "Overloading the Circuit"

Think of the GnRH receptor as a dimmer switch. If you gently turn it, the lights (testosterone) get brighter (initial flare). But if you hold it at maximum for weeks, the circuit breaker trips (receptor downregulation) and the lights go completely dark (castrate-level testosterone). Flutamide is the lampshade you put over the bulb during the initial bright flash so the room (patient) doesn't get burned.

🃏 Clinical Vignette Flashcards

Tap a card to reveal the answer, mechanism, clinical pearl, and exam trap. Twenty high-yield scenarios spanning neoplastic disease pharmacology.

01 A patient with CML has a BCR-ABL fusion gene and a white cell count of 280 ×10⁹/L. Imatinib is started. Explain the mechanism and why resistance with T315I mutation renders imatinib ineffective.
Imatinib is a tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) that occupies the ATP-binding pocket of BCR-ABL kinase, locking it in the inactive conformation → prevents phosphorylation of downstream substrates (RAS, PI3K, STAT5) → blocks proliferation and promotes apoptosis in CML cells. T315I mutation (threonine → isoleucine at residue 315, the 'gatekeeper' residue): isoleucine adds a bulky side chain that clashes sterically with imatinib and also removes a critical hydrogen bond between imatinib and the kinase → imatinib cannot bind → resistance. T315I also confers resistance to dasatinib and nilotinib. Ponatinib (3rd-generation TKI) was specifically designed to accommodate the T315I mutation and is the treatment of choice.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Imatinib also inhibits c-Kit and PDGFR — hence its use in GIST (gastrointestinal stromal tumour, KIT-driven) and dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans (PDGFR-driven). Asciminib is a novel 'STAMP' inhibitor targeting the myristoyl pocket of ABL (not the ATP pocket) — active against T315I.
⚠️ Exam Trap: CML has three phases: chronic → accelerated → blast crisis. Imatinib in chronic phase achieves major molecular response in ~80%. Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)) is present in ~95% of CML AND ~30% of ALL (Ph+ ALL). Ph+ ALL requires TKI + chemotherapy ± stem cell transplant — different management from CML.
02 A 55-year-old woman with HER2-positive breast cancer is treated with trastuzumab. After 6 months she develops reduced LVEF and heart failure. Explain the mechanism of cardiotoxicity and how it differs from anthracycline cardiotoxicity.
Trastuzumab (anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody) blocks HER2 signalling. HER2 (ErbB2) is essential for cardiomyocyte survival and stress response — it activates PI3K/Akt survival pathways in response to mechanical stress. Trastuzumab-induced HER2 blockade → cardiomyocytes cannot mount adaptive responses to stress → contractile dysfunction. Key distinction from anthracyclines: Trastuzumab cardiotoxicity is type II — NOT cumulative dose-dependent, NOT associated with myocyte necrosis or ultrastructural damage, largely reversible with cessation and cardiac therapy (usually recovers in 2–4 months). Anthracycline cardiotoxicity is type I — cumulative dose-dependent (risk rises sharply above 450 mg/m² doxorubicin), involves ROS-mediated myocyte necrosis, irreversible fibrosis.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Monitor LVEF before trastuzumab, every 3 months during therapy. Hold trastuzumab if LVEF falls ≥10 percentage points or falls below 50%. Restart after recovery. Concurrent use of anthracyclines + trastuzumab (synchronous) is generally avoided — sequential administration is standard.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Pertuzumab (anti-HER2, different epitope from trastuzumab) + trastuzumab is dual HER2 blockade — standard in HER2+ metastatic breast cancer and neoadjuvant HER2+ early breast cancer. Ado-trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1) is an antibody-drug conjugate delivering DM1 (microtubule inhibitor) specifically to HER2+ cells — active in trastuzumab-resistant disease.
03 Cisplatin is given for testicular cancer. The patient develops tinnitus and bilateral high-frequency hearing loss after the third cycle. What is the mechanism, and what renal toxicity requires specific prevention?
Ototoxicity: cisplatin accumulates in cochlear outer hair cells and stria vascularis → forms platinum-DNA adducts + generates ROS → irreversible outer hair cell death → permanent high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss (tinnitus is often the first symptom). Nephrotoxicity: cisplatin is filtered and concentrated in proximal tubular cells → platinum-DNA adducts + oxidative stress → proximal tubule necrosis → AKI (most common dose-limiting toxicity). Prevention: aggressive IV hydration (1–2 L 0.9% NaCl before and after each dose), ensure urine output >100 mL/hr, mannitol diuresis, avoid concurrent NSAIDs/aminoglycosides. Amifostine (free-radical scavenger) can reduce nephrotoxicity and neuropathy — used selectively.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Cisplatin causes a characteristic hypomagnesaemia (renal magnesium wasting — tubular damage → impaired Mg²⁺ reabsorption) and hypokalaemia — may persist for months after treatment ends. Check Mg²⁺ and replace aggressively. Cisplatin-induced hypomagnesaemia can itself cause hypocalcaemia (Mg²⁺ required for PTH secretion).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Carboplatin is a platinum analogue with significantly lower nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity — it is dosed by AUC using the Calvert formula (dose = AUC × [GFR + 25]). Carboplatin's dose-limiting toxicity is myelosuppression (thrombocytopenia > neutropenia), not nephrotoxicity. Oxaliplatin causes distinctive cold-triggered peripheral neuropathy and is used in colorectal cancer.
04 A patient with BRAF V600E-mutant metastatic melanoma is started on vemurafenib (BRAF inhibitor). Three months later, a new squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) appears on her arm. Explain this paradoxical toxicity.
Paradoxical cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (and keratoacanthoma) is a class effect of BRAF V600E inhibitors (vemurafenib, dabrafenib). Mechanism: in melanoma, BRAF V600E constitutively activates the MAPK pathway (BRAF → MEK → ERK). In normal keratinocytes with wild-type RAS mutations (present in ~60% of pre-malignant keratinocytes), BRAF inhibition paradoxically causes transactivation of CRAF → amplified MAPK signalling through the RAS-CRAF axis → accelerated keratinocyte proliferation → SCC induction. Prevention: combining a MEK inhibitor (trametinib) with the BRAF inhibitor blocks the downstream paradoxical MAPK activation → reduces cutaneous SCC incidence (and also improves anti-tumour efficacy — dual BRAF+MEK blockade is now standard).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Dabrafenib + trametinib is the standard BRAF-targeted regimen for BRAF V600E/K metastatic melanoma. Also approved in BRAF V600E NSCLC, anaplastic thyroid cancer, and biliary tract cancer. Encorafenib + binimetinib is an alternative with a better tolerability profile (less pyrexia).
⚠️ Exam Trap: BRAF inhibitors ONLY work when the BRAF V600E/K mutation is present — they are INEFFECTIVE (and potentially harmful) in BRAF wild-type tumours or in colorectal cancer with BRAF V600E (due to EGFR-mediated feedback re-activation of MAPK — requires triple combination: BRAF + MEK + EGFR inhibition).
05 A patient with metastatic NSCLC has an EGFR exon 19 deletion. Osimertinib is started as first-line therapy. What is the mechanism, and why is osimertinib preferred over first-generation EGFR TKIs (erlotinib, gefitinib)?
Osimertinib is a 3rd-generation, irreversible EGFR TKI that covalently binds Cys797 in the EGFR kinase domain → permanently inhibits EGFR (including T790M-mutant EGFR). Exon 19 deletions (and exon 21 L858R substitution) are activating EGFR mutations — constitutive EGFR kinase activity drives tumour proliferation. Advantages over 1st-gen TKIs (erlotinib, gefitinib): (1) T790M resistance mutation — the most common resistance mechanism to 1st-gen TKIs (70%) — osimertinib retains full activity against T790M; (2) Superior CNS penetration (critical given 25–40% of EGFR-mutant NSCLC develop brain metastases); (3) FLAURA trial demonstrated longer PFS and OS vs 1st-gen TKIs as first-line therapy.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Osimertinib resistance mechanisms: C797S mutation (abolishes covalent binding), MET amplification, HER2 amplification, histological transformation to SCLC. For acquired resistance, re-biopsy or liquid biopsy (ctDNA) to identify the mechanism guides next therapy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: EGFR TKIs cause a characteristic acneiform rash (papulopustular, folliculocentric — different from acne vulgaris) on face/chest/back. Paradoxically, rash severity correlates positively with tumour response. Manage with topical clindamycin/tetracycline; severe rash → doxycycline + dose interruption. Also causes diarrhoea and paronychia (nail fold inflammation).
06 A patient with ALL is receiving high-dose methotrexate. 48 hours after infusion, MTX level is dangerously elevated and creatinine is rising. What is the antidote and its mechanism?
Glucarpidase (carboxypeptidase G2) — a bacterial enzyme that cleaves MTX into inactive metabolites (DAMPA + glutamate) in the plasma. It rapidly reduces plasma MTX levels by >98% within 15 minutes. Mechanism: carboxypeptidase G2 hydrolyses the C-terminal glutamate of MTX → DAMPA (which does not inhibit DHFR and is rapidly cleared). Used when MTX levels are dangerously elevated AND standard leucovorin rescue is insufficient (usually indicated when MTX >1 µmol/L at 42 h in a patient with AKI). Continue leucovorin (folinic acid) alongside glucarpidase — leucovorin rescues cells that have taken up MTX by providing pre-reduced folate bypassing the DHFR block.
💎 Clinical Pearl: High-dose MTX requires leucovorin rescue starting 24 hours after MTX infusion — leucovorin (folinic acid, 5-formyl-THF) enters the folate pool directly without requiring DHFR → rescues normal cells. Alkalinise urine (sodium bicarbonate IV) to keep pH >7 — MTX is more soluble at alkaline pH, preventing tubular precipitation and the AKI that delays clearance.
⚠️ Exam Trap: After glucarpidase, do NOT measure plasma MTX levels for 48 hours — glucarpidase interferes with standard MTX immunoassays (DAMPA cross-reacts) → falsely low readings. Use an alternative assay (HPLC) or wait until glucarpidase is cleared. Continue leucovorin doses based on the pre-glucarpidase level.
07 A 60-year-old man with multiple myeloma receives bortezomib. He develops a painful vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution on his trunk. What happened and how is it prevented?
Bortezomib-induced herpes zoster (shingles) reactivation. Bortezomib is a proteasome inhibitor (reversible inhibitor of the 26S proteasome's chymotryptic-like activity) → blocks ubiquitin-proteasome degradation of regulatory proteins → accumulation of pro-apoptotic proteins (p21, p27, IκB) → malignant plasma cell death. However, proteasome inhibition also suppresses T-cell immune surveillance → reactivation of latent varicella-zoster virus (VZV) in dorsal root ganglia → shingles. Prevention: prophylactic antiviral therapy (acyclovir 400 mg BD or valacyclovir 500 mg OD) is mandatory for ALL patients receiving bortezomib — started before treatment and continued throughout.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Bortezomib also causes peripheral neuropathy (painful, predominantly sensory, dose-limiting) — due to proteasome inhibition in sensory neurons leading to ER stress and axonal dysfunction. Subcutaneous administration significantly reduces neuropathy vs IV (same efficacy). Carfilzomib (irreversible proteasome inhibitor) causes less neuropathy but more cardiovascular toxicity (hypertension, HF).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Bortezomib causes thrombocytopenia with a unique cyclical pattern — nadir at day 11 of each cycle, recovery by day 21. This is predictable and manageable; it is NOT cumulative myelosuppression. The mechanism involves proteasome inhibition in megakaryocytes transiently impairing platelet release.
08 Anthracyclines (doxorubicin) are used for breast cancer and lymphoma. A 45-year-old woman has received 400 mg/m² cumulative doxorubicin. Should her next course be given?
Standard cumulative dose limit for doxorubicin is 450–550 mg/m² — above this threshold, cumulative cardiomyopathy risk rises steeply (~7–26% at 550 mg/m²). At 400 mg/m², she is approaching this limit but has not exceeded it. Decision requires baseline and current LVEF assessment — if LVEF is preserved (>50%) and cardiac risk factors are low, one more cycle may be feasible with close monitoring. Mechanism of anthracycline cardiotoxicity: doxorubicin + iron → Fenton reaction → hydroxyl radical formation → ROS → lipid peroxidation of cardiomyocyte membranes → irreversible myocyte necrosis and fibrosis. Also inhibits topoisomerase IIβ in cardiomyocytes (distinct from topoisomerase IIα in cancer cells).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Dexrazoxane — an iron chelator (EDTA analogue, also a topoisomerase IIβ inhibitor) — is the only FDA-approved cardioprotectant for anthracycline cardiotoxicity. Given before each doxorubicin dose. Cardio-protective liposomal doxorubicin (Doxil/Caelyx) reduces peak plasma concentrations and preferentially accumulates in tumour vasculature → less cardiac exposure at equivalent anti-tumour doses.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Anthracyclines cause two types of cardiotoxicity: (1) Acute — arrhythmias during or immediately after infusion (self-limiting, not dose-dependent); (2) Chronic — cardiomyopathy months-years later (dose-dependent, irreversible). Only chronic cardiotoxicity is cumulative dose-related. Childhood cancer survivors who received anthracyclines require lifelong cardiac surveillance.
09 A patient with ovarian cancer receives paclitaxel (Taxol). During infusion she develops flushing, urticaria, and bronchospasm within 10 minutes. After recovery, she is rechallenged with a slower infusion and premedication. Why does this work for paclitaxel but is contraindicated in true drug allergy?
Paclitaxel hypersensitivity reactions are predominantly non-IgE-mediated (pseudo-allergic/anaphylactoid) — caused by the Cremophor EL (polyoxyethylated castor oil) vehicle that solubilises paclitaxel. Cremophor EL directly activates complement and stimulates mast cell degranulation → histamine release → anaphylactoid reaction. Standard premedication (dexamethasone + diphenhydramine + H2 blocker given the night before and morning of) + slow infusion rate successfully prevents recurrence in ~80%. This is NOT true IgE-mediated allergy to paclitaxel itself — rechallenge with premedication is safe. nab-paclitaxel (Abraxane — albumin-bound nanoparticle paclitaxel, Cremophor-free) eliminates the vehicle-related reactions entirely.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Paclitaxel mechanism: binds β-tubulin and stabilises polymerised microtubules → prevents depolymerisation → mitotic spindle cannot disassemble → cell arrested in G2/M → apoptosis. This is the OPPOSITE of vinca alkaloids (vincristine/vinblastine) which destabilise microtubules (inhibit polymerisation).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Docetaxel (also a taxane) uses polysorbate 80 as a vehicle — different from Cremophor EL but also causes hypersensitivity reactions. Docetaxel causes distinctive fluid retention (pleural effusion, peripheral oedema, ascites) due to capillary leak syndrome — managed with prophylactic dexamethasone for 3 days around each infusion.
10 A patient with KRAS wild-type metastatic colorectal cancer is treated with cetuximab. After 4 months, disease progresses. Testing reveals a new KRAS G12C mutation. Explain the resistance mechanism.
Cetuximab is a chimeric anti-EGFR monoclonal antibody (IgG1) that blocks EGF binding → prevents EGFR dimerisation and downstream MAPK/PI3K activation. Efficacy requires wild-type RAS (KRAS/NRAS) — mutant KRAS G12C locks RAS in a permanently active GTP-bound state → downstream MAPK pathway is constitutively active regardless of EGFR inhibition → primary resistance. Acquired KRAS G12C mutation represents clonal selection under cetuximab pressure — a minor pre-existing clone with KRAS G12C expands because it survives EGFR blockade. Treatment: sotorasib or adagrasib (KRAS G12C-specific covalent inhibitors — bind the GDP-bound form of KRAS G12C via Cys12 → lock KRAS in inactive state).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Before starting anti-EGFR therapy (cetuximab, panitumumab) in colorectal cancer, extended RAS testing (KRAS exons 2/3/4, NRAS exons 2/3/4) AND BRAF V600E must be done. Any RAS mutation → anti-EGFR ineffective. BRAF V600E → poor prognosis, requires BRAF+MEK+EGFR triple inhibition (encorafenib + cetuximab ± binimetinib).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Cetuximab (IgG1) mediates ADCC — antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity contributes to its efficacy. Panitumumab (IgG2) does NOT mediate ADCC — no Fc effector function. Cetuximab causes infusion reactions (first dose especially) and acneiform rash. The rash is biomarker of response — patients with severe rash have better outcomes.
11 Tamoxifen is used for ER-positive breast cancer. A patient on tamoxifen also takes fluoxetine for depression. Her oncologist is concerned. Why?
Tamoxifen is a prodrug requiring CYP2D6-mediated activation to endoxifen (the active metabolite with 30–100× higher affinity for ER than tamoxifen). Fluoxetine (and paroxetine) are potent CYP2D6 inhibitors → reduce endoxifen plasma levels by ~50–75% → potentially compromised anti-tumour efficacy. Studies suggest CYP2D6 poor metabolisers (or those on CYP2D6 inhibitors) have higher breast cancer recurrence rates. Safe antidepressant alternatives: venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine, citalopram, escitalopram — these do not significantly inhibit CYP2D6 and do not reduce endoxifen levels.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Tamoxifen increases risk of endometrial cancer (it is an ER agonist in the uterus — selective ER modulator/SERM) and thromboembolic events (DVT/PE). Annual gynaecological review and prompt investigation of abnormal uterine bleeding are mandatory during tamoxifen therapy. Postmenopausal patients: aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane) are preferred — no uterine stimulation.
⚠️ Exam Trap: CYP2D6 poor metabolisers (~7–10% of Caucasians) convert tamoxifen to endoxifen very inefficiently even without inhibitors — consider aromatase inhibitor if postmenopausal. Tamoxifen also undergoes CYP3A4 metabolism — strong CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine) reduce tamoxifen levels. Avoid concurrent use.
12 Irinotecan (CPT-11) is given for colorectal cancer. The patient develops severe diarrhoea and neutropenia. A UGT1A1*28 polymorphism is found. Explain.
Irinotecan is a topoisomerase I inhibitor (prodrug) → converted by carboxylesterases to SN-38 (active metabolite, 100–1000× more potent). SN-38 is glucuronidated by UGT1A1 → SN-38-G (inactive, excreted in bile). In the gut, intestinal bacteria deconjugate SN-38-G → SN-38 re-released → damages intestinal epithelium → late-onset diarrhoea. UGT1A1*28 polymorphism (7 TA repeats in TATA box instead of 6) → reduced UGT1A1 expression → impaired SN-38 glucuronidation → SN-38 accumulates → severe diarrhoea AND neutropenia (SN-38 damages bone marrow). Homozygous *28/*28 patients require dose reduction at the outset.
💎 Clinical Pearl: The UGT1A1*28 polymorphism is the SAME polymorphism responsible for Gilbert syndrome (mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia) — UGT1A1 also glucuronidates bilirubin. Gilbert syndrome patients (benign condition) have elevated bilirubin under fasting/stress — but also have increased irinotecan toxicity. Test UGT1A1 before high-dose irinotecan.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Irinotecan causes TWO types of diarrhoea: (1) Early (during or shortly after infusion) — cholinergic (irinotecan inhibits acetylcholinesterase → excess ACh → GI hypermotility, flushing, salivation) — treat with atropine; (2) Late (≥24h after) — SN-38 mucosal toxicity — treat with high-dose loperamide (4 mg immediately then 2 mg every 2h until diarrhoea-free for 12h).
13 A patient with CLL is given fludarabine + cyclophosphamide + rituximab (FCR). Why is Pneumocystis jirovecii prophylaxis mandatory, and what immunological effect persists for years?
FCR causes profound and prolonged lymphocyte depletion: fludarabine (purine analogue → incorporates into DNA → chain termination, also inhibits DNA polymerase and ribonucleotide reductase) preferentially kills lymphocytes (high deoxynucleotide pools). FCR dramatically depletes CD4+ T cells — CD4 counts can fall to <200 cells/µL and remain low for 12–24 months or longer. Below CD4 <200, PCP risk is high → TMP-SMX prophylaxis mandatory. Also: CMV reactivation monitoring, VZV prophylaxis (acyclovir), and avoidance of live vaccines throughout and for 12+ months after. Hypogammaglobulinaemia (from B-cell depletion by rituximab) persists for 6–12 months → IVIG replacement if recurrent infections.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Fludarabine is contraindicated as a single agent in the Coombs-positive setting — it can precipitate or worsen autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (AIHA) in CLL by unknown mechanisms (possibly immune dysregulation). Rituximab + chlorambucil is safer in CLL with AIHA.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Fludarabine causes a distinctive haemolytic transfusion reaction risk — fludarabine-treated patients must receive only irradiated blood products (to prevent transfusion-associated GVHD — residual viable donor T cells can engraft in the profoundly lymphocytopaenic recipient → fatal GVHD). This requirement persists for LIFE after fludarabine.
14 A patient with chronic myelomonocytic leukaemia (CMML) and IDH2 mutation receives enasidenib. Twelve days later he develops fever, rapidly increasing WBC, and pulmonary infiltrates. What syndrome is occurring?
Differentiation syndrome (DS) — previously called all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) syndrome or retinoic acid syndrome when described in APL. IDH2 (isocitrate dehydrogenase 2) mutation produces 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG) — an oncometabolite that inhibits TET2 and histone demethylases → DNA hypermethylation → differentiation block → accumulation of immature myeloid blasts. Enasidenib inhibits mutant IDH2 → 2-HG levels fall → epigenetic reprogramming → blasts undergo rapid differentiation into mature neutrophils → massive cytokine release (IL-6, IL-8, MMP) → capillary leak → pulmonary infiltrates, pleural/pericardial effusions, fever, leucocytosis, weight gain. Treatment: dexamethasone 10 mg IV BD — reduces inflammatory cytokine cascade.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Differentiation syndrome also occurs with: ATRA in APL (classic), arsenic trioxide in APL, ivosidenib (IDH1 inhibitor). The same syndrome is now called differentiation syndrome across all these contexts. APL treatment: ATRA + arsenic trioxide — two agents that together induce PML-RARα fusion protein degradation via distinct mechanisms → higher cure rates (~90%).
⚠️ Exam Trap: In APL (AML-M3), never give cytotoxic chemotherapy without ATRA — APL blasts have high intracellular t-PA (tissue plasminogen activator) → spontaneous fibrinolysis → catastrophic DIC/bleeding. ATRA must be started immediately on clinical suspicion, before molecular confirmation. The t(15;17) PML-RARα fusion creates the RARA fusion — ATRA bypasses the PML-RARα differentiation block.
15 A patient with metastatic renal cell carcinoma (RCC) is started on sunitinib. After 4 weeks, blood pressure rises to 165/105 mmHg. Should sunitinib be stopped?
No — hypertension is an on-target effect of VEGFR inhibition and is a pharmacodynamic biomarker of therapeutic activity. Sunitinib inhibits VEGFR1/2/3, PDGFR, c-Kit, FLT3 (multi-targeted TKI). VEGFR blockade → reduced VEGF signalling in endothelial cells → reduced nitric oxide (NO) production → vasoconstriction → hypertension. This is manageable with antihypertensives — do NOT stop sunitinib. Use: amlodipine (CCB — does not inhibit CYP3A4 significantly), or ACE inhibitor/ARB (also may be renoprotective). Paradoxically, patients who develop more severe hypertension have better tumour responses to VEGFR inhibitors.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other common sunitinib/VEGFR inhibitor toxicities: hand-foot syndrome (palmar-plantar erythrodysaesthesia — painful blistering of palms and soles), hypothyroidism (thyroid VEGF dependency → follicular atrophy — check TFTs every 3 months), QT prolongation, hepatotoxicity. Cabozantinib additionally inhibits MET and AXL — used in sunitinib-refractory RCC and HCC.
⚠️ Exam Trap: VEGFR inhibitors carry a risk of arterial thromboembolism (MI, stroke) — VEGF is normally anti-thrombotic (promotes NO and prostacyclin production). Also: impaired wound healing and GI perforation (VEGF required for mucosal repair) — hold VEGFR inhibitors perioperatively (>2 weeks before elective surgery).
16 Pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) is started for NSCLC. Six weeks later the patient develops acute-onset watery diarrhoea 8 times per day. Colonoscopy shows diffuse colonic inflammation. What is this and how is it treated?
Immune-related adverse event (irAE) — immune-mediated colitis. Pembrolizumab blocks PD-1 on T cells → removes inhibitory signal → enhanced T-cell activation against tumour AND self-antigens → loss of peripheral immune tolerance → autoimmune colitis (resembling inflammatory bowel disease, predominantly CD8+ T-cell mediated). Grade 3 colitis (≥7 stools above baseline per day): (1) Hold pembrolizumab permanently; (2) High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1–2 mg/kg/day); (3) If no improvement in 2–3 days: add infliximab (anti-TNF, 5 mg/kg) — effective in steroid-refractory irAE colitis (vedolizumab is an alternative for infliximab-contraindicated patients).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other common irAEs by checkpoint inhibitor: pneumonitis (anti-PD-1/PD-L1 — dry cough, dyspnoea, bilateral GGOs), hepatitis (anti-CTLA-4 — elevated ALT/AST), endocrinopathies (hypophysitis, hypothyroidism, adrenalitis — common with both classes), dermatitis (maculopapular rash, vitiligo). Most irAEs respond to corticosteroids; severe/refractory require infliximab (colitis) or mycophenolate (hepatitis).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab) has a higher rate of colitis than anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab). Combined ipilimumab + nivolumab (dual checkpoint blockade for melanoma) has the highest irAE rate (~59% grade 3–4). Importantly: do NOT use infliximab for immune-mediated hepatitis (anti-TNF is hepatotoxic) — use mycophenolate or azathioprine for steroid-refractory hepatitis instead.
17 Methotrexate 25 mg/week is used for psoriasis. The patient's GP prescribes trimethoprim for a UTI. Two weeks later, the patient presents with mouth ulcers, pancytopenia, and fever. Explain.
A life-threatening drug interaction: both methotrexate and trimethoprim inhibit dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR). MTX (competitive DHFR inhibitor) + TMP (also inhibits DHFR) → additive folate depletion → impaired thymidylate and purine synthesis → bone marrow failure (pancytopenia), mucositis. Low-dose MTX for psoriasis/RA has a narrow therapeutic window — adding any DHFR inhibitor can push into toxicity. Management: stop both drugs immediately; IV leucovorin (folinic acid) rescue; G-CSF if severe neutropenia; supportive care. Prevention: warn all MTX patients to alert their pharmacist/GP before any new antibiotic — TMP and TMP-SMX are absolutely contraindicated with concurrent MTX.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other drugs that potentiate MTX toxicity: NSAIDs (reduce MTX renal tubular secretion → elevated MTX levels → toxicity — particularly dangerous with high-dose MTX in cancer); penicillins (same mechanism). Probenecid also increases MTX levels by blocking tubular secretion.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Folic acid (1–5 mg/day) is prescribed alongside low-dose MTX for RA/psoriasis to reduce GI and mucosal toxicity WITHOUT reducing anti-inflammatory efficacy. Folate supplementation does NOT prevent MTX hepatotoxicity. For methotrexate toxicity rescue (overdose or impaired clearance), use leucovorin (folinic acid) — not folic acid — as it bypasses the DHFR block.
18 A patient with colorectal cancer on FOLFOX (5-FU + leucovorin + oxaliplatin) develops chest pain and coronary artery spasm during 5-FU infusion. How does 5-FU cause this?
5-FU cardiotoxicity — coronary artery vasospasm is the most common cardiac manifestation, occurring in ~1–19% of patients. Mechanisms: (1) 5-FU metabolites (fluoroacetate, fluorocitrate) inhibit Krebs cycle in cardiomyocytes → impaired energy production → vascular smooth muscle dysfunction; (2) Direct endothelial toxicity → thromboxane A2 release → coronary vasospasm; (3) Protein kinase C activation → vasoconstriction. Management: stop 5-FU infusion immediately, sublingual nitrates ± calcium channel blocker for acute spasm. Future: switch to capecitabine (oral prodrug, lower peak plasma levels) — lower cardiotoxicity rate OR substitute raltitrexed in truly 5-FU-intolerant patients.
💎 Clinical Pearl: 5-FU toxicity is significantly influenced by dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPD) deficiency — DPD metabolises 80% of 5-FU. DPD-deficient patients (DPYD*2A and other variants, ~3–8% of population) have dramatically elevated 5-FU levels → severe mucositis, diarrhoea, myelosuppression, potentially fatal. DPYD genotyping recommended before 5-FU/capecitabine therapy in Europe (FDA guidance in US is evolving).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Leucovorin (folinic acid) in FOLFOX does NOT rescue folate — it is given to potentiate 5-FU. Leucovorin stabilises the ternary complex between 5-FdUMP (active 5-FU metabolite), thymidylate synthase (TS), and the folate cofactor → prolonged TS inhibition → greater anti-tumour effect. This is the opposite of leucovorin's role as MTX antidote.
19 A patient with Philadelphia-chromosome-positive ALL is on dasatinib. She develops dyspnoea, bilateral pleural effusions, and leg oedema. What is this toxicity and its mechanism?
Dasatinib-induced pleural effusion — occurs in up to 35% of patients. Dasatinib inhibits BCR-ABL (more potently than imatinib) AND SRC kinases. SRC inhibition disrupts vascular endothelial and lymphatic tight junctions → increased vascular permeability and impaired lymphatic drainage → pleural fluid accumulation. Also: dasatinib inhibits PDGFR which normally promotes pleural lymphatic drainage. Management: hold dasatinib, diuretics (furosemide), thoracentesis if large/symptomatic, short course of corticosteroids (prednisone 20 mg tapering over 1–2 weeks). Resume at lower dose or switch to ponatinib/bosutinib.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Dasatinib vs imatinib: dasatinib is ~325× more potent against BCR-ABL, penetrates the CNS better (important in Ph+ ALL where CNS prophylaxis/treatment is needed), and is active against most imatinib-resistant mutations (except T315I). However, pleural effusion and pulmonary arterial hypertension are class-specific dasatinib toxicities not seen with imatinib.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Nilotinib (2nd-gen BCR-ABL inhibitor) causes QT prolongation and has a black-box warning for sudden cardiac death — avoid in patients with long QT, hypokalaemia, or on other QT-prolonging drugs. Nilotinib also causes hyperglycaemia and is associated with peripheral arterial occlusive disease (PAD) — check ABI annually and avoid in patients with established PAD.
20 Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd) is given for HER2-positive breast cancer. What makes this antibody-drug conjugate different from standard trastuzumab, and what is the key pulmonary toxicity to monitor?
T-DXd is an antibody-drug conjugate (ADC): trastuzumab (anti-HER2 antibody) linked via a cleavable linker to DXd (an exatecan derivative, topoisomerase I inhibitor — 10× more potent than irinotecan's active metabolite SN-38). Key advantage: bystander killing effect — DXd is membrane-permeable; after ADC enters HER2+ tumour cells and the linker is cleaved, free DXd diffuses into adjacent HER2-low or HER2-negative cells → kills heterogeneous tumour populations. This explains efficacy in HER2-low breast cancer (IHC 1+ or 2+/ISH-negative). Key pulmonary toxicity: interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis — occurs in ~10–15% of patients (grade ≥3 in ~3%). Monitor for new respiratory symptoms; CT chest for any new dyspnoea. Hold for grade 2; permanently discontinue for grade 3–4.
💎 Clinical Pearl: T-DXd has dramatically expanded the HER2 therapeutic landscape — DESTINY-Breast04 showed efficacy in HER2-LOW breast cancer (previously untreatable with HER2-targeted therapy). T-DXd is also approved for HER2-mutant (not amplified) NSCLC and HER2+ gastric cancer — demonstrating tumour-agnostic HER2 targeting.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Distinguish T-DXd from T-DM1 (ado-trastuzumab emtansine) — both are trastuzumab ADCs but different payloads: T-DM1 uses DM1 (maytansinoid, antimicrotubule) with a non-cleavable linker → NO bystander killing. T-DXd uses DXd (topo I inhibitor) with a cleavable linker → bystander killing → broader efficacy spectrum. T-DXd is significantly more active than T-DM1 in post-trastuzumab HER2+ breast cancer.

04 Immunotherapy, mAbs & CAR-T 20 Questions
1What do Pembrolizumab and Nivolumab inhibit?
PD-1 (Programmed Death-1) immune checkpoint.
2What does Ipilimumab target?
CTLA-4. Enhances T-cell priming in lymph nodes.
3Why do checkpoint inhibitors cause autoimmune side effects?
They remove inhibitory signals on T-cells, leading to hyperactivation against normal tissues (irAEs).
4Name 5 immune-related adverse events (irAEs).
Colitis, pneumonitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis, type 1 DM, hypophysitis, dermatitis.
5How are irAEs primarily managed?
Corticosteroids (and drug discontinuation for severe cases).
6What is the target of Rituximab?
CD20 on B-cells.
7What serious infection risk is associated with Rituximab?
Hepatitis B reactivation and Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML, JC virus).
8What is the target and use of Trastuzumab?
HER2 receptor. HER2-positive breast cancer.
9What is the major toxicity of Trastuzumab?
Cardiotoxicity (reversible, unlike Doxorubicin).
10Why must Trastuzumab never be given concurrently with Doxorubicin?
Both are cardiotoxic. The combination causes additive/synergistic cardiac damage.
11What is an Antibody-Drug Conjugate (ADC)?
An antibody linked to a cytotoxic payload. The antibody delivers the toxin specifically to tumor cells.
12Name an ADC and its components.
T-DM1 (ado-trastuzumab emtansine): Trastuzumab (anti-HER2) + emtansine (microtubule inhibitor).
13What are the 4 steps of CAR-T cell therapy?
1) Harvest patient T-cells. 2) Engineer CAR receptor. 3) Expand ex vivo. 4) Infuse after lymphodepletion.
14What is the most common target for CAR-T cells?
CD19 (found on B-cell malignancies).
15What is Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS)?
Massive cytokine release (IL-6, TNF-α) from activated CAR-T cells causing fever, hypotension, and multi-organ failure.
16How is CRS treated?
Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6 receptor antibody). Corticosteroids if refractory.
17What is ICANS?
Immune Effector Cell-Associated Neurotoxicity Syndrome. A neurological complication of CAR-T therapy (confusion, seizures, cerebral edema).
18Name two FDA-approved CAR-T products.
Tisagenlecleucel (Kymriah) and Axicabtagene ciloleucel (Yescarta).
19Where does PD-1 blockade act: lymph node or tumor microenvironment?
Tumor microenvironment. CTLA-4 blockade acts in the lymph node.
20What is Bevacizumab and its mechanism?
Anti-VEGF monoclonal antibody. Neutralizes VEGF to starve tumors of blood supply (anti-angiogenic).
05 Hormonal Therapies & Integration 20 Questions
1How does Tamoxifen act in breast tissue vs uterine tissue?
Antagonist in breast (blocks ER), agonist in uterus (stimulates endometrium → cancer risk).
2What distinguishes Raloxifene from Tamoxifen?
Raloxifene is antagonist in both breast and uterus. No endometrial cancer risk. Used for osteoporosis prevention.
3What is Fulvestrant?
A pure ER antagonist (SERD) that causes ER degradation. Used after Tamoxifen failure.
4In which patient population are Aromatase Inhibitors effective?
Postmenopausal women only (they block peripheral aromatase, the sole estrogen source post-menopause).
5Name 2 aromatase inhibitors.
Anastrozole, Letrozole (non-steroidal). Exemestane (steroidal, irreversible).
6What is the key side effect of aromatase inhibitors?
Osteoporosis and musculoskeletal pain (due to estrogen depletion).
7What is the mechanism of Leuprolide?
GnRH agonist. Continuous use downregulates GnRH receptors, decreasing LH/FSH and testosterone.
8Why is an antiandrogen co-administered when starting Leuprolide?
To block the initial testosterone flare (first 1–2 weeks) before receptor downregulation occurs.
9What advantage does Degarelix (GnRH antagonist) have over Leuprolide?
No initial testosterone flare. Directly blocks GnRH receptors.
10What is Enzalutamide?
A next-generation antiandrogen with higher AR affinity. Effective in castration-resistant prostate cancer.
11What does Abiraterone inhibit?
CYP17 — blocks androgen synthesis in testes, adrenals, and tumor.
12Why must Abiraterone be given with Prednisone?
CYP17 blockade causes mineralocorticoid excess (hypertension, hypokalemia). Prednisone suppresses ACTH, preventing this.
13What is the role of glucocorticoids in cancer therapy?
Induce apoptosis in lymphoid malignancies, reduce cerebral edema, manage irAEs, part of many regimens (CHOP).
14What do CDK4/6 inhibitors (Palbociclib) do?
Block cell cycle G1-to-S transition. Combined with hormonal therapy in ER+ breast cancer.
15What is the key toxicity of CDK4/6 inhibitors?
Neutropenia.
16A premenopausal woman with ER+ breast cancer should receive which hormonal therapy?
Tamoxifen (not an aromatase inhibitor, which won't work in premenopausal women).
17A postmenopausal woman with ER+ breast cancer should receive which hormonal therapy?
Aromatase inhibitor (Anastrozole, Letrozole) — more effective than Tamoxifen post-menopause.
18What is the BEP regimen for testicular cancer?
Bleomycin, Etoposide, and Platinum (Cisplatin). Highly curative.
19What is tumor lysis syndrome and how is it prevented?
Rapid cell lysis releases K+, phosphate, uric acid → renal failure, arrhythmias. Prevented with hydration, Allopurinol, or Rasburicase.
20Match: Doxorubicin, Cisplatin, Bleomycin, Vincristine to their unique toxicities.
Doxorubicin: cardiomyopathy. Cisplatin: nephrotoxicity. Bleomycin: pulmonary fibrosis. Vincristine: peripheral neuropathy.
