Section 1 · Diuretics & Renal Pharmacology

▸★★★Classify diuretics (sites & mechanisms). Pharmacological basis, indications, ADRs & rationale of potassium-sparing diuretics; note on diuretic resistance & non-diuretic uses.

Potassium-sparing diuretics — basis & rationale

  • Aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone): block mineralocorticoid receptor in collecting duct → ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ retention.
  • ENaC blockers (amiloride, triamterene): directly block epithelial Na⁺ channel.
  • Rationale: weak diuretics alone, but conserve K⁺ → used with K⁺-wasting loop/thiazide to offset hypokalaemia; spironolactone gives mortality benefit in HF (RALES) & treats resistant HTN, primary hyperaldosteronism, ascites.

Indications

Heart failure, ascites/cirrhosis, hyperaldosteronism (Conn's), resistant hypertension, hirsutism/PCOS (spironolactone anti-androgen), Liddle's syndrome (amiloride).

Adverse effects

  • Hyperkalaemia (key danger — esp. with ACEi/ARB/renal failure), metabolic acidosis.
  • Spironolactone: gynaecomastia, menstrual irregularity (eplerenone more selective → fewer).

Diuretic resistance

Failure to achieve adequate diuresis despite full dose. Causes: poor adherence/excess Na intake, ↓ absorption (gut oedema), ↓ renal perfusion, nephron remodelling/distal hypertrophy, NSAIDs, hypoalbuminaemia, CKD. Management: IV/continuous infusion, ↑ dose, sequential nephron blockade (add thiazide/metolazone to loop), treat cause, restrict salt.

Non-diuretic uses

Acetazolamide — glaucoma, altitude sickness, epilepsy, alkalinise urine; mannitol — ↑ICP/cerebral oedema, acute glaucoma; thiazides — nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, idiopathic hypercalciuria/renal stones; loop — hypercalcaemia, hyperkalaemia.

▸★★Physiology of urine formation & drugs affecting it; inhibitors of renal epithelial Na⁺ channels & high-ceiling (loop) diuretics with clinical applications.

Physiology of urine formation

  1. Glomerular filtration (~180 L/day) of plasma.
  2. Tubular reabsorption: PCT (~65% Na, via Na/H, glucose, CA), thin/thick loop of Henle (NKCC2, countercurrent multiplier → medullary hypertonicity), DCT (NCC), collecting duct (ENaC, aquaporins under ADH, aldosterone).
  3. Tubular secretion (K⁺, H⁺, organic acids/bases).

Diuretics act at each segment (see Q1 table).

Renal epithelial Na⁺ channel (ENaC) inhibitors

  • Amiloride, triamterene — block luminal ENaC in late DCT/collecting duct → ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption & ↓ K⁺ secretion (K⁺-sparing).
  • Weak diuretics; uses: with thiazide/loop to prevent hypokalaemia, Liddle's syndrome, lithium-induced nephrogenic DI (amiloride).

High-ceiling (loop) diuretics

  • Mechanism: inhibit NKCC2 in thick ascending limb → most efficacious (up to 20–25% of filtered Na); abolish medullary gradient; ↑ Ca²⁺ & Mg²⁺ excretion; venodilation (acute pulmonary oedema).
  • Clinical use: acute pulmonary oedema/HF, oedema (renal/hepatic), hypertension with renal failure, hypercalcaemia, hyperkalaemia, forced diuresis.
  • ADR: hypokalaemia, hypocalcaemia, hypomagnesaemia, metabolic alkalosis, ototoxicity (esp. with aminoglycosides), hyperuricaemia, dehydration.

Section 2 · RAAS Modifiers, Antihypertensives & Heart Failure

▸★★★Drugs modulating RAAS — basis, MOA, uses, ADRs, rationale of ACEi+ARB / ARNI in HF & HTN; note on direct renin inhibitors & triphasic response to angiotensin-II.

The RAAS cascade & drug targets

Cascade: Angiotensinogen →(renin)→ Ang I →(ACE)→ Ang II → AT1 receptor (vasoconstriction, aldosterone, Na/water retention, remodelling) + stimulates aldosterone. Drug targets: renin (aliskiren), ACE (ACEi), AT1 (ARB), aldosterone (MRA), neprilysin (sacubitril ↑ natriuretic peptides).
ClassDrugsMechanism
ACE inhibitorsEnalapril, ramipril, lisinopril, perindopril↓ Ang II, ↑ bradykinin
ARBsLosartan, telmisartan, valsartan, olmesartanBlock AT1 receptor
Direct renin inhibitorAliskirenInhibit renin (first step)
MRASpironolactone, eplerenoneBlock aldosterone
ARNISacubitril/valsartanNeprilysin inhibition + ARB

Uses

Hypertension, heart failure (HFrEF), post-MI, diabetic nephropathy/proteinuric CKD, LV dysfunction.

Adverse effects

  • ACEi: dry cough & angioedema (bradykinin), hyperkalaemia, ↑ creatinine, first-dose hypotension, teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy). ARBs: similar but no cough.

Rationale: ACEi + ARB and ARNI

  • ACEi + ARB combination: dual RAAS blockade — generally avoided (ONTARGET) due to ↑ hyperkalaemia, hypotension & renal dysfunction without survival benefit.
  • ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan): neprilysin inhibition ↑ natriuretic peptides (vasodilation, natriuresis, anti-remodelling) + AT1 blockade → superior to enalapril in HFrEF (PARADIGM-HF). Do not combine with ACEi (angioedema; 36-h washout).

Direct renin inhibitor

Aliskiren blocks RAAS at the rate-limiting step; modest antihypertensive; limited use; not combined with ACEi/ARB in diabetics (ALTITUDE — harm).

Triphasic response to angiotensin-II

IV Ang II classically gives: (1) rapid pressor rise (direct vasoconstriction via AT1); (2) a brief secondary rise/plateau from sympathetic facilitation & catecholamine release; (3) a delayed sustained pressor via aldosterone-mediated Na/water retention. (Demonstrates Ang II's vascular, neural & renal actions.)

Goals

Relieve symptoms, slow remodelling, reduce hospitalisation & mortality.

"Four pillars" of HFrEF (guideline-directed medical therapy)

PillarDrugsBenefit
1. ARNI / ACEi / ARBSacubitril-valsartan (preferred)↓ remodelling, mortality
2. β-blockerCarvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate↓ sympathetic harm, mortality ↓

Other agents

  • Diuretics (loop) — symptomatic congestion relief.
  • Ivabradine — If-channel, if HR >70 in sinus rhythm.
  • Vericiguat — soluble guanylate cyclase stimulator (worsening HF).
  • Digoxin — symptom/rate control. Hydralazine + ISDN — esp. African-Americans or ACEi-intolerant.
  • Omecamtiv mecarbil — cardiac myosin activator (investigational).

HFpEF

SGLT2 inhibitors now have proven benefit (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER); manage comorbidities, diuretics for congestion.

Recent/guidelines (ESC 2021/2023, ACC/AHA 2022): start all four pillars early & together (not sequentially); SGLT2i across the EF spectrum; finerenone for HF + CKD/diabetes.
▸★★Classify antihypertensives; MOA, uses & ADRs of ACEi & ARBs; combination therapy, resistant HTN, hypertensive emergencies & HTN in renal failure.

Classification of antihypertensives

  • Diuretics: thiazides, loop, K⁺-sparing.
  • RAAS blockers: ACEi, ARB, renin inhibitor, ARNI.
  • Calcium channel blockers: amlodipine (DHP), verapamil/diltiazem (non-DHP).
  • Sympatholytics: β-blockers, α1-blockers, central α2-agonists (clonidine, methyldopa).
  • Vasodilators: hydralazine, minoxidil, nitroprusside.

ACEi & ARBs (MOA/uses/ADRs)

See Section 2 Q1. Key: ACEi cause cough/angioedema; both contraindicated in pregnancy & bilateral renal artery stenosis; renoprotective in proteinuric CKD/diabetes.

Combination therapy principles

  • Preferred: ACEi/ARB + CCB, ACEi/ARB + thiazide, CCB + thiazide.
  • Avoid ACEi + ARB together; single-pill combinations improve adherence; start 2 drugs if BP ≥20/10 above target.

Resistant hypertension

BP uncontrolled on ≥3 drugs (incl. a diuretic) at optimal doses. Confirm adherence, exclude white-coat & secondary causes; add spironolactone (4th-line, PATHWAY-2); consider device therapy (renal denervation).

Hypertensive emergency

Severe BP + acute target-organ damage → IV agents, controlled lowering (~25% in first hour): labetalol, nicardipine, nitroprusside, GTN, esmolol, clevidipine; fenoldopam (renal-sparing).

HTN in renal failure

ACEi/ARB renoprotective (monitor K⁺/creatinine; loop diuretic preferred over thiazide at low GFR); CCBs effective & safe; salt restriction; often need multiple drugs.

▸★★Present status & recent advances in management of hypertension; experimental & clinical methods for evaluation of antihypertensive agents.

Present status & recent advances

  • Earlier & lower BP targets (ACC/AHA <130/80; SPRINT trial — intensive control ↓ CV events).
  • Single-pill combinations first-line; emphasis on out-of-office (home/ABPM) monitoring.
  • Renal denervation (catheter-based) — device therapy for resistant HTN.
  • Novel drugs: aprocitentan (dual endothelin antagonist, 2024), zilebesiran (siRNA against angiotensinogen — twice-yearly, trials), finerenone (nonsteroidal MRA), sacubitril combinations.

Evaluation of antihypertensive agents

Experimental (preclinical)Clinical
Spontaneously hypertensive rats (SHR); renal artery clamp (Goldblatt — 2K1C/1K1C); DOCA-salt model; tail-cuff/telemetry BP; isolated aortic ring; anaesthetised normotensive dog/cat BPSphygmomanometry, ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM); randomised controlled trials with hard endpoints (stroke, MI, mortality); surrogate markers (LVH regression, proteinuria); dose-ranging & comparative trials

Section 3 · Antianginal Drugs & Ischemic Heart Disease

▸★★★Classify antianginals; pathophysiology & pharmacotherapy of stable & variant angina; MOA/uses/limitations of nitrates, β-blockers, CCBs & newer agents (metabolic modulators, K⁺-channel openers); recent advances.

Pathophysiology

Angina = myocardial O₂ demand > supply. Stable (exertional): fixed atheromatous stenosis. Variant (Prinzmetal): coronary vasospasm. Treatment aims to ↓ demand and/or ↑ supply.

Classification & mechanisms

ClassDrugsMechanism
Organic nitratesGTN, isosorbide di/mononitrate↑NO→↑cGMP→venodilation→↓preload→↓O₂ demand; coronary dilation
β-blockersMetoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol↓HR, contractility → ↓demand (first-line stable angina; NOT in variant)
CCBsAmlodipine, diltiazem, verapamilCoronary & peripheral vasodilation, ↓afterload; drug of choice in variant
K⁺-channel openerNicorandilOpens K-ATP + nitrate-like → arterial & venodilation
Metabolic modulatorTrimetazidine, ranolazineShift metabolism to glucose oxidation; ranolazine inhibits late Na⁺ current
If-channel inhibitorIvabradine↓HR (sinus) without inotropy

Therapy by type

  • Stable angina: acute relief — sublingual GTN; prophylaxis — β-blocker and/or CCB ± long-acting nitrate; add ranolazine/ivabradine/nicorandil if needed; plus aspirin + statin + risk-factor control.
  • Variant angina: CCBs + nitrates (avoid non-selective β-blockers — may worsen spasm).

Limitations

Nitrates — tolerance (need nitrate-free interval), headache, hypotension, reflex tachycardia. β-blockers — bradycardia, bronchospasm, fatigue. CCBs — oedema, constipation (verapamil), reflex tachycardia (DHP).

Recent: Ranolazine & trimetazidine for refractory angina without haemodynamic effects; combination metabolic + haemodynamic therapy; aggressive LDL lowering (PCSK9i).
▸★★Experimental & clinical methods for evaluation of antianginal drugs; cytoprotective (metabolic) agents in angina.

Experimental methods

  • Coronary artery ligation (occlusion/reperfusion) in dogs/rats → infarct-size estimation.
  • Isolated heart (Langendorff) — coronary flow & contractility.
  • Isolated coronary artery strips for vasodilation; ischaemia-reperfusion models; pacing-induced ischaemia.

Clinical methods

  • Exercise/treadmill ECG stress test — time to ST-depression/angina, exercise duration.
  • Ambulatory ECG (Holter) for ischaemic episodes; frequency of anginal attacks & GTN consumption.
  • Stress echo, myocardial perfusion imaging; RCTs with endpoints (MI, mortality).

Cytoprotective / metabolic agents

Improve myocardial energy efficiency without altering haemodynamics: trimetazidine (inhibits fatty-acid β-oxidation → shifts to glucose oxidation), ranolazine (late Na⁺ current inhibition → ↓ Ca²⁺ overload), perhexiline. Useful as add-on in refractory/microvascular angina.

Section 4 · Anticoagulants, Antiplatelets & Fibrinolytics

▸★★★Pharmacology of drugs maintaining blood fluidity. Classify anticoagulants, antiplatelets & fibrinolytics; MOA/uses/ADRs/status of heparin, LMWH, DTIs, factor Xa inhibitors & NOACs; note on reversal agents & recent advances.

Classification

GroupSubclasses & examples
AnticoagulantsParenteral: UFH, LMWH (enoxaparin), fondaparinux, DTIs (bivalirudin, argatroban). Oral: warfarin (vit-K antagonist), DOACs — dabigatran (DTI), rivaroxaban/apixaban/edoxaban (Xa)
AntiplateletsAspirin (COX-1), P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor), GpIIb/IIIa (abciximab, eptifibatide), dipyridamole, cilostazol, vorapaxar (PAR-1)
FibrinolyticsStreptokinase, alteplase, reteplase, tenecteplase

Heparin (UFH)

MOA: activates antithrombin III → inactivates thrombin (IIa) & Xa. Parenteral, immediate, monitored by aPTT. ADR: bleeding, HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia), osteoporosis. Reversal: protamine sulfate.

LMWH (enoxaparin, dalteparin)

Mainly anti-Xa; SC, predictable kinetics, no routine monitoring (anti-Xa if needed), less HIT. Partly reversed by protamine. Fondaparinux = pure anti-Xa pentasaccharide.

Direct thrombin inhibitors

Dabigatran (oral), bivalirudin/argatroban (IV) — directly inhibit IIa; used in AF, VTE, PCI, HIT. Reversal of dabigatran: idarucizumab.

Factor Xa inhibitors / NOACs (DOACs)

Rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban — oral, fixed dose, no routine monitoring, fewer interactions/bleeds than warfarin. Uses: AF stroke prevention, VTE treatment/prophylaxis. Reversal: andexanet alfa (or PCC).

Reversal agents (summary)

Protamine (heparin/LMWH), vitamin K + PCC/FFP (warfarin), idarucizumab (dabigatran), andexanet alfa (Xa inhibitors), platelet transfusion ± desmopressin (antiplatelets).

Recent: DOACs preferred over warfarin for non-valvular AF/VTE; Factor XIa inhibitors (asundexian, milvexian — trials) promise anticoagulation with less bleeding.
▸★★Recent advances in anticoagulation & antiplatelet drugs; role in various cardiovascular disorders.

Recent advances

  • DOACs replacing warfarin (predictable, safer, no monitoring) for AF & VTE.
  • Specific reversal agents (idarucizumab, andexanet alfa).
  • Factor XI/XIa inhibitors (asundexian, milvexian, abelacimab) — uncouple thrombosis from bleeding (trials).
  • Ticagrelor & prasugrel (faster, more potent P2Y12); de-escalation & shorter DAPT strategies; vorapaxar (PAR-1).

Chronic asthma — GINA approach (stepwise)

  • Key GINA shift: no SABA-only treatment. All adults/adolescents use ICS-containing therapy. Preferred reliever = ICS-formoterol.
  • Track 1 (preferred): as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (Steps 1–2) → low then medium-dose ICS-formoterol maintenance + reliever (MART, Steps 3–4) → add-on LAMA / refer for biologic (Step 5).
  • LABA must never be used without ICS.

Acute exacerbation

Oxygen (target SpO₂ 93–95%), repeated SABA (± ipratropium) via nebuliser/MDI-spacer, systemic corticosteroids (oral/IV early), IV magnesium sulfate in severe cases, consider ICS; monitor & escalate to ICU/ventilation if life-threatening.

▸★★Pharmacotherapy of pulmonary arterial hypertension; role of endothelin receptor antagonists & other targeted therapies.

PAH = ↑ pulmonary arterial pressure from vasoconstriction & remodelling. Therapy targets three pathways:

PathwayDrug classExamples
EndothelinERAsBosentan, macitentan (non-selective); ambrisentan (ETA)
Nitric oxide / cGMPPDE5 inhibitors; sGC stimulatorSildenafil, tadalafil; riociguat

Endothelin receptor antagonists

Block ETA (±ETB) → ↓ vasoconstriction & proliferation. Hepatotoxic & teratogenic → monthly LFTs & pregnancy tests (REMS). Improve symptoms, exercise capacity & delay clinical worsening.

General & supportive

  • High-dose CCBs only if vasoreactivity-test positive (minority).
  • Diuretics, oxygen, anticoagulation (selected), treat underlying cause.
  • Combination/upfront therapy now standard (e.g. ambrisentan + tadalafil — AMBITION).
Recent: Sotatercept (activin-signalling inhibitor, 2024) — first disease-modifying agent targeting pulmonary vascular remodelling.

SGLT2 inhibitors — detail

  • Mechanism: block SGLT2 in proximal tubule → glucosuria + natriuresis → ↓ glucose, BP, weight; insulin-independent.
  • Cardio-renal benefit: ↓ HF hospitalisation & CV death (EMPA-REG, DAPA-HF, EMPEROR), ↓ CKD progression (DAPA-CKD, CREDENCE) — now used in HF & CKD even without diabetes.
  • ADR: genital/UTI mycotic infections, volume depletion, euglycaemic DKA, (canagliflozin — amputation/fracture signal).

DPP-4 inhibitors — detail

  • Mechanism: inhibit DPP-4 → prolong incretin action → glucose-dependent insulin ↑, glucagon ↓.
  • Profile: weight-neutral, low hypoglycaemia, oral, well tolerated; modest HbA1c drop; CV-safe (neutral). Caution: pancreatitis; saxagliptin — ↑ HF hospitalisation signal.
Recent: GLP-1 RAs & tirzepatide transforming diabetes + obesity care; SGLT2i are now cardio-renal protective drugs; oral semaglutide available.

Statins — MOA & pleiotropic effects

  • MOA: inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting cholesterol synthesis) → ↑ hepatic LDL receptors → ↓ plasma LDL.
  • Pleiotropic (lipid-independent): plaque stabilisation, anti-inflammatory (↓CRP), improved endothelial function, antithrombotic, antioxidant.

Indications & ADRs

Indications: hypercholesterolaemia, primary & secondary CV prevention, ACS, diabetes with risk factors. ADR: myalgia/myopathy-rhabdomyolysis, ↑ transaminases, new-onset diabetes, drug interactions (CYP3A4 — simvastatin). Contraindicated in pregnancy.

PCSK9 inhibitors & newer agents

  • PCSK9i: ↑ LDL-receptor recycling → LDL ↓50–60%; for familial hypercholesterolaemia, statin-intolerant, very-high-risk not at goal. Inclisiran (siRNA) — twice-yearly.
  • Bempedoic acid (ATP-citrate lyase inhibitor — oral, less myalgia); icosapent ethyl (REDUCE-IT — CV risk ↓).
Newer targets: ANGPTL3 inhibitors (evinacumab — HoFH), Lp(a)-lowering antisense (pelacarsen, trials), CETP inhibitors (obicetrapib).

Section 7 · Gastrointestinal Pharmacology

▸★★★"Prescribing PPIs — time to pause & rethink?" Role of PPIs in PUD, current status, misuse & ADRs; compare P-CABs with PPIs.

PPIs — role & mechanism

Omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole, rabeprazole. Irreversibly inhibit the gastric H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump) → most potent acid suppression. Uses: peptic ulcer, GERD, H. pylori eradication (with antibiotics), NSAID-ulcer prophylaxis, Zollinger-Ellison, stress-ulcer prophylaxis.

Misuse / "time to rethink"

  • Widely over-prescribed & continued without indication ("just in case"), inappropriate long-term use, lack of de-prescribing review.

Adverse effects of long-term use

  • ↓ absorption: vitamin B12, magnesium, calcium, iron → osteoporosis/fractures.
  • ↑ infection: C. difficile, community-acquired pneumonia, enteric infections.
  • Hypomagnesaemia, ↑ risk of CKD/AKI & interstitial nephritis, rebound acid hypersecretion, ?dementia (association), clopidogrel interaction (omeprazole — CYP2C19).

P-CABs vs PPIs

FeaturePPIsP-CABs (vonoprazan, tegoprazan)
MechanismIrreversible covalent pump inhibitionReversible K⁺-competitive pump block
OnsetSlow (days for full effect)Rapid (day 1)
ActivationProdrug, acid-activated, food-timing mattersAcid-stable, no activation needed, food-independent
Duration / nocturnal controlShorter; nocturnal acid breakthroughLonger, better nocturnal & 24-h control
CYP2C19 dependenceYes (variable response)Minimal

P-CABs give faster, stronger, more sustained acid suppression — promising for refractory GERD, H. pylori & healing erosive oesophagitis.

Potassium-channel modulators in peptic ulcer

The gastric proton pump is a H⁺/K⁺-ATPase that requires luminal K⁺ recycling (via KCNQ1 channels). P-CABs (vonoprazan) are K⁺-competitive acid blockers — they competitively block the K⁺-binding site of the pump → potent reversible acid suppression — representing the "potassium channel/site modulation" approach to acid-peptic disease.

Section 8 · Erectile Dysfunction & Other Special Topics

▸★★Drugs used in erectile dysfunction; recent advances in its pharmacotherapy.

First-line — PDE5 inhibitors

  • Sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil, avanafil. Mechanism: inhibit PDE5 → ↑ cGMP in corpus cavernosum (potentiate NO) → smooth-muscle relaxation, ↑ blood flow → erection (needs sexual stimulation).
  • Tadalafil — long acting (36 h, "weekend pill"), also daily dosing & BPH.
  • ADR: headache, flushing, dyspepsia, nasal congestion, blue vision (sildenafil), priapism. Contraindicated with nitrates & caution with α-blockers (hypotension).

▸★Drug therapy for hyperprolactinemia and prolactin inhibitors.

Principle: Prolactin secretion is tonically inhibited by hypothalamic dopamine acting on pituitary D2 receptors. Hence dopamine agonists are the mainstay.

Section 9 · Special & Regulatory Topics

▸★★Constitution, functions & guidelines of CCSEA/IAEC/CPCSEA; methods of euthanasia approved for rodents; parallels with the IEC for clinical trials.

CCSEA / CPCSEA

CCSEA (Committee for Control & Supervision of Experiments on Animals; formerly CPCSEA) — statutory body under the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act 1960 (MoEFCC). Registers animal facilities, frames guidelines, and oversees ethical animal use; ensures 3R compliance.

IAEC — constitution

  • Chairperson; Member-Secretary; biological/veterinary scientists; a scientist from a different discipline; a non-scientific socially-aware member; and a CCSEA-nominated main & link nominee (mandatory).

Functions

  • Review & approve animal protocols (justification, numbers, species, endpoints); ensure humane care, anaesthesia/analgesia, 3R; inspect facilities; maintain records; can stop non-compliant work.

Approved euthanasia methods for rodents

  • CO₂ inhalation (gradual fill — most common), overdose of anaesthetic (e.g. pentobarbital).
  • Physical (require trained personnel/justification): cervical dislocation (small rodents), decapitation — often after sedation.
  • Must ensure rapid loss of consciousness & confirm death; align with AVMA/CCSEA humane-endpoint guidelines.

Parallel with IEC (clinical trials)

Both are independent ethics committees ensuring ethical, justified, well-designed research with continuous monitoring.

▸★Principles for prescribing drugs in patients with renal and hepatic dysfunction.

Renal dysfunction

  • Estimate function (eGFR/CrCl, Cockcroft-Gault); adjust dose of renally-cleared drugs.
  • Methods: ↓ dose and/or ↑ dosing interval; give loading dose normally (Vd unchanged), reduce maintenance.
  • Avoid nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, contrast); watch active/renally-excreted metabolites (morphine, pethidine).
  • Account for dialysis (supplemental dosing for dialysable drugs); monitor K⁺ with RAAS blockers.

Hepatic dysfunction

  • No single reliable marker — use Child-Pugh score to gauge severity.
  • ↓ first-pass metabolism → ↑ bioavailability of high-extraction drugs (propranolol, morphine); ↓ clearance of hepatically-metabolised drugs.
  • Hypoalbuminaemia → ↑ free fraction of protein-bound drugs (phenytoin, warfarin).
  • Avoid hepatotoxins (paracetamol high dose, methotrexate); caution with sedatives (precipitate encephalopathy); altered coagulation → care with anticoagulants.
  • General principle: "start low, go slow," use drugs with wide therapeutic index, monitor clinically & with TDM where possible.

🩺 Clinical Case Vignettes

Apply the concepts — read the scenario, decide your answer, then open to check. Filter to just these with the 🩺 Cases chip.

▸🩺 CaseA patient with sudden breathlessness, frothy sputum and bilateral basal crepitations is in acute pulmonary oedema. Immediate drugs?
Clue: Reduce preload quickly.

Sit up, high-flow oxygen, IV furosemide (venodilation + diuresis lowers preload), GTN (lowers preload/afterload), morphine in selected cases; treat the precipitant; CPAP/NIV if hypoxic.

▸🩺 CaseA 60-year-old man with HFrEF (EF 30%) is on furosemide only. What disease-modifying therapy should be added?
Clue: The four pillars.

Add the four pillars: ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) or ACEi/ARB + beta-blocker (carvedilol/bisoprolol) + MRA (spironolactone) + SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin) — each reduces mortality; the diuretic treats symptoms only.

▸🩺 CaseA 72-year-old woman presents with new atrial fibrillation. How do you decide on anticoagulation and which agent?
Clue: CHA2DS2-VASc.

Assess stroke risk with CHA2DS2-VASc (and bleeding with HAS-BLED). If anticoagulation is indicated, prefer a DOAC (apixaban/rivaroxaban/dabigatran) over warfarin — except mechanical valves or moderate-severe mitral stenosis (warfarin). Add rate/rhythm control.

▸🩺 CaseA man with crushing chest pain has ST-elevation on ECG. Outline the acute pharmacotherapy.
Clue: Antiplatelet plus reperfusion.

MONA-B + DAPT + reperfusion: aspirin + a P2Y12 inhibitor (ticagrelor/prasugrel), an anticoagulant (heparin), nitrate, analgesia, beta-blocker; primary PCI preferred, otherwise a fibrinolytic (tenecteplase) if no contraindication; then statin and ACEi.

▸🩺 CaseA CKD patient on ramipril and spironolactone presents with potassium 6.5 and peaked T waves. What happened and how is it managed?
Clue: Dual RAAS blockade.

ACEi + MRA + renal impairment → hyperkalaemia. Emergency: IV calcium gluconate (cardioprotection), insulin-dextrose, salbutamol; stop the offending drugs; loop diuretic / potassium binder / dialysis. Avoid combining ACEi with ARB or MRA in advanced CKD without monitoring.

▸🩺 CaseA patient presents with BP 230/130, headache and papilloedema. Treatment principle?
Clue: Controlled lowering.

Hypertensive emergency → IV agents (labetalol, nicardipine, nitroprusside, GTN, esmolol); lower BP gradually (about 25% in the first hour) to avoid cerebral and renal hypoperfusion.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on simvastatin is started on clarithromycin and develops severe muscle pain and dark urine. Explain.
Clue: CYP3A4 inhibition.

Clarithromycin inhibits CYP3A4 → raised simvastatin levels → myopathy/rhabdomyolysis (check CK and renal function). Stop the statin, hydrate; avoid simvastatin with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (use pravastatin or rosuvastatin, or hold the statin).

▸🩺 CaseA young woman has rest angina with transient ST-elevation but normal coronary arteries. Best therapy and what to avoid?
Clue: Coronary vasospasm.

Prinzmetal (variant) angina. Use calcium channel blockers + nitrates; avoid non-selective beta-blockers (unopposed alpha may worsen spasm). Stop smoking.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on round-the-clock isosorbide dinitrate finds it no longer relieves angina. Why, and the fix?
Clue: Tolerance.

Nitrate tolerance from continuous exposure. Provide a nitrate-free interval (about 8–12 hours/day) to restore responsiveness.

▸🩺 CaseA diabetic with heart failure needs better glycaemic control. Which add-on helps the heart, and which should be avoided?
Clue: SGLT2 inhibitor.

Add an SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin) — lowers glucose and reduces HF hospitalisation and mortality. Avoid pioglitazone (fluid retention) and saxagliptin (increased HF hospitalisation) in heart failure.
