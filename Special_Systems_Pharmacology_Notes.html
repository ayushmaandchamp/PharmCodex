Section 1 · Antibiotic Use, Resistance & Stewardship

▸★★★Rational & irrational use of antibiotics; mechanisms of antibiotic action & bacterial resistance; measures to reduce resistance; note on Antibiotic Stewardship Programme & antibiotic policy.

Mechanisms of antibiotic action

TargetClass / example
Cell-wall synthesisβ-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems), vancomycin
Protein synthesisAminoglycosides, tetracyclines, macrolides, clindamycin, linezolid, chloramphenicol
Nucleic acid synthesisFluoroquinolones (DNA gyrase), rifampicin (RNA polymerase)
Folate synthesisSulfonamides, trimethoprim
Cell membranePolymyxins, daptomycin

Rational vs irrational use

RationalIrrational
Right drug/dose/duration; culture-directed; correct indication (bacterial); de-escalationFor viral illness; wrong/excess dose; inadequate or excessive duration; OTC misuse; unnecessary broad-spectrum/combinations

Mechanisms of bacterial resistance

  • Enzymatic inactivation (β-lactamases, aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes).
  • Altered target (PBP change — MRSA; ribosomal/DNA-gyrase mutation; altered penicillin-binding in pneumococci).
  • ↓ Permeability (porin loss) & efflux pumps (tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones).
  • Bypass pathway (alternate enzyme — sulfonamide resistance).
  • Genetic spread: mutation; horizontal transfer via plasmids, transposons, integrons (conjugation/transduction/transformation).

Measures to reduce/overcome resistance

  • Rational prescribing, culture & sensitivity-guided therapy, correct dose/duration, de-escalation.
  • Infection control & hand hygiene; vaccination; restrict OTC sale (Schedule H1); avoid agricultural/veterinary misuse (One Health).
  • Develop new antibiotics & β-lactamase inhibitors; combination therapy; surveillance.

Antibiotic Stewardship Programme (AMSP) & policy

  • Coordinated hospital programme to optimise antimicrobial use: hospital antibiogram, formulary restriction, prior-authorisation of reserve drugs, prospective audit & feedback, de-escalation, IV-to-oral switch, duration limits.
  • WHO AWaRe classification (Access, Watch, Reserve) guides selection.
  • Indian context: National Action Plan on AMR (NAP-AMR), ICMR treatment guidelines, Schedule H1, "Red Line" campaign.
▸★★Resistance to β-lactam antibiotics; newer β-lactams & β-lactamase inhibitors with clinical applications.

Mechanisms of β-lactam resistance

  • β-lactamase production — hydrolyse β-lactam ring (penicillinase, ESBL, AmpC, carbapenemases — KPC, NDM-1, OXA).
  • Altered PBPs — e.g. PBP2a in MRSA (mecA gene); altered PBP in penicillin-resistant pneumococci.
  • ↓ Permeability (porin loss) & efflux in gram-negatives.

Newer β-lactams

Cefiderocol — siderophore cephalosporin (uses iron-transport to enter; active vs MDR gram-negatives incl. carbapenem-resistant); newer cephalosporins (ceftaroline — anti-MRSA); carbapenems for ESBL.

Recent: Newer BLI combinations (avibactam, relebactam, vaborbactam) & cefiderocol combat carbapenem-resistant gram-negatives (NDM, KPC).

Treatment principles

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa is intrinsically resistant to many drugs & develops resistance rapidly.
  • Combination therapy often used for serious infections (β-lactam + aminoglycoside or fluoroquinolone) — broaden cover, synergy, ↓ resistance.
  • Conditions: hospital-acquired/ventilator pneumonia, burns, febrile neutropenia, UTIs, cystic fibrosis (inhaled tobramycin/colistin), osteomyelitis, endocarditis.
  • De-escalate per culture; MDR strains → ceftolozane-tazobactam, ceftazidime-avibactam, cefiderocol, colistin.

Section 2 · Ocular Pharmacology

▸★★★Pharmacotherapy of glaucoma; classify drugs (mechanisms); autonomic drugs in ophthalmic practice, ocular NSAIDs, ocular drug-delivery; note on drug-induced ocular toxicity & ocular dyes.

Glaucoma drug classes

ClassDrugMechanism
Prostaglandin analogues (1st-line)Latanoprost, bimatoprost, travoprost↑ uveoscleral outflow
β-blockersTimolol, betaxolol↓ aqueous production
α2-agonistsBrimonidine, apraclonidine↓ production + ↑ outflow
Carbonic anhydrase inhibitorsDorzolamide (topical), acetazolamide (oral)↓ aqueous production
Cholinergic mioticsPilocarpine↑ trabecular outflow

HyperosmoticsMannitol, glycerol↓ vitreous volume (acute)

Acute angle-closure: IV acetazolamide + mannitol + topical pilocarpine + timolol → laser iridotomy.

Autonomic drugs in ophthalmic practice

  • Mydriatics/cycloplegics: atropine, homatropine, cyclopentolate, tropicamide (antimuscarinic); phenylephrine (α1 — mydriasis without cycloplegia).
  • Miotics: pilocarpine (muscarinic agonist).
  • β-blockers & α2-agonists for IOP (above).

Ocular uses of NSAIDs

Ketorolac, nepafenac, diclofenac, flurbiprofen — control postoperative inflammation/pain, prevent cystoid macular oedema, maintain intraoperative mydriasis, allergic conjunctivitis.

Ocular drug-delivery strategies

  • Topical drops/ointments (most common), gels, inserts (Ocusert), soft contact lenses.
  • Subconjunctival, intravitreal injection (anti-VEGF, steroids), intravitreal implants (dexamethasone, fluocinolone), periocular; nanocarriers/iontophoresis (emerging).

Drug-induced ocular toxicity

Chloroquine/hydroxychloroquine — retinopathy; ethambutol — optic neuritis; corticosteroids — cataract & glaucoma; amiodarone — corneal deposits; topiramate — acute glaucoma; tamoxifen — retinopathy; digoxin — xanthopsia; sildenafil — blue vision.

Ocular dyes

Fluorescein — corneal abrasion/ulcer detection, angiography, applanation tonometry; rose bengal/lissamine green — devitalised cells (dry eye).

▸★★Pharmacological management of glaucoma with emphasis on newer agents (including Brimonidine).

Stepwise management

  • First-line: prostaglandin analogue (latanoprost) — most effective, once-daily, ↑ uveoscleral outflow.
  • Add/alternative: β-blocker, α2-agonist, topical CAI; combination eye-drops to improve adherence.
  • Refractory → laser trabeculoplasty / surgery.

Brimonidine (α2-agonist)

  • Selective α2-agonist → ↓ aqueous production and ↑ uveoscleral outflow; possible neuroprotection.
  • Uses: open-angle glaucoma/ocular hypertension, peri-laser IOP spikes.
  • ADR: ocular allergy, dry mouth, fatigue; avoid in infants/young children (CNS depression, apnoea).

Newer agents

  • Netarsudil (Rho-kinase inhibitor) — ↑ trabecular outflow, ↓ episcleral venous pressure.
  • Latanoprostene bunod — NO-donating prostaglandin (dual outflow).
  • Fixed-combination drops; sustained-release bimatoprost implant (Durysta).
Recent: Rho-kinase inhibitors & NO-donating prostaglandins; intracameral sustained-release implants reduce adherence burden.

Drug classes in dermatology (roles)

  • Topical glucocorticoids: anti-inflammatory/immunosuppressive; classified by potency (mild → super-potent). ADR: skin atrophy, striae, telangiectasia, tachyphylaxis, HPA suppression. Avoid prolonged use on face/flexures.
  • Retinoids: topical (tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene — acne, psoriasis) & systemic (isotretinoin — severe acne; acitretin — psoriasis); highly teratogenic (pregnancy prevention).
  • Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus): topical steroid-sparing for atopic dermatitis & sensitive areas (face, eyelids); no skin atrophy.
  • TNF-α inhibitors/biologics: moderate-severe psoriasis/psoriatic arthritis; screen latent TB.
Recent: IL-17 & IL-23 biologics give high skin-clearance (PASI 90/100); oral deucravacitinib (TYK2) & JAK inhibitors (atopic dermatitis — upadacitinib, abrocitinib); topical roflumilast/tapinarof.

Section 4 · Corticosteroids — Uses & Safety

▸★★★Corticosteroids — MOA, therapeutic uses (endocrine & non-endocrine), ADRs; life-saving uses, role in malignancy/COVID-19/dermatology; note on long-term & topical therapy.

Mechanism of action

  1. Lipophilic steroid enters cell → binds cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor (HSP90-bound) → complex translocates to nucleus.
  2. Binds Glucocorticoid Response Elements (GRE) → transactivation of anti-inflammatory genes (annexin-1/lipocortin, IκB) & transrepression of NF-κB/AP-1 → ↓ cytokines, COX-2, phospholipase A2.
  3. Net: anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, metabolic (gluconeogenesis), plus rapid non-genomic effects.

Therapeutic uses

Endocrine (replacement)Non-endocrine (pharmacological)
Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's), CAH, acute adrenal crisisInflammatory/allergic disease, autoimmune disease, asthma/COPD, dermatoses, IBD, transplant immunosuppression, malignancy, cerebral oedema, shock

Life-saving uses

  • Anaphylaxis (adjunct), acute severe asthma, adrenal crisis, septic shock (refractory), cerebral oedema, severe COVID-19, status asthmaticus, severe drug reactions (SJS/TEN), autoimmune crises.

Role in malignancy

Lympholytic → apoptosis of lymphoid cells: ALL, lymphoma, multiple myeloma, CLL (CHOP/VAD regimens); antiemetic with chemo; ↓ cerebral oedema (brain tumours/metastases); hypercalcaemia of malignancy; appetite/palliation.

Role in COVID-19

Dexamethasone (RECOVERY trial) reduced mortality in patients needing oxygen/ventilation by dampening the cytokine-mediated inflammatory/hyperimmune response.

Role in dermatology

Topical (eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis) & systemic (pemphigus, severe drug reactions). Topical potency graded; ADR — skin atrophy, striae, tachyphylaxis.

Adverse effects (esp. long-term)

  • Iatrogenic Cushing's, weight gain, moon face, truncal obesity.
  • Hyperglycaemia/diabetes, hypertension, osteoporosis, avascular necrosis, myopathy.
  • Peptic ulcer, immunosuppression/infection (TB reactivation), cataract & glaucoma, growth retardation (children), mood/psychosis, skin thinning.
  • HPA-axis suppression → never stop abruptly (taper to avoid adrenal crisis).

Precautions for long-term & topical therapy

  • Lowest effective dose, shortest duration; alternate-day dosing; morning dosing (mimic circadian); taper gradually.
  • Calcium + vitamin D ± bisphosphonate (bone protection); monitor BP, glucose, weight, eyes; PPI if ulcer risk; screen/treat latent TB.
  • Topical: use appropriate potency for site, avoid prolonged use on face/flexures, beware systemic absorption in children/large areas.

Section 5 · Ecopharmacology & One Health

▸★★★Ecopharmacology — pharmaceuticals in the environment, ecopharmacovigilance & the One Health concept; recent advances & regulatory aspects.

Definitions

  • Ecopharmacology: study of the entry, fate, distribution and effects of pharmaceuticals (and their metabolites) in the environment.
  • Ecopharmacovigilance (EPV): the science & activities concerning detection, assessment, understanding and prevention of adverse effects of pharmaceuticals in the environment (the environmental analogue of pharmacovigilance).

Pharmaceuticals in the environment — sources & examples

  • Sources: patient excretion (urine/faeces), manufacturing effluent, improper disposal of unused/expired drugs, hospital & livestock waste.
  • Examples of harm: diclofenac → catastrophic decline of Indian vultures (renal failure); synthetic estrogens → feminisation/altered reproduction in fish; antibiotics → environmental antimicrobial resistance; antineoplastics, NSAIDs, hormones & psychoactive drugs detected in surface/drinking water.

One Health concept

A collaborative, multisectoral approach recognising that human, animal and environmental health are interconnected. Crucial for AMR — antibiotic use in humans, animals/agriculture & environmental contamination all drive resistance, requiring coordinated action.

Mitigation & regulatory aspects

  • Environmental Risk Assessment (ERA) now part of drug approval (EMA/FDA).
  • "Green pharmacy"/eco-friendly drug design (benign-by-design, biodegradable molecules).
  • Drug take-back/disposal programmes; improved sewage & effluent treatment; manufacturing discharge norms.
  • Surveillance & classification of environmentally hazardous drugs; ban on veterinary diclofenac (India).
Recent advances: One Health AMR action plans (NAP-AMR), antibiotic discharge limits for manufacturers, environmental monitoring of waterways, and incorporation of EPV into pharmacovigilance frameworks.

Section 6 · Poisoning Management & Chelating Agents

▸★★★General principles of poisoning management; role of chelating agents in heavy-metal poisoning (examples); note on paracetamol poisoning, iron overload & atropine poisoning.

General principles of poisoning management

  1. Resuscitation & stabilisation (ABCDE) — airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure.
  2. Identification — history, examination, toxidromes, lab/levels.
  3. Decontamination: activated charcoal (within 1 h), gastric lavage (selected early presentations), skin/eye irrigation, whole-bowel irrigation (iron, packets, SR drugs).
  4. Enhanced elimination: urine alkalinisation (salicylate, phenobarbitone), multiple-dose charcoal, haemodialysis (methanol, lithium, salicylate, ethylene glycol).
  5. Specific antidotes.
  6. Supportive & symptomatic care, monitoring, psychiatric referral.

Chelating agents in heavy-metal poisoning

Chelator = forms stable, water-soluble, non-toxic complexes with metal ions for excretion.

MetalChelator
LeadCaNa₂-EDTA, DMSA (succimer), dimercaprol (encephalopathy)
Arsenic/Mercury/GoldDimercaprol (BAL), DMSA, DMPS
IronDeferoxamine (IV), deferasirox/deferiprone (oral)
Copper (Wilson's)D-penicillamine, trientine

Avoid oral chelator while metal remains in gut (may ↑ absorption); monitor renal function.

Paracetamol poisoning

Toxic metabolite NAPQI (via CYP2E1) depletes glutathione → hepatic necrosis. Use the Rumack-Matthew nomogram; antidote N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione) — most effective within 8 h; activated charcoal if early.

Iron overload

Acute iron poisoning or chronic transfusional overload (thalassaemia). Chelation: deferoxamine (IV/IM, "vin rosé" urine), oral deferasirox / deferiprone; supportive care, whole-bowel irrigation in acute ingestion.

Atropine (anticholinergic) poisoning

Toxidrome: "hot, dry, red, blind, mad" (hyperthermia, dry skin, flushing, mydriasis, delirium). Treatment: supportive cooling, benzodiazepines for agitation, and physostigmine (reversible anticholinesterase crossing BBB) for severe central toxicity.

Section 7 · Other Important Topics (Overlaps)

▸★★Current status & issues with COX-2 inhibitors; note on non-endocrine uses of corticosteroids & their therapeutic status in malignancy.

COX-2 inhibitors

  • Rationale: selectively inhibit inducible COX-2 (inflammation), sparing constitutive COX-1 (gastric mucosa, platelets) → fewer GI ulcers/bleeds, no antiplatelet effect. Drugs: celecoxib, etoricoxib, parecoxib.
  • Issues: ↑ cardiovascular thrombotic risk (↓ vascular prostacyclin, unopposed thromboxane) → rofecoxib & valdecoxib withdrawn; celecoxib/etoricoxib available with CV/renal cautions; renal effects & hypertension as with NSAIDs.
  • Uses: osteoarthritis, RA, acute pain, ankylosing spondylitis (esp. high GI-bleed risk); lowest dose, shortest duration.
Recent (PRECISION trial): moderate-dose celecoxib CV safety comparable to naproxen/ibuprofen — partly rehabilitating the class.

Non-endocrine uses of corticosteroids

Inflammatory/allergic disorders, autoimmune disease (SLE, RA, vasculitis), asthma/COPD, dermatoses, IBD, nephrotic syndrome, transplant immunosuppression, cerebral oedema, anaphylaxis (adjunct), septic/refractory shock, COVID-19 (dexamethasone), and malignancy.

Corticosteroids in malignancy

Lympholytic → apoptosis of lymphoid cells: used in ALL, lymphomas, multiple myeloma, CLL (CHOP, VAD regimens); also antiemetic with chemotherapy, ↓ cerebral oedema (brain tumour/metastasis), hypercalcaemia of malignancy, appetite stimulation & palliation, prevent hypersensitivity reactions to chemotherapy.

▸★★Pharmacotherapy with recent advances of: Congestive Heart Failure (ARNI & SGLT-2), Type-2 Diabetes (SGLT-2 & DPP-4), and acid-peptic disease & PPIs (use & misuse).
1. Congestive Heart Failure

Four pillars of HFrEF: ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan — neprilysin inhibition ↑ natriuretic peptides; superior to enalapril, PARADIGM-HF) ≫ ACEi/ARB + β-blocker (carvedilol/bisoprolol/metoprolol) + MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) + SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin — ↓ HF hospitalisation & mortality even without diabetes). Plus loop diuretics (symptoms), ivabradine, vericiguat.

Recent: SGLT2 inhibitors benefit across the EF spectrum (HFpEF — EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER); start all four pillars early & together.
2. Type-2 Diabetes Mellitus

Metformin first-line; choose add-ons by comorbidity. SGLT2 inhibitors — ↓ renal glucose reabsorption, weight/BP reduction, cardio-renal protection (used in HF/CKD even without diabetes); ADR genital infection, euglycaemic DKA. DPP-4 inhibitors — ↑ incretins, weight-neutral, low hypoglycaemia, CV-neutral (saxagliptin — ↑HF signal). GLP-1 RAs & tirzepatide give potent glucose + weight benefit.

3. Acid-peptic disease & PPIs (use & misuse)
  • PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole) irreversibly inhibit the gastric H⁺/K⁺-ATPase → potent acid suppression. Uses: peptic ulcer, GERD, H. pylori eradication, NSAID-ulcer prophylaxis, Zollinger-Ellison, stress-ulcer prophylaxis.
  • Misuse: over-prescription & long-term continuation without indication; lack of de-prescribing.
  • Long-term ADRs: ↓ B12/Mg/Ca/iron (fracture risk), C. difficile & pneumonia, hypomagnesaemia, ?CKD/interstitial nephritis, rebound hyperacidity, clopidogrel interaction (omeprazole-CYP2C19).
Recent: P-CABs (vonoprazan, tegoprazan) — K⁺-competitive acid blockers giving faster, stronger, longer, food-independent acid suppression than PPIs; promising for refractory GERD & H. pylori.

▸🩺 CaseA patient has a wound infection growing methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus. Drug of choice and the resistance mechanism?
Clue: Altered penicillin-binding protein.

MRSA resists beta-lactams via PBP2a (mecA gene). Treat with vancomycin (or linezolid, daptomycin, ceftaroline); community MRSA may respond to co-trimoxazole/doxycycline/clindamycin.

▸🩺 CaseA ventilated ICU patient develops pneumonia with Pseudomonas. Antibiotic strategy?
Clue: Intrinsic resistance.

Anti-pseudomonal cover, often a combination (e.g. piperacillin-tazobactam or ceftazidime/cefepime/meropenem plus an aminoglycoside or ciprofloxacin); de-escalate per sensitivity. MDR strains → ceftolozane-tazobactam, ceftazidime-avibactam, colistin, cefiderocol.

▸🩺 CaseA patient has a painful red eye, a fixed mid-dilated pupil and very high intraocular pressure after sitting in a dark cinema. Emergency management?
Clue: Lower the IOP fast.

Acute angle-closure glaucoma. IV acetazolamide + mannitol, topical pilocarpine + timolol (with brimonidine) to lower IOP → definitive laser iridotomy. Avoid mydriatics/anticholinergics.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on prednisolone for several months stops it abruptly and collapses with hypotension. What happened, and the rule?
Clue: HPA-axis suppression.

Adrenal crisis from HPA-axis suppression. Long-term steroids must be tapered, not stopped abruptly; give stress doses during illness/surgery; treat a crisis with IV hydrocortisone and fluids. Add bone and gastric protection for chronic use.

▸🩺 CaseA patient presents 6 hours after a large paracetamol overdose. Antidote and how the need is assessed?
Clue: Glutathione replenishment.

N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione and detoxifies NAPQI) — most effective within 8 hours; plot the level on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram; activated charcoal if the patient presents early.

▸🩺 CaseA child with anaemia, abdominal pain and developmental regression has a high blood-lead level. Treatment?
Clue: Chelation.

Lead poisoning — chelate with DMSA (succimer) or CaNa2-EDTA; add dimercaprol (BAL) for encephalopathy; remove the source of exposure.

▸🩺 CaseWhy was veterinary diclofenac banned in India, and what does this illustrate?
Clue: The vulture collapse.

Diclofenac residues in cattle carcasses caused fatal renal failure in vultures, collapsing their populations — a landmark ecopharmacology / One Health example of a drug harming the environment. (Clinically, diclofenac is also nephrotoxic — avoid in CKD and the elderly.)

▸🩺 CaseBefore starting an anti-TNF biologic for severe psoriasis, which screening is essential, and why?
Clue: Latent infection.

Screen for latent tuberculosis (and hepatitis B/C). TNF-alpha inhibitors impair granuloma maintenance → reactivation of TB. Treat latent TB before or alongside the biologic.

▸🩺 CaseA patient develops profuse diarrhoea after broad-spectrum antibiotics; stool tests positive for Clostridioides difficile toxin. Management?
Clue: Stop and treat.

Antibiotic-associated C. difficile colitis. Stop the offending antibiotic; treat with oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin (metronidazole if mild/unavailable); avoid antimotility agents; recurrent disease → faecal microbiota transplant.
